# C3 Research Group — Writing Guide

*Working standards for C3 papers and working documents. Apply to all drafts from initial composition.*

---

## 1. Argument-First Citations

The most important rule. Citations support arguments; they do not introduce them.

**Do not write:**
> Brett Christophers (2023) has shown that the green transition is organized through asset-management logic...

> Rob Nixon (2011) argues that slow violence is "typically not viewed as violence at all"...

> For Galtung, violence is the cause of the difference between the potential and the actual...

**Write instead:**
> The green transition is organized primarily through asset-management logic... (Christophers, 2023)

> Most environmental harm is "typically not viewed as violence at all" — it accumulates gradually, dispersed across time and space (Nixon, 2011, p. 2).

> Violence is not only what people do to each other; it is the cause of the difference between what a life could be and what it is permitted to become (Galtung, 1969, p. 168).

**The test:** Cover the author name and year. Does the sentence still carry an argument? If the sentence collapses without the author name doing the work, rewrite it so it stands alone. The citation is evidence, not the claim.

**Named concepts:** When introducing a theoretical concept that will be used throughout (slow violence, accumulation by dispossession, extrastatecraft, cheap nature), you may name the author on first introduction as part of the concept's proper name — but the citation still moves to the end.

> What Rob Nixon calls slow violence — gradual, dispersed, legally authorized ecological harm — is not merely hard to see; it is structurally designed to remain beneath the threshold of legal recognition (Nixon, 2011).

After first introduction, use the concept without the author name: "slow violence produces..." not "Nixon's slow violence produces..."

**Direct quotes:** Keep author attribution before or integrated into the quote: *"a violence of delayed destruction" (Nixon, 2011, p. 2)* — both are acceptable.

---

## 2. Voice and Prose Register

Papers should read as arguments, not surveys. Aim for the register of Nixon (*Slow Violence*), Tsing (*Friction*), or Weizman (*Forensic Architecture*): essayistic, precise, willing to make claims.

- One idea per paragraph. State it in the first or second sentence; the rest of the paragraph develops it.
- No mechanical contrast pairs: avoid "it is not X but Y," "this is not X; it is Y" as the organizing structure of a sentence. Find a positive formulation.
- No bullet lists in body text. If a list is unavoidable, work the items into a sentence or a series of short paragraphs.
- Favor concrete over abstract: name the mine, the river, the tribunal, the law. Abstractions should earn their place by connecting concrete cases.
- Avoid hedging verbs that weaken claims: "seems to suggest," "might indicate," "could potentially." Either the argument holds or it doesn't.

---

## 3. Section Structure

- Each section should have a single argumentative task, stated in its opening paragraph.
- Transitions between sections should point forward, not summarize backward (2–4 sentences, no "in summary" or "as shown above").
- Sections end when the argument is made — not when all relevant literature has been reviewed.

---

## 4. Citations and References

- Author-date format throughout (APA): (Christophers, 2023) or (Christophers, 2023, p. 47) for page references.
- Flag unverified citations with [†] in text and in the Inline Flag Register at the end of the paper.
- The References section is alphabetical. Do not annotate references in the list — flags belong in the Inline Flag Register.
- Distinguish between confirmed sources (in hand or directly verified) and cited-from-memory sources (flagged [†]).

---

## 5. The Inline Flag Register

Every working paper ends with an Inline Flag Register in table format:

| Flag | Location | Description |
|------|----------|-------------|
| [†] | §3, para. 4 | Claim X — verify source and page number |
| [review] | §5 | Specialist review recommended before submission |
| [community review] | §6 | Community testimony layer requires consent-based review |

Flag types:
- `[†]` — citation unverified at draft stage
- `[review]` — section needs specialist or co-author review
- `[community review]` — section draws on community knowledge; requires reciprocal review process before publication
- `[tension]` — internal tension in the argument that needs resolution in revision

---

## 6. What This Paper Is Not Doing

At any point in drafting, if a passage reads like any of the following, rewrite it:

- A literature review ("Several scholars have addressed this. Author A argues X. Author B argues Y. Author C argues Z.")
- A textbook definition ("Structural violence, as defined by Galtung, refers to...")
- A neutral survey ("There are multiple perspectives on this issue...")
- A hedge-everything disclaimer ("While this analysis has limitations and further research is needed...")

The paper has a position. State it.
