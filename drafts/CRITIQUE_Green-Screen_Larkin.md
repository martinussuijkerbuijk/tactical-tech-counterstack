# Critique: The Green Screen — Through the Lens of Larkin (2013)

**Working notes for redraft**
*Prepared June 2026*

---

## Prefatory note

These are structured criticisms of the current draft of *The Green Screen* (v0.1) generated by reading Brian Larkin's "The Politics and Poetics of Infrastructure" (*Annual Review of Anthropology*, 42, 2013) against the paper's argumentative architecture. The criticisms are not symmetrical in weight: Critique 1 is foundational and has cascading consequences for §§2–7; the others are more local but each points to a specific revision. The critiques are ordered from most architecturally damaging to most tractable.

---

## Critique 1: The paper is built on a premise Larkin explicitly names as "fundamentally inaccurate"

**The problem.** Section 2 installs Susan Leigh Star's visibility/breakdown thesis as the paper's epistemological anchor. The infrastructure of the battery-mineral economy is invisible "because it is functioning" (§2, §7). The entire methodological arc of the paper — the problem of rendering a "functioning infrastructure legible as a site of ongoing harm" (§7) — derives from this claim. But Larkin directly refutes it: "Thus many studies that begin by stating how infrastructures are invisible until they break down are fundamentally inaccurate. Infrastructures are metapragmatic objects, signs of themselves deployed in particular circulatory regimes to establish sets of effects." He continues: "Invisibility is certainly one aspect of infrastructure, but it is only one at the extreme edge of a range of visibilities that move from unseen to grand spectacles and everything in between."

The battery-mineral economy is, in fact, dramatically visible. Cobalt mining in the DRC has been the subject of major investigative journalism, UN reports, NGO campaigns, and EU legislative processes for over a decade. The lithium salares are photographed from satellites and cited in every major energy transition report. The Sámi wind farm case went to the Norwegian Supreme Court. These are not instances of breakdown revealing the invisible. They are instances of a highly visible infrastructure whose visibility is structured in a way that forecloses challenge rather than enabling it. The paper's core claim — that harm is invisible because the infrastructure functions — is weaker than what the paper actually demonstrates. What the paper actually demonstrates is that harm is *rendered inactionable* even when visible. That is a different, stronger, and more unsettling claim.

**The deeper problem.** By framing its central question as "how do we make the invisible visible," the paper places itself within an Enlightenment epistemological tradition — more data, better documentation, improved transparency will produce accountability. This is precisely the tradition the paper critiques in §4 (the ESG apparatus as "performed transparency"). The paper's methodology section (§7) criticizes the aggregation imperative but then proposes alternatives that still operate within the logic of producing better visibility. It has not escaped the epistemological problem it diagnoses.

**Revision suggestion.** The paper's central question needs to be reframed. Not: *how do we make a functioning infrastructure visible as violence?* But: *how do we contest the particular kind of visibility that the infrastructure produces?* The green transition infrastructure does not hide harm. It produces a specific semiotic order — a mode of seeing — in which harm appears as necessary cost, development externality, or localized grievance rather than systemic violence. The methodological problem is not documentation. It is semiotic and juridical: contesting the terms of legibility rather than filling in what is missing.

This reframing has a significant implication for the paper's title concept, addressed in Critique 2.

## Critique 2: The concept "green screen" is theorized as concealment when it functions as address

**The problem.** The paper defines "the green screen" as an epistemological operation that "reorganizes the political legibility of the infrastructure, enrolling extraction within a discourse of necessity that renders challenge not merely inconvenient but irresponsible" (§1). This is precise. But the paper then frames the screen as a hiding mechanism — it "display[s] a particular image of progress while simultaneously obscuring the infrastructural reality behind it" (§1). The screen-as-concealment framing reproduces the invisibility framework Larkin disrupts.

Larkin's argument about infrastructure's "poetics" is relevant here. Drawing on Jakobson, Larkin argues that infrastructures operate "as entextualized forms that have relative autonomy from their technical function" — they are semiotic objects with their own mode of address, generating affect, desire, and fantasy that are not reducible to their technical or concealment functions. A wind farm is not primarily a device that hides extraction or performs green legitimacy. It is, as Larkin's framework would have it, a thing that addresses subjects — that interpellates viewers as participants in a civilizational project, that generates genuine enthusiasm, genuine pride, genuine identification with the promise of decarbonization. That affective interpellation is not a screen hiding something real behind it. It is the infrastructure's political work.

The paper approaches this insight in §1 ("the green designation is therefore not merely a marketing claim. It is an epistemological operation") but retreats from its full implication by describing the operation as obscuring "the infrastructural reality behind it." There is no unscreened reality waiting to be revealed. The visibility the green infrastructure produces *is* its political reality.

**Revision suggestion.** Theorize "the green screen" through Larkin's concept of metapragmatic address rather than through the concealment metaphor. The screen is not a surface hiding something behind it. It is a projective surface — a screen in the cinematic sense — on which a particular political fantasy of civilizational repair is displayed, and through which subjects are constituted as participants in that fantasy. The critical problem is not unveiling what is behind the screen. It is contesting the screen's power to interpellate — to make the viewer identify with the infrastructure's promise rather than with the communities it displaces. This requires engaging the aesthetic and affective dimensions of the green transition that the paper currently undertheorizes (see Critique 4).

## Critique 3: The "doubling" concept would sharpen the ESG analysis considerably

**The problem.** Sections 3 and 4 argue that ESG certification produces "accountability simulacra" — a performance of transparency that forecloses actual accountability. The analysis is structurally sound. But it is framed as a failure: the ESG apparatus fails to measure what it should measure; the certification does not reflect material conditions; the platform promises transparency and delivers opacity. This framing implies that ESG could, in principle, succeed — that a better-designed system would produce real accountability.

Larkin's reading of Mbembe on the "doubling" of infrastructure disrupts this implication. Mbembe analyzes the proliferation of formal documents — budgets, contracts, job descriptions, certificates — that "bear little relation to reality" in postcolonial African states. But Larkin's gloss is the critical move: these documents are not failures of representation. They are successes of a different kind of representation. The pipe that doesn't connect to sewers is still a pipe that allows "reports to be written, budgets to be satisfied, and sponsors to be mollified." The document functions politically through its doubling quality — its simultaneous technical inadequacy and semiotic sufficiency. Lea and Pholeros's analysis of aboriginal housing pipes in Australia makes this precise: "Pipes turn out to be documents."

Applied to the Green Screen: IRMA certification is not a failed accountability mechanism. It is a successful mechanism for constituting the corporation as a responsible actor within the regulatory imaginary of green finance. It produces a subject — the "responsible miner," the "ESG-compliant operator" — whose existence within that semiotic system is real regardless of what happens at the mine site. The critique should be not that the certification fails to reflect material conditions but that it successfully constitutes a regulatory subject whose obligations are discharged through the act of certification rather than through changed practice. This is a much harder structure to challenge, because it cannot be fixed by better data — the certificate is already doing its work.

**Revision suggestion.** Reframe the §4 critique through the doubling concept. The ESG apparatus does not perform transparency while producing opacity. It successfully constitutes corporate accountability as a documentary category — a kind of paper existence — that is entirely real within the financial and regulatory systems it addresses. The political problem is not that the documents misrepresent reality. It is that the documents *are* the reality within those systems, and the reality of the mine is addressed to a different system entirely.

## Critique 4: The paper is systematically inattentive to affect and desire

**The problem.** Larkin's most sustained contribution is his argument that infrastructure operates through desire, fantasy, and aesthetic address as much as through technical function or political concealment. Roads, railways, satellites — and by extension wind farms and solar installations — generate "complicated emotional investments that induce a range of sometimes counterintuitive responses." He cites De Boeck's analysis of Kinshasa slum demolitions: evicted residents supported the development and "regarded it with a sense of pride, even though they themselves represent exactly the sort of disorder that the development was designed to overcome." One fisherman: "Yes, we'll be the victims... but still it will be beautiful."

The Green Screen paper has no room for this. Its political subjects are either oppressors (asset managers, corporate boards, certification bodies) or victims (Lickanantay communities, Kolwezi miners, Sámi herders, Tuareg pastoralists). The communities in extraction zones appear only as holders of local knowledge and bearers of harm. There is no analysis of how the green transition's infrastructure interpellates those communities — how wind farms, solar installations, or battery-mineral developments are experienced as promise and modernity by some community members even as they impose harm on others. This is not a minor omission. It is analytically disabling.

The paper claims, correctly, that "challenge to the mine is not refuted. It is rendered illegitimate by the framing within which it must be made" (§6). But the mechanism through which this rendering works is not only the climate urgency discourse. It is also the affective interpellation of the infrastructure itself — the genuine desire for development, electricity, formal employment, participation in a modernity that the infrastructure promises. Without analyzing that desire and how it is structured by the infrastructure's address, the paper cannot explain why communities sometimes comply with, support, or fail to resist the infrastructure that harms them, except through either coercion or false consciousness — neither of which is adequate.

**Revision suggestion.** The paper needs at least one sustained analytical moment in which the affective and desire dimensions of green infrastructure are taken seriously — not to relativize the harm but to complicate the politics. The double bind (§6) is partly a bind of desire: communities may genuinely want what the mine promises (roads, electricity, employment) at the same time as they experience what the mine delivers (water degradation, land dispossession, community fragmentation). The accountability problem is not only epistemological; it is also about the political structures through which desire and harm coexist within the same infrastructure project. This requires engaging Larkin's aesthetics of address more directly.

## Critique 5: The ontological claim about "defining infrastructure" needs reckoning

**The problem.** Larkin argues that "the act of defining an infrastructure is a categorizing act. It is a moment of tearing into those heterogeneous networks to define which aspect of which network is to be discussed and which parts will be ignored." He continues: "Infrastructures are not, in any positivist sense, simply 'out there.' The act of defining an infrastructure is a categorizing act... a cultural analytic that highlights the epistemological and political commitments involved in selecting what one sees as infrastructural (and thus causal) and what one leaves out."

The Green Screen paper treats "the infrastructure of the green transition" as if it were a self-evident object of analysis. It never asks: when we call the battery supply chain, the concession framework, the ESG apparatus, and the legal architectures of territorial appropriation a single "infrastructure," what is the analytical cost of that categorization? What becomes invisible through that act of grouping? The paper is, on its own terms, engaged in a categorizing act — and the politics of that categorization deserve interrogation.

More specifically: the paper's infrastructure includes the legal, financial, and certification systems, but not the communities' counter-practices — the legal challenges, the road blockades, the water monitoring networks, the UN petitions. Are those not also infrastructure? If infrastructure is defined by the paper as the system of harm, communities appear only as its raw material. If infrastructure is defined more capaciously as the full sociotechnical ensemble through which the green transition is organized — including the practices of resistance and contestation — a different analysis becomes possible.

**Revision suggestion.** A paragraph in §2 (or a brief methodological aside) should explicitly address the paper's categorization decision: why it defines the infrastructure as the extractive assemblage rather than as the full contested sociotechnical field. This is not merely a caveat. It is substantive, because it acknowledges that the paper's visibility problem is partly produced by its own analytical frame — and that a different frame might make different things visible.

## Critique 6: The colonial continuity argument needs material aesthetic evidence, not only juridical evidence

**The problem.** Section 5's colonial continuity argument is the paper's strongest section analytically. It is supported by Larkin in a way the paper does not fully exploit. Larkin's analysis of Mrazek (2002) on Dutch colonial infrastructure in Indonesia provides an aesthetic-political dimension that would strengthen §5 considerably. Mrazek's argument is that Dutch roads imposed "cleanness" as a civilizational value onto Indonesian territories — the hardness of the road surface against the softness of mud and indigenous bodies was a form of sensory-political colonization. The road was simultaneously a technical system and a mode of civilizational address that constituted indigenous bodies as carriers of "soft stuff" that threatened the road's integrity.

The Green Screen paper makes its colonial continuity argument through legal instruments — the concession, the bilateral investment treaty, the enclave economy. This is correct and necessary. But it misses the aesthetic dimension: the colonial encounter with infrastructure is also an encounter with a specific semiotic order — modernity, progress, cleanness, hardness — that the colonized are positioned relative to as threats or beneficiaries but never as co-producers. The green transition's infrastructure carries exactly this aesthetic-civilizational address. The solar panel, the wind turbine, the lithium processing facility — these are not legally organized as colonial instruments alone; they are also semiotic objects that position territories and communities as "not yet modern," as recipients of a civilizational gift whose terms they cannot negotiate.

**Revision suggestion.** §5 should include at least one analytical moment that engages the aesthetic-civilizational dimension of green infrastructure, following Mrazek/Larkin. The argument becomes stronger if it moves from legal continuity alone to legal and aesthetic continuity — the green transition inherits not only the concession framework but the sensory-political grammar through which extraction zones are constituted as spaces awaiting modernization.

## Critique 7: The methodology section reinscribes what it critiques

**The problem.** §7 criticizes the "aggregation imperative" — the assumption that better data at scale produces accountability — and proposes three alternatives: knowledge-moment reconstruction, refusal of the evidentiary hierarchy, and site-grounded scale. These are valuable moves. But they still operate within the epistemological frame that Larkin's critique disrupts: they are answers to the question "how do we produce better evidence of harm?" rather than to the question "how do we contest the terms of legibility within which harm is constituted?"

Knowledge-moment reconstruction ("who knew what, when, and what they chose to do") is an evidentiary strategy for making decision-making legible. Refusal of the evidentiary hierarchy challenges which kinds of knowledge count. Site-grounded scale prioritizes the community's epistemic position. These are good. But they all presuppose that the problem is one of evidence-production and evidence-reception. Larkin's framework suggests a fourth, prior move: contesting the *subject* of accountability — the kind of actor that the infrastructure produces as accountable.

The Green Screen paper diagnoses the ESG apparatus as producing "accountability as a product" — a corporate subject that has discharged its obligations through certification. But the methodology section does not address how to contest that subject's constitution. The juridical imagination required is not about evidence for a tribunal. It is about what kind of subject the tribunal is designed to call to account, and how to challenge the architecture that produces that subject rather than merely trying to introduce better evidence within it.

**Revision suggestion.** §7 needs a fourth methodological commitment, or a substantial revision of the existing three, oriented toward what might be called *subject-constitution analysis*: tracing how the infrastructure produces its own responsible actors and legitimate victims, and developing practices that can contest that production rather than merely improve its evidentiary record. This is not a methodology for a lawsuit. It is a methodology for a different kind of political claim — one that addresses the infrastructure's semiotic and subject-constituting functions directly.

## Core rewrite thesis

The paper needs to shift its animating question. Currently: *how do we make a functioning infrastructure visible as violence?* After Larkin: *how do we contest the particular visibility the infrastructure produces — its mode of address, its constitution of subjects, its semiotic organization of harm as progress?*

This does not require abandoning the paper's political economy framework. It requires layering a semiotic and aesthetic-political dimension on top of it, such that the double concealment the paper identifies is understood as operating not merely through invisibility but through a very specific, very powerful form of visibility — one that interpellates viewers, produces subjects, generates desire and fantasy, and thereby makes challenge appear not as legitimate contestation but as refusal of a shared future.

The green screen is not a screen because it hides things. It is a screen because it projects.
