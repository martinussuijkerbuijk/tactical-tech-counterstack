## Abstract

The energy transition is narrated as a civilizational break from the fossil-fuel economy that produced the climate crisis. This paper argues that it is not. What the green transition inherits from fossil capitalism is not merely corporate actors or financial instruments but an entire infrastructure: the legal architectures for territorial appropriation, the financial logics of asset management and rentier accumulation, the certification systems that perform accountability without providing it, and the spatial grammar of center-periphery extraction that has organized the metabolic relationship between the Global North and South since the colonial period. The paper develops four interlocking conceptual moves. First, it theorizes the "green screen" not simply as a concealment mechanism but as a dual operation: it simultaneously hides the extractive logic of the transition and provides a projection surface — indeterminate enough to absorb radically different political desires — onto which climate justice, national security, technological modernization, and corporate ESG can all be simultaneously displayed. Second, drawing on Larkin (2013) against Star (1999), it shows that the infrastructure of the green transition is not invisible but operates across a managed spectrum of visibilities: hypervisible at the scale of global climate governance and opaque at the scale of community-level ecological harm. The problem is not invisibility but the political architecture of which visibilities count as actionable. Third, the paper analyses the affective and desire dimension of green infrastructure — the genuine enthusiasm, pride, and aspirations that renewable energy projects generate even among communities they harm — and argues that this affective interpellation is itself part of the infrastructure's political work, not merely a distortion to be corrected by better information. Fourth, the paper develops the concept of para-infrastructure to describe the resistive sociotechnical fields that communities and activists construct in the shadow of dominant extraction systems. Methodologically, the paper asks: what investigative research methodologies and practices are necessary to make contemporary infrastructures — dispersed across time, space, jurisdiction, and ontology — present in forums capable of holding them to account?

## 1. The Green Screen

The energy transition is routinely narrated as a departure — a civilizational break from the fossil-fuel economy that produced the climate crisis. The numbers are genuinely staggering: the International Energy Agency estimates that reaching net-zero by 2050 requires roughly $5 trillion in clean energy investment annually by the early 2030s, a scale of infrastructure construction without precedent in human history. New grids, new mines, new pipelines, new port facilities, new processing plants, new transmission corridors threading through territories that have not previously been integrated into global commodity chains. The physical fact of this construction is real. What is ideological is the claim that it represents a structural break rather than a structural extension — that the energy transition departs from the institutional, legal, and corporate architectures that organized fossil capital, rather than inheriting and renovating them.

The inheritance is not incidental. The green transition is being organized, with uncomfortable precision, primarily through asset-management logic: the same investment vehicles, the same return-on-investment calculus, the same financial architectures that have historically channeled capital into fossil fuel extraction are now channeling it into wind farms, solar arrays, and battery-mineral supply chains (Christophers, 2023). The relevant question is not simply whether clean energy infrastructure gets built, but who profits from it, under what contractual arrangements, and with what distribution of risk between private investors and the publics who will pay for it. The green transition is being built by and for the same class of asset managers and institutional investors that built the fossil fuel economy — not despite the climate crisis but in response to it, treating decarbonization as a new frontier of capital accumulation.

This is the logic of cheap nature made newly legible — what Jason Moore calls the "four cheaps": labor, food, energy, and raw materials appropriated from zones that the market undervalues relative to their actual contribution to social reproduction (Moore, 2015). Each historical frontier of accumulation identifies a new pool of cheap nature and converts it into the metabolic substrate of profitability. Fossil fuels were one such frontier. The battery-mineral economy — cobalt extracted from the Congolese Copperbelt under conditions the UN has documented as constituting forced labor, nickel smelted in Indonesia's Special Economic Zones, lithium brine pumped from salt flats across the Andean lithosphere, rare earths processed in facilities that generate tailings ponds of radioactive waste in Jiangxi province — is another (Riofrancos, 2024). The frontier shifts; the logic of appropriation does not.

The fossil fuel economy was not adopted because coal and oil were the most efficient energy sources available in the nineteenth century — wind and water were abundant, technically exploitable, and in many respects superior. Fossil fuels were adopted because they were portable, accumulating, and because they enabled a decisive shift in the geography of labor discipline. Steam power untethered production from river sites where workers had organized, concentrated it in controllable urban mills, and gave capital a spatial mobility that labor could not match. The choice of energy infrastructure was a choice about social relations (Malm, 2016). The green transition has not disturbed this structural fact. Offshore wind farms in Sámi territory in northern Norway, solar installations across the Saharan margins of Morocco, geothermal projects in the Kenyan Rift Valley: these are organized through legal frameworks that derive from colonial land tenure, financed through capital structures that extract yield upward into asset-management portfolios, and sited in territories where communities lack the legal standing to contest expropriation. The energy source changes; the direction of value flow does not.

Different energy regimes do not merely produce different environmental outcomes — they encode different political possibilities. The infrastructural geography of coal made labor strikes in the mines and at the choke points of distribution politically potent in ways that were structurally unavailable in the oil economy, where pipelines and tankers diffused control and diminished labor's leverage. Energy infrastructure is, on this account, a political constitution written in steel, concrete, and territorial law (Mitchell, 2011). The transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy does not produce a political tabula rasa; it reorganizes the geography of control. The new choke points are the processing facilities for battery minerals — a handful of Chinese facilities process the vast majority of the world's cobalt, lithium, and rare earths [†] — and the corporate structures that hold long-term offtake agreements across the supply chain. The political constitution is being rewritten, but the authors are the same.

What renders this continuity politically illegible is not simply the green designation itself. The green screen operates through a double function that distinguishes it from ordinary ideological concealment. It performs, simultaneously, both concealment and projection. As concealment, it hides the extractive logic: the lithium mine does not appear as an extension of extractive capitalism, but as a contribution to the Paris Agreement. As projection surface, it operates more subtly and more powerfully: the "green" signifier is deliberately empty enough, flexible enough, to receive the imprint of radically different political desires. Climate justice movements, national security strategists, corporate ESG managers, and developmental economists can all project their preferred image onto the same infrastructure. The screen does not impose a single ideological message. It absorbs many, simultaneously, neutralizing their contradictions by enrolling them all under the indeterminate sign of the green. This indeterminacy of projection is what makes the screen so effective: there is no single false claim to refute, no unified ideological line to contest. The screen holds the projections of those who oppose each other within it.

The narrative of planetary necessity — the framing that makes climate action the most urgent imperative of our historical moment — therefore functions to foreclose political contestation in a specific way: by making any challenge to a green infrastructure project appear as a rejection of the transition itself. To challenge a lithium mine is to delay the energy transition; to obstruct a wind farm in Sámi territory is to prioritize local interests over the fate of the climate system. But this is the screen's concealment function alone. Its projection function does something further: it provides each political actor with a genuine stake in the infrastructure, a reason that is specific to their own values and interests. The investor sees yield. The state sees sovereignty. The engineer sees technical achievement. The climate activist sees decarbonization. None of these investments is false. They are all real projections onto the same surface. The communities who bear the infrastructure's costs have no equivalent projection — they have the reality of the mine, not its image.

What follows works through the mechanisms of this double operation. Section 2 analyzes the politics of visibility that the infrastructure produces — neither pure invisibility nor transparent legibility, but a managed spectrum in which different visibilities are made available to different actors. Sections 3 and 4 examine the financial and certification architectures that maintain this management. Section 5 reads the green transition's spatial grammar as colonial continuity — not merely through its legal instruments but through the aesthetic and civilizational grammar it inherits. Section 6 develops the double bind and, following Larkin (2013), extends it to include the dimension of desire and affective interpellation. Section 7 introduces para-infrastructure as a concept for resistive sociotechnical fields and reorients the paper's methodology toward the problem of presence: what does it mean to make a dispersed, multi-temporal infrastructure present in forums capable of holding it to account? Section 8 frames the problem that remains.

## 2. Infrastructure and the Politics of Visibility

The most consequential theoretical claim about infrastructure in recent decades is also its most over-extended: the claim that infrastructure is invisible until it breaks down. This claim, derived from Susan Leigh Star's foundational account (Star, 1999), has become something close to a disciplinary commonplace — invoked in almost every study of infrastructure as its opening premise. Larkin states the corrective plainly: "Thus many studies that begin by stating how infrastructures are invisible until they break down are fundamentally inaccurate. Infrastructures are metapragmatic objects, signs of themselves deployed in particular circulatory regimes to establish sets of effects" (Larkin, 2013, p. 336). Invisibility is one point on a spectrum that runs from the genuinely backgrounded to the hypervisible spectacle, and the politics of infrastructure cannot be read off from either extreme alone.

Star's account remains valuable, but what it describes is a political condition, not a technical one: infrastructure becomes the unremarked background of collective life for those who benefit from it, who have absorbed its conventions through membership in a community of practice. The breakdown that triggers visibility is a breakdown legible to the infrastructure's users and beneficiaries. When the power goes out in Berlin or London, the electrical grid becomes visible. When the cobalt supply chain produces cardiomyopathy in Kolwezi, the infrastructure does not become visible in Berlin or London, because the breakdown is not experienced there. The infrastructure serves different communities in radically different ways, and Star's visibility-upon-breakdown principle applies only to those whom the infrastructure serves. For communities that constitute the infrastructure's resource base rather than its beneficiary population, breakdown may be locally visible but entirely untranslatable into globally actionable claims.

What is more analytically productive than the invisibility thesis is the concept of a managed visibility spectrum. The battery-mineral economy is not invisible. It is one of the most thoroughly documented sectors of the global economy — the subject of IEA Critical Minerals Reports, UN Special Rapporteur investigations, investigative journalism across four continents, major NGO campaigns, and EU legislative processes. The cobalt belt is known; the lithium salares are photographed by satellites and cited in every major energy transition document. The Sámi wind farm cases went to the Norwegian Supreme Court. The harm is visible. What is managed is the *kind* of visibility each actor gets, and the political significance assigned to each register.

For the ESG investor, the infrastructure is visible as a certified, disclosed, scored asset: transparent in the financial sense. For the climate movement, it is visible as decarbonization: praiseworthy and urgently necessary. For the regulatory authority, it is visible as a permitted, impact-assessed, consultation-documented project: legally legitimate. For the community whose aquifer is declining, it is visible as a drying spring, a weakened pasture, a retreating flamingo population: a concrete, embodied, locally observed reality. These are not competing claims about the same invisible thing. They are different modes of visibility, produced by and for the different systems through which the infrastructure operates. The infrastructure manages its own visibility by generating different legitimate images of itself for different audiences simultaneously.

The key political fact this analysis produces is that breakdown — in the sense that Star means — does not interrupt the infrastructure's operation, because the breakdown that would render the system visible to those who benefit from it is precisely what the system's design prevents. The cobalt supply chain continues to function. The lithium operation continues to hold its permits. The ESG score continues to be generated on schedule. The documentation is in order. In Star's terms, breakdown does not occur — and so visibility, in Star's terms, does not occur. But the silence on breakdown at the system level conceals a continuous breakdown at the community level: the spring that weakens, the soil that acidifies, the migration corridor that closes. The community experiences breakdown continuously. The infrastructure experiences smooth function. The gap between these two experiences is not incidental. It is architectured.

The architecture works through the design of knowledge systems. What can be known within an infrastructure — what questions are askable, what data is collected, what counts as evidence — is determined by the infrastructure's design (Edwards, 2010). The infrastructure of mineral certification was designed to answer a specific question: does this mineral originate from a conflict zone as defined by the Dodd-Frank Act's Section 1502? It was not designed to answer questions about long-term aquifer depletion, community health outcomes measured across decades, or the relationship between royalty rates and GDP distribution in mining-dependent economies. The slow harms — what Rob Nixon calls "slow violence," the incremental destructions that unfold across timescales that do not register in news cycles or quarterly financial reporting — are not merely difficult to document within this infrastructure (Nixon, 2011). They are outside the infrastructure's knowledge horizon entirely, because they are outside the questions its design was built to answer.

The violence that extraction produces is therefore not primarily the violence of breakdown. It is the violence of function. The infrastructure works, which means the harm it produces does not register as a malfunction requiring diagnosis. It registers as geology (the mine is where the mineral is), as development (the concession brings roads and formal employment), as necessary cost (the energy transition requires these materials). Each of these registrations is produced by an infrastructure — legal, financial, epistemic, discursive — that is functioning exactly as designed. The harm is infrastructure-shaped: distributed across jurisdictions so that no single actor bears legal responsibility; unfolding slowly enough that no single moment constitutes a legally cognizable harm; certified and documented in ways that produce the appearance of oversight without its substance. And it is, in many registers, entirely visible — it is simply visible in a way that no existing accountability mechanism is designed to receive as a claim.

## 3. The Asset-ification of Nature

If the green screen operates by managing the political visibility of extractive infrastructure, what holds that management in place institutionally is a financial architecture that has converted the living systems from which battery minerals are extracted into investment assets generating predictable yields. This conversion — the transformation of a lithium deposit, a nickel-bearing laterite, a cobalt seam into a balance-sheet item, an offtake agreement, a securitized revenue stream — is the operational heart of what this paper calls the infrastructure of violence. It is not experienced as violence because its accounting categories have no field for harm. The depletion of an aquifer appears as impairment of an asset. The displacement of an indigenous community appears as a development cost, or does not appear at all.

The dominant organizational form of the green transition is the asset-management fund, holding long-term contracted revenue streams from electricity offtake agreements, mining royalties, and infrastructure leases (Christophers, 2023). What distinguishes rentier capitalism from productive capitalism is that value is extracted from ownership rather than production — the landlord, the toll-road operator, the platform monopolist, and now the infrastructure asset manager generate returns from the fact of possession, not the act of making (Christophers, 2021). Clean energy infrastructure is peculiarly well-suited to rentier logic: once built, a wind farm or a lithium mine generates revenue streams that can be securitized, tranched, sold, and re-sold through the financial system. The mine does not need to be productive in any socially meaningful sense; it needs to generate yield. This distinction matters because it clarifies why ESG certification and responsible-sourcing frameworks have so consistently failed to alter the material conditions of extraction: they were designed to satisfy the requirements of the asset-management system, not to govern the mine.

The ESG certification apparatus deserves particular analytical attention because it has successfully occupied the space where accountability mechanisms should be while performing almost none of accountability's substantive functions. Larkin's reading of Mbembe's concept of "doubling" sharpens the diagnosis considerably. Mbembe analyzes the proliferation of formal documents — budgets, contracts, job descriptions, certificates — in postcolonial states that "bear little relation to reality." But Larkin's critical gloss resists reading these as failures: the documents function politically not despite but through their doubling quality. They are not failed representations of a reality they fail to capture. They are successful productions of a different reality — a reality of institutional existence, regulatory standing, contracted obligation. Lea and Pholeros, studying aboriginal housing infrastructure in Australia, make this precise: "Pipes turn out to be documents" (Lea & Pholeros, 2010, p. 191). The pipe that does not connect to a sewer still attaches a dwelling to "techniques of regulation, audit, and administration." The pipe circulates as documentation — it allows reports to be written and budgets to be satisfied — regardless of whether it connects to anything.

Applied to the ESG apparatus: the IRMA certification does not fail to represent the material conditions of the mine. It successfully constitutes the corporation as a responsible actor within the regulatory imaginary of green finance. The certificate produces a subject — the "responsible miner," the "ESG-compliant operator" — whose existence within the financial-regulatory system is entirely real regardless of what happens at the mine site. This is a much harder structure to challenge than documentary failure, because it cannot be fixed by better data. The certification is not claiming to represent the mine accurately; it is claiming to constitute the miner as a subject who has discharged obligations within a specific institutional architecture. That constitution is real. What it is not is accountable to anyone outside that architecture — including the community whose water supply it certified as unaffected.

Corporate baselines for emissions and community impact are generated by the companies themselves, using methodologies they select and metrics they calibrate. Third-party auditors are contracted and paid by the companies they assess, creating a conflict of interest structurally identical to the one that produced fraudulent credit ratings in the 2008 financial crisis. The disclosure thresholds and reporting standards are calibrated for securities regulators — for the informational requirements of financial markets — rather than for the communities whose territories are being mined. A community in the Katanga province whose water supply has been contaminated by cobalt tailings has no standing in the CDP rating system, no mechanism for disputing the corporate baseline, no appeal procedure for the IRMA certification that stamped the mine's social performance as "advancing." The architecture produces legibility within the asset-management system while systematically excluding from legibility the claims of those whose lives constitute the externalities.

The market economy's foundational violence is transposed here into the register of green finance: the treatment of land, labor, and money as "fictitious commodities" — things that behave within the market as if produced for sale, but whose commodification destroys the social fabric of which they are constitutive (Polanyi, 1944). What capitalist accounting calls "free gifts of nature" are not free — they have been produced through accumulated ecological processes and through the active land-management practices of communities who are dispossessed at the moment of their incorporation into the commodity frontier (Moore, 2015). The appropriation of cheap nature is always also the appropriation of what Ariel Salleh calls metabolic value — the accumulated relational labor, ecological knowledge, and social reproduction that communities invest in their territories and that disappears from the balance sheet at the moment of expropriation (Salleh, 2017). The mine arrives in a landscape that has been produced — hydrologically, ecologically, relationally — by generations of human and non-human labor. It arrives, in the accounting system's terms, in an empty space awaiting valorization.

## 4. Green Platform Capitalism and the New Opacity Layer

The last decade has produced a new stratum of institutional infrastructure layered over the extractive economy: a sprawling complex of ESG ratings agencies, carbon accounting platforms, supply chain transparency initiatives, and mineral certification schemes. The Responsible Minerals Initiative, the Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance, the Carbon Disclosure Project, the Science Based Targets initiative: these organizations present themselves as the accountability infrastructure of a cleaned-up capitalism. They are, in structural terms, something else: not failed accountability mechanisms but successful mechanisms for something other than accountability — permission infrastructures for extraction, dressed in the idiom of transparency.

Platform capitalism offers more than a metaphor here; it offers a structural diagnosis. The platform, as a business model, extracts value from data by positioning itself as the indispensable intermediary between parties who cannot find or evaluate each other without it. The platform need not produce the service it mediates; it need only make itself necessary to the transaction (Srnicek, 2017). Applied to green accountability, this logic generates a specific outcome: ESG data providers, supply chain auditors, and carbon accountants extract value from the performance of transparency rather than from transparency itself. The corporation that commissions the audit, defines its scope, selects the certification standard, and funds the body that issues the standard has not been made accountable — it has purchased accountability as a product. The certification is simultaneously a commercial service and a political shield.

What the ESG platform accomplishes — beyond the documentary doubling analyzed in the previous section — is a specific form of epistemic capture. The platform does not merely produce documentation. It sets the parameters within which accountable behavior is defined: what counts as responsible sourcing, what a passing score on community engagement looks like, how environmental impact is aggregated across a portfolio. To own the metric is to own the definition of responsibility. The corporation that operates within the metric's parameters is, within the system the metric produces, genuinely responsible. This is not a trick. It is an architecture. And the architecture has no addressable point of appeal for the community that experiences the mine's effects as harm rather than as metric compliance.

Supply chain disclosure requirements in the battery-mineral economy have thus far operated within this logic: disclosure generates data whose formats are determined by corporate reporting standards, which are shaped through consultation processes in which industry representatives hold disproportionate weight (Riofrancos, 2024). The result is a disclosure architecture that produces enormous quantities of information while foreclosing the comparisons that would make that information actionable. A community in the Katanga province cannot compare its recorded groundwater levels against the corporate monitoring baseline that was set before the concession began — because establishing a meaningful baseline would have made the harm measurable, and measurable harm creates liability. The absence of the baseline is not an oversight. It is a designed condition that forecloses attribution. Mapping designed absences in the data record is itself a form of accountability work, and one that the existing certification architecture cannot produce.

Data extraction constitutes a new form of colonial appropriation — structurally homologous to the enclosure of land, converting the social relation of accountability itself into a commodity (Couldry & Mejias, 2019). The entity being assessed loses the ability to be judged by any standard it did not help construct. The platform owns the metric; the metric produces the score; the score circulates as fact. The green platform does not resolve the politics of extraction. It archives them.

## 5. Colonial Continuity and the Grammar of Green Modernity

There is a spatial pattern to the green transition that any honest cartography makes immediately visible. The cobalt that stabilizes electric vehicle batteries comes primarily from the Congolese Copperbelt. The nickel that anchors cathode chemistry is processed at scale in Indonesia's Sulawesi corridor. The lithium that powers storage systems is pumped from brine operations across the Andean altiplano. Rare earths critical to wind turbine magnets are mined and processed across Inner Mongolia and Jiangxi province [†]. Solar farms are being built at scale across the Sahara and the Sahel to supply European electricity demand. Wind concessions are being issued on Sámi territory in Norway and on Diné land in the American Southwest [†]. The metabolic geography of the green transition replicates, with remarkable fidelity, the metabolic geography of colonial capitalism: extraction occurs in the periphery; value accumulates at the center; the costs are borne by those who did not negotiate the terms.

This is not merely an analogy. The legal, territorial, and financial architectures through which the battery-mineral economy operates are not reminiscent of colonial extraction — they are substantially the same architectures, updated for the contemporary legal order. The concession system, the enclave economy, the bilateral investment treaty, the free trade zone, the public-private partnership: these instruments were developed, refined, and juridically entrenched across decades of colonial and postcolonial resource extraction. They do not need to be redesigned for the green transition because they were never dismantled. What changes is the idiom. "Development" becomes "green development." "Resource extraction for metropolitan industry" becomes "energy transition investment." The concession issued for copper mining in the 1960s and the concession issued for lithium brine extraction today are instruments of the same legal genealogy operating in the same territorial logic.

But the green transition inherits more than legal instruments. It inherits a sensory-political grammar — an aesthetic order through which modernity imposes itself on the territories it appropriates, and through which those territories come to appear as surfaces awaiting improvement. This dimension of colonial infrastructure has been analyzed most precisely by Mrazek (2002) in his study of Dutch road-building in colonial Indonesia and, following him, by Larkin (2013). Dutch roads imposed a civilizational order through their material properties: the hardness of asphalt against the softness of Indonesian mud, the cleanness of the road surface against the dust carried by indigenous bodies, the steady surface against the variable, wet, tropically resistant terrain. "Cleanness of the roads, in this logic," Mrazek argues, "was purity of the times, democracy even" (Mrazek, 2002, p. 8). The colonial road was not only a technical system; it was a language — a set of sensory-political propositions about what modernity was, what carried it, and what threatened it. Indigenous bodies were constituted as risks to the road's integrity, carriers of the soft stuff that would degrade its surface.

Green energy infrastructure carries an analogous sensory-political grammar. The sleek white wind turbine against a sky; the solar panel array extending across a semi-arid landscape; the gleaming lithium processing facility at altitude: these are not technically neutral forms. They are aesthetic propositions about what modernity looks like, what kind of territory is a good surface for progress, and what kinds of life-forms are obstacles to the clean transition. The pastoral landscape of Sámi reindeer herding, the subsistence agriculture of the high Andean salares, the artisanal mining of the Copperbelt: within the aesthetic grammar of green modernity, these appear as the soft stuff — the not-yet-modern that the infrastructure will improve, connect, replace, or simply absorb. This grammar does not need to be consciously deployed to function. It is built into the material form of the infrastructure: into what a solar installation looks like, into what a modern mining facility's corporate communications convey about the territory it enters, into the visual language of impact assessment documents.

The same territories organized as extraction peripheries under colonial and postcolonial fossil capitalism are now being organized as solar and wind energy peripheries for European consumption — a pattern the green transition in the Arab world and North Africa makes visible with particular clarity (Hamouchene, 2023). The EU's Mediterranean energy policy replicates the spatial logic of earlier extractive arrangements. The legal instrument is the concession in each case. The political question — who determines what happens to the land, the water, the wind, the sun — is structured by the same power asymmetries. The addition of a "green" justification alters neither the concession nor the power asymmetry; it provides both with a new legitimating vocabulary that is harder to contest because it speaks in the language of global survival.

Rendering the aesthetic-civilizational dimension of this inheritance legible is a task to which artistic research methods are specifically suited — and to which conventional investigative documentation cannot easily reach. The sensory-political grammar of a wind farm is not visible in a corporate permit, a financial prospectus, or an environmental impact assessment. It is visible in the photographs, the design language, the site ceremonies, the corporate communications that construct the infrastructure's public image; and it is felt in the embodied experience of communities who encounter its form before they feel its technical effects. Artistic research that engages this dimension — that attends to the aesthetics of address through which green infrastructure constitutes its subjects — is not supplementary to the political economy analysis. It is a methodological necessity for reaching dimensions of the problem that political economy alone cannot access.

Recognition frameworks — the consultation requirement, the impact and benefit agreement, the community benefit fund, the FPIC protocol — can function as instruments of continued dispossession when what they extend is recognition of cultural identity rather than practical territorial sovereignty (Coulthard, 2014). The practical capacity to refuse extraction — to say no and have that no be binding — is not what recognition frameworks in their current form deliver. They deliver consultation. They deliver a process. They deliver documentation that the community was heard. What they do not deliver is the territorial authority to determine whether extraction proceeds at all.

Coloniality is not a historical period that ended with formal decolonization but an ongoing structure of power — a matrix that organizes knowledge, authority, and economy in ways that persist long after the administrative form of colonial governance has been withdrawn (Mignolo & Walsh, 2018). The green transition demonstrates this with particular clarity: it has arrived with new institutions, new vocabularies, and new legitimating frameworks, but the underlying territorial arrangements reproduce the colonial matrix with a fidelity that formal institutional analysis consistently underestimates. The green screen, on this reading, does not merely project an image of progress onto extractive reality. It projects, specifically, an image of civilizational improvement onto territories whose non-modern forms of life have been constituted, through the same colonial grammar, as awaiting that improvement.

## 6. The Double Bind of Desire and Harm

There is a trap built into the political logic of the green energy transition, and it operates at two levels that reinforce each other. The first is the level the paper has already described: the epistemic and political foreclosure achieved by the climate urgency frame. When communities in the high Andean salares contest the expansion of lithium brine extraction, they are contesting an operation whose legal legitimacy, financial backing, and political cover derive substantially from its position in the battery supply chain — and therefore from its contribution to the prevention of climate breakdown. The chain of equivalences that converts challenge to the mine into challenge to decarbonization is a structural feature of how the two systems — extraction infrastructure and climate governance — have been articulated together (Riofrancos, 2024; Hamouchene, 2023). The foreclosure is architectural.

The second level of the bind is affective, and it is the level that the paper's earlier draft undertheorized. Larkin (2013), following Benjamin and De Boeck, argues that infrastructure operates through desire and fantasy as much as through technical function or political concealment. Roads, railways, and energy systems generate "complicated emotional investments that induce a range of sometimes counterintuitive responses." De Boeck's ethnography of Kinshasa is precise on this: residents evicted to make way for an elite urban development overwhelmingly supported the project and experienced it with pride, even as they acknowledged themselves to be its primary victims. One fisherman: "Yes, we'll be the victims... but still it will be beautiful" (De Boeck, 2011, p. 278). This is not false consciousness. It is the genuine affect generated by infrastructure's promise — its capacity to constitute subjects as participants in a project of modernity, development, and possibility, simultaneously with its displacement of those same subjects.

The green transition generates this affect at considerable scale. The wind turbine and the solar installation are beautiful to many people who encounter them, including to communities in territories where they are built. The engineering achievement is genuine. The promise of connection to global modernity — of being on the right side of a civilizational transformation — is emotionally real. These investments are not cynical projections imposed on passive recipients. They are the infrastructure's mode of address, its specific way of constituting political subjects as stakeholders in a shared project.

The Brazilian federal highway BR-319, connecting Porto Velho to Manaus through approximately 900 kilometers of Amazon rainforest, offers an instructive case study in this affective dimension. The highway, built under the military dictatorship in the 1970s and largely abandoned thereafter, has been a persistent subject of controversy as successive governments consider reconstruction and paving. Environmentalists and many indigenous rights advocates have opposed reconstruction consistently, citing projected deforestation patterns from road access. But a significant number of communities along the route — including some indigenous communities whose territories the highway passes through or adjoins — have expressed support for road reconstruction, citing decades of enforced isolation: inadequate access to healthcare, educational facilities, and markets; difficulty in reaching administrative centers; an infrastructure of disconnection that the decaying highway has imposed on them (IBGE [†]; ISA [†]). The desire for the road is not ignorance of deforestation dynamics. It is a rational response to the harm that the absence of infrastructure has also produced — a harm that the environmental opposition to reconstruction rarely accounts for. The double bind closes at the community level: the infrastructure threatens territorial sovereignty and generates genuine desire simultaneously, within the same subject.

The same dynamic operates wherever the green transition deploys its infrastructure. The Kenyan Rift Valley geothermal project brings electricity — not merely as a resource but as a transformative material condition — to communities that have not had reliable grid access. The wind farm on Sámi land generates employment and, in some cases, community benefit agreements whose cash flows represent the first formal income stream from a territory whose economic integration into the national economy has been on the state's terms. The desire for these benefits is real. The harm from the infrastructure is also real. The politics produced by this coexistence is not the politics of straightforward resistance that the infrastructure's critics prefer to narrate. It is a politics of ambivalence, negotiation, and uneven distribution — in which some community members accept the infrastructure, some resist, some do both in sequence, and all are constituted as subjects by the infrastructure's address before they have the chance to decide.

What this means for the analysis of the double bind is that the bind is tighter and more durable than the epistemic version alone suggests. The epistemic bind — climate urgency forecloses challenge — can in principle be loosened by better politics, by decoupling climate action from the specific financial form through which it is being organized. The affective bind — desire and harm coexist in the same infrastructure, in the same subject — cannot be loosened by exposing the infrastructure's extractive character. The communities who desire the road, the electricity, the mine's employment, the connection to global modernity that the infrastructure promises are not making a mistake that better information will correct. They are holding, simultaneously, two real relationships to the same infrastructure, and the political challenge is to develop forms of collective action adequate to that complexity rather than narrating desire as distortion.

The second level of the bind is also epistemic, in a specific sense. Communities in extraction zones are not ignorant of the harm. The farmers in Lickanantay communities on the margins of the high-altitude salares know that the springs their ancestors used no longer flow. The women in Kolwezi who draw water from streams running orange know that something has changed. The Sámi reindeer herder knows which migration corridors are now blocked by transmission lines. This knowledge is local, embodied, and historically deep. What it lacks is not accuracy but convertibility: the capacity to translate local, embodied knowledge of harm into forms that achieve legibility within legal, financial, or journalistic systems calibrated to a different standard of evidence. This is where the threshold of detectability operates. The threshold is not a natural feature of the harm but is politically set and technically maintained — states and corporations produce harm in ways calibrated to remain below the threshold at which evidence achieves legal or political recognition (Weizman, 2017). The communities bearing these costs are the primary holders of evidence that the harm has crossed that threshold. They are also the actors with the least institutional standing to present that evidence in the forums where it would matter.

## 7. Para-Infrastructure and the Problem of Presence

The methodological problem this paper has been building toward can now be stated more precisely than in most existing accounts. It is not the problem of making the invisible visible — the infrastructure is not primarily invisible. It is the problem of making a dispersed, multi-temporal, multi-scalar infrastructure *present* — present in forums and institutions capable of holding it to account; present in a form that connects the community's local embodied knowledge to the financial decision made in Luxembourg; present in a way that is adequate to its ontological complexity, which is simultaneously material, legal, financial, aesthetic, and affective.

The aggregation imperative carries a misleading logic. It is the belief that visibility is achieved through data at scale, that the accumulation of evidence in one place produces knowledge, that comprehensive mapping is the condition of accountability. This is the epistemology of the extractive infrastructure itself: the belief that the world is a set of resources to be drawn toward a center, processed at that center's resolution, and returned as insight to actors positioned to act on it. The counter-platform that aggregates harm data across the global battery-mineral supply chain is structurally isomorphic to the supply chain it documents — both extract information from peripheral sites, process it at industrial scale, and return value to actors with institutional capacity to use it. The communities in the Andean altiplano, the Congo's Copperbelt, and the rare-earth mining regions provide the raw material for both. The aggregation platform promises comprehensiveness and delivers a different kind of invisibility — one in which all the data is present and the accountability claim still cannot be made.

Infrastructure becomes present — in the specific sense we are developing — not through comprehensive documentation but through the specificity of situated encounters between the infrastructure and the lives it shapes. The communities living near lithium extraction operations in the high Andean salares have known for years that the aquifer is declining. Indigenous water guardians carry intergenerational knowledge of pre-extraction hydrological conditions that no satellite sensor can reproduce — a temporal depth of observation that provides calibrated baselines the corporate monitoring systems never established, because establishing them would have made the harm measurable. The knowledge exists. The problem is that knowledge of harm does not become accountability until it can move through institutions capable of receiving it. The community's knowledge of the drying spring cannot achieve legal legibility in a tribunal system designed to receive corporate-generated, quantitatively-formatted, causally-disaggregated evidence. The harm is known but legally mute — not because the data is absent, but because the evidentiary architecture was calibrated to exclude this kind of knowing.

A central concept for the methodological work that follows is **para-infrastructure**. Communities that resist, contest, or attempt to survive extractive infrastructure do not only respond. They build. The community water monitoring network, the documentation project that maps ancestral land use, the legal cooperative that pools resources for tribunal claims, the activist journalism network that traces beneficial ownership chains, the social media archive that records community meetings and consultation processes: these are not simply resistance. They share the key characteristics of infrastructure — they route, distribute, accumulate, standardize, connect, and provide a taken-for-granted background for the practices they enable. They are counter-formations that operate in the shadow of and in tension with the dominant extractive infrastructure. Para-infrastructure names this quality: not anti-infrastructure in the sense of pure opposition, but an alternative infrastructural logic that makes different flows possible, creates different communities of practice, and generates different knowledge about the territory it operates in.

Para-infrastructure is epistemologically significant precisely because it is infrastructural: it generates an installed base of knowledge, practice, and relationship that is not dependent on the extractive infrastructure for its operation. The community monitoring network that establishes its own hydrological baseline before the mine arrives — or reconstructs one from oral history and intergenerational knowledge after it has — is not merely producing counter-data. It is establishing a counter-knowledge infrastructure whose temporal depth and community embeddedness the corporate monitoring system cannot replicate. The question of what para-infrastructural formations are already operating in extraction zones, and what conditions allow them to generate knowledge that achieves legibility beyond their immediate communities, is one of the central methodological questions for the research program this paper inaugurates.

Three practices follow from this reframing — not as a deliverable methodology but as intellectual and political commitments about what this kind of work requires.

The first is **knowledge-moment reconstruction**. The infrastructure of violence is not only a structure; it is a sequence of decisions made by specific actors with specific authority at specific moments of awareness. The regulatory official who receives a hydrological assessment identifying aquifer stress and renews the extraction permit without modification makes a decision at a datable moment with documented institutional knowledge. The corporate board that receives environmental audit findings and authorizes continued operations makes decisions. The financial institutions that extend credit lines while receiving sustainability risk reports make decisions. Forensic architecture demonstrates the power of this reconstructive move: the material traces of events can be assembled into cross-examinable temporal accounts that name who knew what, and when, and what they chose to do with that knowledge (Weizman, 2017). Applied to the infrastructure of the green transition, this approach produces the claim that these specific actors, in these specific positions, with this specific knowledge, made these specific decisions — a claim that courts can hear, that investigative journalism can verify, that UN mechanisms can receive.

The second practice concerns the **evidentiary hierarchy** that accountability processes currently reproduce. Satellite observation is treated as epistemically superior to intergenerational memory. Corporate-formatted quantitative data is treated as superior to community testimony. These assumptions are not neutral; they are a sediment of the same colonial epistemology that structured the extraction economy (Mignolo & Walsh, 2018). An adequate methodology must refuse these subordinations procedurally, not just rhetorically — developing evidentiary practices that hold a water guardian's decades of observation and a satellite interferometry reading of groundwater subsidence in the same argumentative frame, neither subordinated to the other, each serving as a check on the other's blind spots. The absence of well-specific extraction data from a corporation's public filings is not a gap to be filled; it is evidence of a disclosure architecture designed to prevent attribution. Mapping the absences with the same analytical rigor applied to present data is itself a form of accountability.

The third practice addresses the **aesthetic-political register** that conventional investigative and legal methods cannot access. The sensory-political grammar through which green infrastructure constitutes its subjects — the visual language of wind turbines and solar arrays as signs of modernity; the affective address of clean energy as civilizational progress — operates below the threshold of documentary evidence. It cannot be captured in corporate filings or satellite images. Artistic research methodologies — those that attend to embodied experience, material qualities, and aesthetic address — are not supplementary to the investigation of green infrastructure's harms. They are necessary for reaching dimensions of the problem that the evidentiary systems of law and journalism are not designed to recognize. The aesthetic-civilizational grammar of green modernity, which positions certain territories and communities as surfaces for improvement, is as much a site of political contestation as the concession agreement. Making it legible as such requires methods adequate to its form.

## 8. Conclusion: The Dispersion Problem

This paper has argued that the green energy transition does not constitute a break from the infrastructure of extractive capitalism. It constitutes that infrastructure's renovation: the same legal architectures, territorial arrangements, and corporate structures that enabled the fossil fuel economy now power the battery-mineral economy, extended and re-legitimized by the moral emergency of the climate crisis. The green screen that secures this re-legitimization is not a simple hiding device. It performs a double operation: it conceals the extractive logic behind the green designation, and it provides a projection surface blank enough to accommodate the desires and political investments of actors who would otherwise be in conflict with each other. What makes it structurally durable is precisely this indeterminacy — the screen does not impose a single ideological message, which means it cannot be challenged by refuting any single claim.

The scale problem that the paper's first draft named as its central unresolved question has been reframed as a dispersion problem, and the reframing matters. Scale is a question of size: the infrastructure is planetary; accountability mechanisms are national or local; the gap persists. Dispersion is a question of ontological distribution: the infrastructure is simultaneously material (the mine, the pipeline, the transmission line), legal (the concession, the bilateral investment treaty, the certification standard), financial (the offtake agreement, the asset-management vehicle, the ESG score), aesthetic (the visual language of clean modernity, the sensory-political grammar of green development), and affective (the desire for electricity, employment, connection, the pride of participating in a civilizational project). No single investigative methodology is adequate to all of these dimensions simultaneously. No single accountability forum is designed to receive claims made across all of them at once.

The challenge is not, however, primarily one of comprehensiveness. The aggregation platform — the database that maps all of these dimensions into a single comprehensive picture — reproduces the extractive logic it documents. The challenge is one of *presence*: developing investigative methodologies and practices that can make the dispersed infrastructure present in specific, situated forums in ways that are actionable. Presence is not visibility. Something can be visible without being present — the harm at the cobalt belt is visible in dozens of well-documented reports; it is not present in the board meeting where the offtake agreement is renewed. Making it present there requires a chain of translations, practices, and institutional formations that are themselves infrastructural in character — not a single platform, but a set of connected para-infrastructural capacities that route, distribute, accumulate, and translate local knowledge into forms receivable in multiple forums simultaneously.

This paper is a problem-statement, not a solution. More papers in this series will address specific dimensions of the dispersion problem: the legal architectures of accountability, the epistemic politics of certification, the artistic research methods adequate to the aesthetic-political register, the para-infrastructural formations that communities and allies are already building in extraction zones. The claim of this paper is that these subsequent investigations share a common orientation: they begin not from the infrastructure's global map but from its local address — from the drying spring, the blocked migration corridor, the river running orange, the community consultation that took place on a timeline controlled by the project's proponents — and work outward through the corporate and regulatory documentation surrounding that specific site of harm. They hold, simultaneously, the community's territorial knowledge and the financial instrument that authorized the extraction. They treat the designed absences in the documentation record as evidence rather than as gaps. And they take seriously — rather than dismissing as distortion — the genuine desire that the infrastructure generates in the communities it harms, because a politics that cannot account for that desire will not be adequate to the politics that desire helps produce.

The infrastructure is designed to look like a solution to the problem it perpetuates. Making that legible — making the green screen present as a political object rather than a background condition — is the work of the research program this paper opens.
