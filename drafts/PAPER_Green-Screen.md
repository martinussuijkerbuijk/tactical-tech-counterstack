# The Green Screen: Infrastructure of Violence and the Legitimation of Extractive Capitalism in the Energy Transition

**C3 Research Group — Working Paper**
*Draft v0.1 — June 2026*

## Abstract

The energy transition is being narrated as a civilizational break — a departure from the fossil-fuel economy that produced the climate crisis. This paper argues that it is not. What the green transition inherits from fossil capitalism is not merely a set of corporate actors or financial instruments but an entire infrastructure: the legal architectures for territorial appropriation, the financial logics of asset management and rentier accumulation, the certification systems that perform accountability without providing it, and the spatial grammar of center-periphery extraction that has organized the metabolic relationship between the Global North and South since the colonial period. The paper develops three interlocking conceptual moves. First, drawing on Star, Larkin, and Easterling, it shows how infrastructure operates as an invisibility machine — not by hiding harm but by making its production appear as the normal functioning of neutral technical systems. Second, it traces the "green screen" as a specific epistemological operation: the deployment of climate urgency as a legitimating frame that forecloses political challenge to extraction in the same gesture that it authorizes construction. Third, it develops the concept of a double bind structuring the politics of visibility in extraction zones: the conditions that would make local ecological breakdown legible are suppressed by the very frame — planetary climate emergency — that makes the infrastructure politically untouchable. Methodologically, the paper argues that the problem is not insufficient data but the absence of evidentiary practices capable of rendering a functioning infrastructure legible as a site of ongoing harm. The key question the paper reaches but cannot fully resolve: how do we make a system that is working exactly as designed visible as violence?

## 1. The Green Screen

The energy transition is routinely narrated as a departure — a civilizational break from the fossil-fuel economy that produced the climate crisis. The numbers are genuinely staggering: the International Energy Agency estimates that reaching net-zero by 2050 requires roughly $5 trillion in clean energy investment annually by the early 2030s, a scale of infrastructure construction without precedent in human history. New grids, new mines, new pipelines, new port facilities, new processing plants, new transmission corridors threading through territories that have not previously been integrated into global commodity chains. The physical fact of this construction is real. What is ideological is the claim that it represents a structural break rather than a structural extension — that the energy transition departs from the institutional, legal, and corporate architectures that organized fossil capital, rather than inheriting and renovating them.

The inheritance is not incidental. The green transition is being organized, with uncomfortable precision, primarily through asset-management logic: the same investment vehicles, the same return-on-investment calculus, the same financial architectures that have historically channeled capital into fossil fuel extraction are now channeling it into wind farms, solar arrays, and battery-mineral supply chains (Christophers, 2023). The relevant question is not simply whether clean energy infrastructure gets built, but who profits from it, under what contractual arrangements, and with what distribution of risk between private investors and the publics who will pay for it. The answer is that the green transition is being built by and for the same class of asset managers and institutional investors that built the fossil fuel economy — not despite the climate crisis but in response to it, treating decarbonization as a new frontier of capital accumulation.

This is the logic of cheap nature made newly legible — what Jason Moore calls the "four cheaps": labor, food, energy, and raw materials appropriated from zones that the market undervalues relative to their actual contribution to social reproduction (Moore, 2015). Each historical frontier of accumulation identifies a new pool of cheap nature and converts it into the metabolic substrate of profitability. Fossil fuels were one such frontier. The battery-mineral economy — the cobalt extracted from the Congolese Copperbelt under conditions the UN has documented as constituting forced labor, the nickel smelted in Indonesia's Special Economic Zones, the lithium brine pumped from salt flats across the Andean lithosphere, the rare earths processed in facilities that generate tailings ponds of radioactive waste in Jiangxi province — is another (Riofrancos, 2024). The frontier shifts; the logic of appropriation does not. Describing this shift as a transition from one energy regime to another is accurate at the level of the fuel source. Describing it as a transition from one economic logic to another is not.

The fossil fuel economy was not adopted because coal and oil were the most efficient energy sources available in the nineteenth century — wind and water were abundant, technically exploitable, and in many respects superior. Fossil fuels were adopted because they were portable, accumulating, and — crucially — because they enabled a decisive shift in the geography of labor discipline. Steam power untethered production from river sites where workers had organized, concentrated it in controllable urban mills, and gave capital a spatial mobility that labor could not match. The choice of energy infrastructure was a choice about social relations (Malm, 2016). The green transition has not disturbed this structural fact. Offshore wind farms in Sámi territory in northern Norway, solar installations across the Saharan margins of Morocco, geothermal projects in the Kenyan Rift Valley — these are organized through legal frameworks that derive from colonial land tenure, financed through capital structures that extract yield upward into asset-management portfolios, and sited in territories where communities lack the legal standing to contest expropriation. The energy source changes; the direction of value flow does not.

Different energy regimes do not merely produce different environmental outcomes — they encode different political possibilities. The infrastructural geography of coal made labor strikes in the mines and at the choke points of distribution politically potent in ways that were structurally unavailable in the oil economy, where pipelines and tankers diffused control and diminished labor's leverage. Energy infrastructure is, on this account, a political constitution written in steel, concrete, and territorial law (Mitchell, 2011). The transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy does not produce a political tabula rasa; it reorganizes the geography of control. The new choke points are the processing facilities for battery minerals — a handful of Chinese facilities process more than eighty percent of the world's cobalt, lithium, and rare earths [†] — and the corporate structures that hold long-term offtake agreements across the supply chain. The political constitution is being rewritten, but the authors are the same.

What renders this continuity politically illegible is the green designation itself — and this is what "the green screen" means. The investment in a lithium processing facility or a wind corridor across a reindeer-herding commons does not appear as an extension of extractive capitalism. It appears as a contribution to the Paris Agreement. The narrative of planetary necessity — the framing that makes climate action the most urgent imperative of our historical moment — functions to foreclose the political contestation of green infrastructure in the same gesture that it authorizes its construction. To challenge a lithium mine is to delay the energy transition; to obstruct a wind farm in Sámi territory is to prioritize local interests over the fate of the climate system. The green designation is therefore not merely a marketing claim. It is an epistemological operation: it reorganizes the political legibility of the infrastructure, enrolling extraction within a discourse of necessity that renders challenge not merely inconvenient but irresponsible.

This is not to argue that climate action is not urgent, or that renewable energy infrastructure is indistinguishable from fossil fuel extraction in its consequences. The point is more precise: the urgency of the climate crisis has become the legitimating frame for an extractive expansion that follows the institutional and financial template of the economy that produced the crisis. When the same asset managers, the same legal architectures for territorial appropriation, and the same frameworks for externalizing ecological cost are deployed in the service of "clean" energy, the green designation functions as a screen — in both senses: it displays a particular image of progress while simultaneously obscuring the infrastructural reality behind it. What is made invisible is not the infrastructure itself, which is highly visible in its material impositions on communities and landscapes. What is made invisible is the infrastructure's political character — its status as a site of accumulation, dispossession, and contested sovereignty, rather than a neutral technical apparatus for solving a planetary problem.

What follows works through the mechanisms of this invisibility. Section 2 analyzes infrastructure as an epistemological form — the conditions under which functioning systems produce harm that cannot be registered as harm. Section 3 examines the financial architecture of the green transition: how the asset-management model converts living systems into yield instruments, and what ESG certification conceals in that conversion. Section 4 turns to the new opacity layer produced by platform accountability: the certification industry that performs transparency while foreclosing it. Section 5 reads the green transition's spatial grammar as colonial continuity — not by analogy but through the persistence of the actual legal and financial instruments through which colonial extraction was organized. Sections 6 and 7 develop the double bind and its methodological consequences. Section 8 names the problem that remains.

## 2. Infrastructure as Invisibility Machine

The most consequential feature of infrastructure is not what it does but what it disappears. Susan Leigh Star's foundational account of infrastructure identifies a cluster of properties that together explain this vanishing: infrastructure is embedded in other structures and social arrangements; it is transparent in use, meaning it does not need to be reinvented for each task but is taken as a given background condition; it is learned as part of membership in a community of practice; it embodies accumulated standards and conventions; and, crucially, it becomes visible only upon breakdown (Star, 1999). The list is deceptively clean. What it describes is not merely a set of technical properties but a political condition: the condition under which the arrangements that organize collective life — including arrangements that reproduce harm, dispossession, and inequality — are rendered unremarkable. Infrastructure does not hide. It is simply how things work.

Infrastructure is not just a technical form but a form of governance — a medium through which political arrangements are stabilized, projected, and reproduced (Larkin, 2013). Its "promissory" character, the way it encodes visions of futurity and progress into physical and legal architectures, means that infrastructure carries ideological content that is not experienced as ideology but as material fact. The cable is there because electricity is needed. The road is there because commerce requires movement. The concession agreement is there because mining requires legal certainty. Each element appears as a response to a necessity, not as a choice among alternatives. What this adds to Star's account is the insistence that this invisibility is not incidental. It is how infrastructure works politically. The gap between infrastructure's visible form and its operative political content — between the pipeline and the territorial arrangement it enforces, between the certification mark and the labor conditions it conceals — is not a failure of transparency. It is the mode of operation.

Keller Easterling's concept of "extrastatecraft" sharpens this further. Infrastructure space operates as a form of governance that works beneath and beyond statutory law (Easterling, 2014). Its "active form" — the protocols, standards, and spatial formats that make an infrastructure function — encodes political arrangements without appearing to do so. Free trade zones, port authorities, special economic zones, and, by extension, the concession frameworks that organize mineral extraction share this quality: they reorganize territory, sovereignty, and legal liability without requiring explicit political authorization at each step. They work through the logic of technical necessity rather than political decision. The violence they produce is therefore not illegal, not exceptional, and not visible within the representational systems — legal, journalistic, financial — that are calibrated to detect deviation from norms rather than the operation of norms themselves. The infrastructure is the norm. And norms do not appear as choices.

The battery-mineral economy provides a near-perfect illustration of this dynamic, precisely because it is so new and because its infrastructural architecture is, in many respects, the fossil-fuel infrastructure with different minerals flowing through it. The cobalt refining chain that moves material from artisanal and industrial mines in the Democratic Republic of Congo through processing facilities in China to battery cells in South Korea or Poland is exactly the kind of infrastructure Star and Larkin describe. It works. Cobalt moves from ground to cathode with sufficient reliability that the battery supply chains of major automotive companies have been organized around it. The legal architecture — the combination of DRC mining law, Chinese processing-sector regulation, the London Metal Exchange's spot-price mechanisms, the due-diligence frameworks of the OECD and the Responsible Minerals Initiative — functions as an installed base in Star's sense: it does not need to be invented anew for each transaction. It is the taken-for-granted condition within which transactions occur.

That the conditions of production in the Congolese Copperbelt involve child labor, artisanal miners with no respiratory protection working in tunnels adjacent to industrial operations, and communities exposed to acid drainage from tailings ponds — this is known. What is less often analyzed is why this knowledge does not interrupt the infrastructure's operation. The answer is structural. The due-diligence frameworks that nominally govern the chain are certification systems rather than monitoring systems. They produce documents that certify compliance with standards that were partly designed by the industries they certify. The violence they make invisible is not hidden by secrecy — it is made invisible by the functioning of the system. The cobalt moves, the documentation is produced, the auditors are paid, the batteries are manufactured. Breakdown, in Star's sense, does not occur. And so visibility, in Star's sense, does not occur either.

What can be known within an infrastructure — what questions are askable, what data is collected, what counts as evidence — is determined by the infrastructure's design (Edwards, 2010). The infrastructure of mineral certification was designed to answer a specific question: does this mineral come from a conflict zone as defined by the Dodd-Frank Act's Section 1502? It was not designed to answer questions about long-term aquifer depletion, community health outcomes measured across decades, or the relationship between royalty rates and GDP distribution in mining-dependent economies. The epistemic architecture of the infrastructure determines the horizon of visibility within it. The slow harms — the ones Rob Nixon calls "slow violence," the incremental destructions that unfold across timescales that do not register in news cycles or quarterly financial reporting — are not merely difficult to document within this infrastructure. They are outside the infrastructure's knowledge horizon entirely (Nixon, 2011). The problem is not that evidence is suppressed. It is that the infrastructure was never designed to collect it.

The violence that extraction produces along the cobalt belt, around lithium brine operations, or in nickel laterite mining regions is not primarily the violence of breakdown. It is the violence of function. The infrastructure works, which means the harm it produces does not register as a malfunction requiring diagnosis. It registers as geology (the mine is where the mineral is), as development (the concession brings roads and formal employment), as necessary cost (the energy transition requires these materials). Each of these framings is produced by an infrastructure — legal, financial, epistemic, discursive — that is functioning exactly as designed. The violence is, in this precise sense, infrastructure-shaped: distributed across jurisdictions so that no single actor bears legal responsibility; unfolding slowly enough that no single moment constitutes a legally cognizable harm; certified and documented in ways that produce the appearance of oversight without its substance.

Slow violence is necessary here as a concept but not quite sufficient. The concern is primarily with the temporal politics of legibility: the failure of legal and representational systems to recognize violence that unfolds across years or decades rather than in the sudden, spectacular forms that generate political response (Nixon, 2011). The battery-mineral economy extends this analysis spatially. The jurisdictional distribution of the extraction chain means that the slow violence is also geographically dispersed: the health harm occurs in the DRC, the financial return is booked in Luxembourg, the product is sold in Germany, and the legal framework governing due diligence is negotiated in Brussels. No single jurisdiction contains the full chain. No single legal system is capable of seeing it whole. The infrastructure's spatial architecture is its political architecture: fragmentation of jurisdiction is a mechanism of invisibility, not a side effect of globalization.

The argument this section advances is consequently this: the infrastructure of the battery-mineral economy is not designed to conceal — it is designed to function, and concealment is the effect of its functioning. Render it visible, and what becomes legible is not a deviation from the system but the system itself. That is why the visibility problem is not primarily a documentation problem. It is a problem about what counts as legible evidence of harm within systems whose purpose is to keep harm below the threshold at which it becomes politically actionable. The financial architecture through which these systems are organized makes this threshold-setting an interest as much as a design choice — which is what Section 3 examines.

## 3. The Asset-ification of Nature

If the green screen operates by shifting the political legibility of extractive infrastructure, what holds that screen in place institutionally is a financial architecture that has converted the living systems from which battery minerals are extracted into investment assets generating predictable yields. This conversion — the transformation of a lithium deposit, a nickel-bearing laterite, a cobalt seam into a balance-sheet item, an offtake agreement, a securitized revenue stream — is the operational heart of what this paper calls the infrastructure of violence. It is not experienced as violence because its accounting categories have no field for harm. The depletion of an aquifer appears as impairment of an asset. The displacement of an indigenous community appears as a development cost, or does not appear at all. The accounting system is not ideologically neutral; it was designed to produce this particular invisibility.

The dominant organizational form of the green transition is not the public utility, the cooperative, or the community energy project — it is the asset-management fund, holding long-term contracted revenue streams from electricity offtake agreements, mining royalties, and infrastructure leases (Christophers, 2023). What distinguishes rentier capitalism from productive capitalism is that value is extracted from ownership rather than production — the landlord, the toll-road operator, the platform monopolist, and now the infrastructure asset manager generate returns from the fact of possession, not from the act of making (Christophers, 2021). Clean energy infrastructure is peculiarly well-suited to rentier logic: once built, a wind farm or a lithium mine generates revenue streams that can be securitized, tranched, sold, and re-sold through the financial system. The mine does not need to be productive in any socially meaningful sense; it needs to generate yield. This distinction matters because it clarifies why ESG certification and responsible-sourcing frameworks have so consistently failed to alter the material conditions of extraction: they were designed to satisfy the requirements of the asset-management system, not to govern the mine.

The ESG certification apparatus — CDP climate scores, IRMA audits for mining operations, Responsible Minerals Initiative chain-of-custody certifications, the proliferating architecture of voluntary disclosure — deserves particular analytical attention because it has successfully occupied the space where accountability mechanisms should be while performing almost none of accountability's functions. The mechanisms of this failure are specific and worth naming. Corporate baselines for emissions and community impact are generated by the companies themselves, using methodologies they select and metrics they calibrate. Third-party auditors are contracted and paid by the companies they assess, creating a conflict of interest that is structurally identical to the one that produced fraudulent credit ratings in the 2008 financial crisis, and that produces similarly predictable results: ratings that satisfy disclosure requirements while bearing diminishing relationship to material conditions on the ground. The disclosure thresholds and reporting standards are calibrated for securities regulators — for the informational requirements of financial markets — rather than for the communities whose territories are being mined. A community in the Katanga province whose water supply has been contaminated by cobalt tailings has no standing in the CDP rating system, no mechanism for disputing the corporate baseline, no appeal procedure for the IRMA certification that stamped the mine's social performance as "advancing." The architecture produces legibility within the asset-management system while systematically excluding from legibility the claims of those whose lives constitute the externalities.

This is the market economy's foundational violence transposed into the register of green finance: the treatment of land, labor, and money as "fictitious commodities" — things that behave, within the market, as if they were produced for sale, but that were not in fact produced at all and whose commodification therefore destroys the social fabric of which they are constitutive (Polanyi, 1944). Land is the substrate of community, of food production, of ecological relationship; when it is treated as an investment asset, what is destroyed is not merely an economic arrangement but a set of social and ecological relations that market accounting cannot register. What capitalist accounting calls "free gifts of nature" are not free — they have been produced through millennia of ecological processes and, frequently, through the active land-management practices of communities who are dispossessed in the moment of their incorporation into the commodity frontier (Moore, 2015). The appropriation of cheap nature is always also the appropriation of what Ariel Salleh calls metabolic value — the accumulated relational labor, ecological knowledge, and social reproduction that communities invest in their territories and that disappears from the balance sheet at the moment of expropriation (Salleh, 2017).

Accumulation by dispossession — David Harvey's term for the process through which contemporary capitalism generates accumulation not primarily through expanded production but through the conversion of commons into private assets — provides the political-economic framework for understanding what ESG certification specifically obscures (Harvey, 2003). The green transition is a paradigm case: mineral deposits that were, in many jurisdictions, legally or customarily held in common, or whose exploitation was governed by community consent, are being converted through legal reform, treaty override, and administrative reclassification into privately held assets from which communities derive no direct benefit and over which they exercise no meaningful governance. In the Democratic Republic of Congo, the 2018 Mining Code revision extended state ownership claims while simultaneously facilitating long-term corporate concessions; in Indonesia's Sulawesi nickel belt, Special Economic Zone legislation has suspended customary land rights for indigenous communities while enabling the construction of the world's largest nickel processing corridor [†]; in Sámi territories across Norway and Sweden, wind energy development has proceeded over indigenous objections upheld in domestic courts, with corporations and state energy ministries treating legal rulings as obstacles to be managed through appeals rather than as substantive constraints. The prior destruction of territorial and legal frameworks — the delegitimation of indigenous governance, the reclassification of communal land — is not merely preparatory to extraction but constitutive of it (Escobar, 2008). The frontier of the battery-mineral economy has been produced through exactly this prior destruction, and the ESG apparatus ratifies the outcome rather than interrogating the process.

What the asset-management system makes structurally impossible is a question that communities in every extraction zone are asking: what was this territory before it was a deposit? The question is not nostalgic. It is ontological. The lithium brine of the high Andean salares is, within the corporate balance sheet, a reserve — a quantity of extractable commodity measured in tonnes of lithium carbonate equivalent, assigned to a project with a net present value. Within the cosmological and legal frameworks of the communities whose water systems depend on the hydrological equilibrium that brine extraction disrupts, it is something else entirely — something for which financial accounting has no field. Metabolic value names what is being destroyed: not merely value in an extended economic sense but a set of metabolic relations — relations between bodies, soils, waters, and communities — whose destruction cannot be undone by carbon offsets, community benefit agreements, or IRMA certification at the "advancing" level (Salleh, 2017).

The violence of the asset-management infrastructure is therefore not primarily physical, though it frequently becomes so when communities resist. It is ontological: it converts living systems into yield-generating instruments and provides no mechanism — no accounting category, no certification standard, no legal standing — through which the prior forms of life can make claims against their own conversion. The Responsible Minerals Initiative's chain-of-custody framework can tell you with reasonable precision where a cobalt atom was refined and whether the smelter met its disclosure obligations; it cannot tell you what the Congolese artisanal miner lost when the concession was formalized, or what the Sámi herder loses when the migration corridor is crossed by transmission lines. These losses are not incidental to the system. They are the condition of its profitability. The accounting system was not designed to see them, and the ESG apparatus was not designed to change that. It was designed to make the asset legible to the investor — to translate ecological and social complexity into the ratings, scores, and benchmarks through which capital identifies and prices risk. The community that pays the cost of that risk translation has no seat at the table where the rating is assigned.

The new opacity layer installed over this system — the certification industry proper — merits its own analysis, because it has added a specifically digital dimension to the concealment that the financial architecture already achieves.

## 4. Green Platform Capitalism and the New Opacity Layer

The last decade has produced a new stratum of institutional infrastructure layered over the extractive economy: a sprawling complex of ESG ratings agencies, carbon accounting platforms, supply chain transparency initiatives, and mineral certification schemes. The Responsible Minerals Initiative, the Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance, the Carbon Disclosure Project, the Science Based Targets initiative — these organizations present themselves as the accountability infrastructure of a cleaned-up capitalism. They are, in structural terms, something else: accountability simulacra. They perform the function of transparency while concentrating the production of knowledge in the hands of the actors being assessed. The opacity architecture of extraction has acquired a digital face, and that face is earnest, well-funded, and thoroughly credentialed.

To understand what is happening here, platform capitalism offers more than a metaphor — it offers a structural diagnosis. The platform, as a business model, extracts value from data by positioning itself as the indispensable intermediary between two parties who cannot find or evaluate each other without it. The genius of the model is that the platform need not produce the service it mediates; it need only make itself necessary to the transaction (Srnicek, 2017). Applied to green accountability, this logic generates a specific and consequential outcome: ESG data providers, supply chain auditors, and carbon accountants extract value from the performance of transparency rather than from transparency itself. The corporation that commissions the audit, defines its scope, selects the certification standard, and funds the body that issues the standard has not been made accountable — it has purchased accountability as a product. The certification is simultaneously a commercial service and a political shield.

Data extraction constitutes a new form of colonial appropriation — not merely by analogy but through a structural homology between the enclosure of land and the enclosure of social life as raw material for value production (Couldry & Mejias, 2019). This concept of data colonialism has typically been applied to personal behavioral data, but the architecture it describes maps directly onto the environmental accountability industry. What the ESG platform extracts is not ore or timber but the social relation of accountability itself — the capacity to demonstrate, certify, and narrate responsible practice. Once that capacity has been commodified and intermediated through a platform logic, the entity being assessed loses the ability to be judged by any standard it did not help construct. The platform owns the metric; the metric produces the score; the score circulates as fact.

Supply chain disclosure requirements in the battery-mineral economy have thus far operated within this logic: disclosure generates data whose formats are determined by corporate reporting standards, which are themselves shaped through consultation processes in which industry representatives have disproportionate weight (Riofrancos, 2024). The result is a disclosure architecture that produces enormous quantities of information while foreclosing the comparisons that would make that information actionable — across time, across sites, across ownership structures. The ESG rating is a financial product whose production logic serves the needs of portfolio managers who require standardized, comparable, commercially-licensed data — not the logic of ecological monitoring (Christophers, 2023). That a financial product is marketed as environmental accountability is not incidental; it is the business model.

The capitalist world-ecology has always required a system of accounting that makes the unpaid work of nature and of reproductive labor invisible — not accidentally but as a structural precondition for the appropriation of that work as "free" input (Moore, 2015). The green accountability complex extends this accounting logic into the era of the energy transition. What it cannot count — what its certification methodologies systematically exclude — is the cumulative metabolic cost of extraction on the subsistence environments, water systems, and territorial relations within which mining takes place. The IRMA standard includes community engagement requirements; it cannot measure what community engagement costs a community whose land base is being reorganized around extraction. The cost appears nowhere on the score sheet.

This is not a critique of the individuals who work within these systems. Many of the researchers, auditors, and advocates inside the certification complex are doing genuine accountability work under severe structural constraints — constrained by the institutional architecture they did not design, the funding relationships they cannot easily refuse, and the legal frameworks that determine what "impact" is measurable and what is not. The critique is structural: the system's architecture produces opacity regardless of the intentions of its participants. The accountability platform is not a failed version of what it claims to be. It is a successful version of something else — a permission infrastructure for extraction, dressed in the idiom of transparency.

What this adds to the broader infrastructure of extractive capitalism is an epistemic capture that is harder to challenge than the older forms. A mining permit can be appealed to a tribunal. An environmental impact assessment can be contested on procedural grounds. The challenge to an IRMA certification score — issued by a third-party body whose methodology was partly designed in consultation with the industry it certifies, whose appeals process runs through that same body, and whose data is proprietary to the companies that commissioned it — has no addressable forum in any legal system currently operating at scale. The data circulates; its circulation is taken as evidence of accountability; the accountability forecloses challenge. The green platform does not resolve the politics of extraction. It archives them.

The spatial implication of this archiving is that communities in extraction zones across the Global South are not merely subject to green platform capitalism's opacity layer. They are also subject to the longer colonial geography through which the green transition's metabolic arrangement has been organized — a geography that the platforms encode without naming.

## 5. Colonial Continuity in Green Clothing

There is a spatial pattern to the green transition that any honest cartography makes immediately visible. The cobalt that stabilizes electric vehicle batteries comes primarily from the Congolese Copperbelt. The nickel that anchors cathode chemistry is processed at scale in Indonesia's Sulawesi corridor. The lithium that powers storage systems is pumped from brine operations across the Andean altiplano. Rare earths critical to wind turbine magnets are mined and processed across Inner Mongolia and Jiangxi province under conditions that generate radioactive tailings whose regulatory oversight is, to put it diplomatically, incomplete [†]. Solar farms are being built at scale across the Sahara and the Sahel to supply European electricity demand. Wind concessions are being issued on Sámi territory in Norway and on Diné land in the American Southwest. The metabolic geography of the green transition replicates, with remarkable fidelity, the metabolic geography of colonial capitalism: extraction occurs in the periphery; value accumulates at the center; the costs are borne by those who did not negotiate the terms.

This is not merely an analogy, and that distinction matters analytically. The legal, territorial, and financial architectures through which the battery-mineral economy operates are not reminiscent of colonial extraction — they are substantially the same architectures, updated for the contemporary legal order. The concession system, the enclave economy, the bilateral investment treaty, the free trade zone, the public-private partnership: these instruments were developed, refined, and juridically entrenched across decades of colonial and postcolonial resource extraction. They do not need to be redesigned for the green transition because they were never dismantled. What changes is the idiom. "Development" becomes "green development." "Resource extraction for metropolitan industry" becomes "energy transition investment." The concession issued for copper mining in the 1960s and the concession issued for lithium brine extraction today are instruments of the same legal genealogy operating in the same territorial logic. The continuity is not incidental; it is the infrastructure.

The same territories organized as extraction peripheries under colonial and postcolonial fossil capitalism are now being organized as solar and wind energy peripheries for European consumption — a pattern the green transition in the Arab world and North Africa makes visible with particular precision (Hamouchene, 2023). The EU's Mediterranean energy policy — the interconnectors, the hydrogen pipelines, the large-scale renewable concessions — replicates the spatial logic of earlier extractive arrangements. The legal instrument is the concession in each case. The political question — who has the practical authority to determine what happens to the land, the water, the wind, the sun — is structured by the same power asymmetries in each case. The addition of a "green" justification does not alter that structure; it provides it with a new legitimating vocabulary that is harder to contest because it speaks in the language of global survival.

The colonial spatial partition — organized by the same administrative system that presents itself as neutral — offers a structural grammar for reading the green transition's geography (Fanon, 1961/2004). The partition is not simply about racial segregation; it is about who bears the costs of a system of value production organized around their exclusion from its benefits. The green energy zone constructed on Sahrawi territory, the solar concession built over Tuareg pastoral land, the battery-mineral mine opened on indigenous territory after a FPIC process conducted on a timeline controlled by the project's proponents: these are not aberrations from a green transition that is otherwise operating equitably. They are expressions of the partition logic that the transition inherited and has not yet had reason to abandon.

Recognition frameworks — the consultation requirement, the impact and benefit agreement, the community benefit fund, the FPIC protocol — can function as instruments of continued dispossession when what they extend is recognition of cultural identity rather than practical territorial sovereignty (Coulthard, 2014). The practical capacity to refuse extraction — to say no and have that no be binding — is not what recognition frameworks in their current form deliver. They deliver consultation. They deliver a process. They deliver documentation that the community was heard. What they do not deliver, in the vast majority of cases where extraction proceeds, is the territorial authority to determine whether extraction proceeds at all. The green transition has not generated new legal architectures for indigenous territorial sovereignty; it has generated new rhetorical contexts in which the old architectures operate.

The green transition, as currently constituted, reproduces rather than restructures the unequal exchange that has characterized the metabolic relationship between the Global North and the Global South since the colonial period (Ajl, 2021). The energy transition requires enormous mineral inputs; those inputs are extracted at low cost in the periphery; the value added in processing, manufacturing, and deployment accrues at the center; the ecological liabilities remain at the extraction site. This is not a description of how capitalism sometimes operates. It is a description of how the world-system's metabolic geography has been organized for centuries, and the green transition has given it new life without altering its terms of trade.

Metabolic value names what the accounting system cannot see: the reproductive labor and ecological relations that sustain subsistence environments — the water management practices, the grazing patterns, the seed-saving knowledge, the ritual economies of land use — are not merely lost when extraction reorganizes a territory. They are appropriated: their prior work is what made the territory productive, inhabitable, and legible in the first place, and the extraction economy captures that accumulated value while attributing zero cost to its destruction (Salleh, 1997/2017). This converges with the logic of cheap nature and with the argument that the prior destruction of territorial frameworks is a precondition for extractive development (Moore, 2015; Escobar, 2008). The mine does not arrive in an empty landscape. It arrives in a territory whose metabolism has been sustained over generations by labor and knowledge that the accounting system cannot see — and the green certification schemes that now overlay the extraction economy are no more capable of measuring that metabolic debt than the colonial cadastral surveys that preceded them.

The encounter between global capital and local place cannot be reduced to a clean topology of center and periphery: value is produced at the frontier through friction, and that friction insists that the peripheral zones of extraction are not inert surfaces onto which metropolitan capital writes its logic, but places inhabited by actors with their own political intelligence, strategic capacity, and territorial knowledge (Tsing, 2005). The asymmetry is structural, not natural. It was built, juridically and spatially, and it can be mapped. That mapping is the precondition for any politics adequate to the situation. Coloniality is not a historical period that ended with formal decolonization but an ongoing structure of power — a matrix that organizes knowledge, authority, and economy in ways that persist long after the administrative form of colonial governance has been withdrawn (Mignolo & Walsh, 2018). The green transition demonstrates this with particular clarity: it has arrived with new institutions, new vocabularies, and new legitimating frameworks, but the underlying territorial arrangements — who holds the concession, who bears the liability, who captures the value — reproduce the colonial matrix with a fidelity that formal institutional analysis consistently underestimates.

The green screen, on this reading, is not a false image placed over an otherwise visible reality. It is itself a form of infrastructure — built from legal instruments, certification standards, corporate disclosure requirements, and climate urgency rhetoric — whose function is to make the colonial metabolism of the energy transition appear as its opposite. The double bind through which this appearance is maintained is the subject of the section that follows.

## 6. The Double Bind of Visibility

There is a trap built into the political logic of the green energy transition, and it is precise enough to deserve a precise name. It is not a paradox, which implies two equally weighted possibilities held in tension. It is a double bind: a structure in which the conditions that would produce visibility for one kind of harm are generated by — and therefore serve to suppress — a second kind of harm. Climate breakdown is real, urgent, and the dominant frame within which the energy transition is politically justified. Local ecological breakdown — the aquifer drawn down beyond recharge, the river metal-loaded from tailings drainage, the community whose water supply is no longer reliable — is also real. The green framing does not synthesize these two breakdowns. It subordinates one to the other. And the terms of that subordination are not accidental. They reproduce, at a planetary scale, the same distributional logic that has organized extraction zones since the colonial period: the costs land where political power is weakest; the benefits accumulate where political power is concentrated.

Star's foundational observation — that infrastructure becomes visible upon breakdown — carries an implicit political geography that she did not fully develop. The breakdown that triggers visibility is a breakdown legible to the infrastructure's users, its beneficiaries, the communities it serves. When the power goes out in Berlin or London, the electrical grid becomes visible. When the cobalt supply chain produces cardiomyopathy in Kolwezi, the infrastructure does not become visible in Berlin or London — because the breakdown is not experienced there. It is experienced by communities that are not the infrastructure's intended beneficiaries; they are its resource base. Star's insight, transplanted into the context of transnational extraction, reveals something she did not theorize directly: visibility upon breakdown is a privilege that accrues to those whom the infrastructure serves. For communities that are the infrastructure's raw material, breakdown may generate visibility only within a local political context that has been, by design, stripped of the capacity to convert local knowledge into globally legible claims.

The green transition has added a specific mechanism to this general structure. When communities in the high Andean salares contest the expansion of lithium brine extraction on the grounds that it is depleting the altitude aquifer systems on which their territories depend, they are not merely contesting a mining operation. They are contesting an operation whose legal legitimacy, financial backing, and political cover derive substantially from its position in the battery supply chain — and therefore from its contribution to the prevention of climate breakdown. The objection to the mine becomes, within this logic, an objection to the battery. And the objection to the battery becomes an objection to the electric vehicle. And the objection to the electric vehicle becomes an objection to decarbonization. The chain of equivalences is not constructed cynically by individual bad actors, although it is certainly deployed cynically in specific political contests. It is a structural feature of how the two systems — the infrastructure of extraction and the infrastructure of climate governance — have been articulated together (Riofrancos, 2024; Hamouchene, 2023). The articulation produces political immunity for extraction. Challenge to the mine is not refuted. It is rendered illegitimate by the framing within which it must be made.

The same dynamic operates in Moroccan solar development, where communities whose land and water access is affected by concentrated solar power installations are positioned, within the EU's Mediterranean energy partnership framing, as obstacles to European decarbonization; in the Norwegian offshore wind corridors, where Sámi reindeer herding communities whose courts upheld their rights nonetheless find that the wind farms continue to operate while litigation continues; in the Kenyan Rift Valley geothermal projects, where local communities contesting displacement face the combined authority of the state, the international development finance institutions backing the project, and the climate narrative enrolling it as green investment. Energy infrastructure shapes political possibility — what kinds of collective action are structurally enabled or disabled by the infrastructure's technical form (Mitchell, 2011). The battery-mineral economy's globally distributed, jurisdictionally fragmented, climate-legitimated architecture forecloses political challenge in ways that are not reducible to the intentions of any actor within it. The foreclosure is architectural.

Green capitalism represents the capture of the climate movement by the same extractive interests the movement was ostensibly challenging — market mechanisms and corporate frameworks deployed to address climate change reproduce the structural features of the system generating climate change, while providing those structures with a new form of legitimacy (Klein, 2014). A decade on, the battery-mineral economy makes this argument concrete. The specific mechanism is not simply that corporations have co-opted green language. It is that the infrastructure of green extraction has been built, in significant part, by the same institutional actors, using the same legal templates and financial architectures, as the fossil-fuel infrastructure it nominally replaces. The same law firms that drafted production-sharing agreements for deepwater oil concessions now draft battery-mineral concession agreements. The same asset managers that financialized oil-field royalties now financialize lithium royalty streams. The installed base, in Star's sense, is the same installed base.

The second level of the double bind is epistemic rather than political, and it is the level at which the structural suppression of local knowledge operates most durably. Regulatory authorities in resource-rich states frequently hold monitoring data at spatial and temporal resolutions insufficient for independent assessment of extraction impacts. Certification systems surrounding mineral extraction were developed in close consultation with the mining companies and electronics manufacturers whose supply chains they govern. The conflict of interest is structural, not incidental (Sovacool et al., 2020 [†]). The result is that the threshold at which harm becomes legible within these systems is set at a level that the harm, by design, does not reach.

This is the concept Eyal Weizman develops under the term "threshold of detectability." The threshold is not a natural feature of the harm but is politically set and technically maintained — states and corporations produce harm in ways calibrated to remain below the threshold at which evidence achieves legal or political recognition (Weizman, 2017). Legal systems require proof of causation at standards that distributed, slow harm cannot meet. Journalistic systems require events legible as events. Financial disclosure systems require material information in a specific technical sense that excludes most forms of ecological harm. Each system sets its own threshold — and the parties with interests in keeping harm invisible have significant institutional power in setting it.

The communities bearing these costs are not ignorant of them. The farmers in Lickanantay communities on the margins of the high-altitude salares know that the springs their ancestors used no longer flow. The women in Kolwezi who draw water from streams running orange know that something has changed. The Sámi reindeer herder knows which migration corridors are now blocked by transmission lines. The Tuareg pastoralist knows which wells have been compromised by geotechnical survey activity. This knowledge is local, embodied, and historically deep. What it lacks is not accuracy. What it lacks is convertibility: the capacity to translate local, embodied knowledge of harm into forms that achieve legibility within legal, financial, or journalistic systems calibrated to a different standard of evidence. This is the politics of representation (Nixon, 2011). But representation here is not just a cultural problem of narrative form. It is a methodological and political problem of evidence: of what instruments, what standards, what institutional platforms are required to make locally legible harm into globally actionable knowledge.

This is where the double bind closes. The breakdown that would render the battery-mineral infrastructure visible is real and, in many extraction zones, already in progress. But it cannot achieve the recognition that Star's framework implies breakdown automatically generates, because the systems designed to recognize breakdown were designed by and for the infrastructure's beneficiaries. And the political challenge that might force those systems to expand their thresholds of recognition is foreclosed by the climate urgency that the same infrastructure invokes as its legitimating frame. The harm is real. The evidence exists. The knowledge is present. What is absent is not documentation but a methodology capable of rendering a functioning infrastructure legible as a site of ongoing harm — capable of making visible not the breakdown of the system but the violence of its successful operation. Building that methodology is the work of the section that follows.

## 7. Toward a Methodology of Functioning Infrastructure

The methodological problem this paper has been building toward is not obvious, and stating it clearly requires a detour. The previous sections have shown that the green energy transition renovates and re-legitimizes the infrastructure of extractive capitalism; that this continuity is doubly concealed; and that the central question is how to render the infrastructure legible as a site of violence when it is functioning normally. The answer that presents itself almost automatically is: build counter-infrastructure at scale, aggregate satellite data, corporate filings, supply chain records, community testimony, beneficial-ownership networks; construct a platform that maps the whole system. This is the answer that the evidence, the problem, and the political stakes appear to demand. It is also the wrong answer, or at minimum one that must be confronted before it can be used.

The aggregation imperative carries its own logic, and the logic is recognizable. It is the same logic that the analysis of platform capitalism identified as a form of opacity in its own right: the belief that visibility is achieved through data at scale, that the accumulation of evidence in one place produces knowledge, that comprehensive mapping is the condition of accountability. This is not a neutral epistemology. It is the epistemology of the extractive infrastructure itself — the belief that the world is a set of resources to be drawn toward a center, processed at that center's resolution, and returned as insight to actors positioned to act on it. The counter-platform that aggregates harm data across the global battery-mineral supply chain is structurally isomorphic to the supply chain it documents: both extract information from peripheral sites, process it at industrial scale, and return value to actors with the institutional capacity to use it. The communities in the Andean altiplano, in the Congo's Copperbelt, in the rare-earth mining regions of Inner Mongolia provide the raw material for both. The platform model of accountability reproduces the relation it is designed to critique.

This is not a counsel of abstention. The argument is more precise, and more demanding. The error is not in aggregation per se but in the assumption that aggregation is the primary problem — that what prevents accountability is the absence of a sufficiently comprehensive dataset. Infrastructure becomes invisible through use: it is visible at the moment of breakdown, when it fails, and recedes from view when it functions (Star, 1999). The infrastructure of the green transition is not invisible because data about it is unavailable. It is invisible because it is functioning. The legal structures work. The permitting systems work. The supply chain certification schemes produce their ratings on schedule. The regulatory approvals flow. The corporate disclosures satisfy the securities commissions that receive them. What makes the infrastructure invisible is not the absence of evidence but the absence of a political and juridical framework capable of receiving the evidence that already exists and making accountability claims from it.

The communities living near lithium extraction operations in the high Andean salares have known for years that the aquifer is declining. They have watched the vegas dry, observed the springs weaken, seen flamingo populations retreat from nesting sites that sustained their territories for generations. Indigenous water guardians carry intergenerational knowledge of pre-extraction hydrological conditions that no satellite sensor can reproduce — a temporal depth of observation that provides calibrated baselines the corporate monitoring systems never established, because establishing them would have made the harm measurable. The knowledge exists. The problem is that knowledge of harm does not become accountability until it can move through institutions capable of recognizing it as such — the lesson Paul Farmer drew from his work on tuberculosis and HIV in Peru and Haiti (Farmer, 2004). The community's knowledge of the drying spring cannot achieve legal legibility in a tribunal system designed to receive corporate-generated, quantitatively-formatted, causally-disaggregated evidence. The harm is known but legally mute — not because the data is absent, but because the evidentiary architecture has been calibrated to exclude this kind of knowing.

This reframing shifts the methodological problem from technical to political and juridical. The methodology needed is not primarily about producing new data at scale. It is about building evidentiary practices that can hold different kinds of knowledge simultaneously — embodied, temporal, relational, community-held knowledge alongside satellite imagery and financial disclosure — and make them jointly legible in accountability forums. Situated knowledge is the condition of accountability rather than its obstacle — the pretense of the view from nowhere, the comprehensive platform that aggregates all evidence without a standpoint, is not epistemically superior to knowledge generated from a particular position and from specific relations of care and consequence (Haraway, 1988). It is epistemically impoverished, because it has evacuated the very specificity that makes harm recognizable as harm rather than as data. The spring that has weakened is a different kind of claim from the aggregate groundwater level for a 200-kilometer basin. Both are necessary. The methodological failure is the assumption that the latter supersedes the former.

Three specific practices follow from this reframing — not as a methodology proposal in the sense of a deliverable system, but as an intellectual and political commitment about what this kind of work requires.

The first is knowledge-moment reconstruction. The infrastructure of violence is not only a structure; it is a sequence of decisions made by specific actors with specific authority at specific moments of awareness. The regulatory official who receives a hydrological assessment identifying aquifer stress and renews the extraction permit without modification makes a decision at a datable moment with documented institutional knowledge. The corporate board that receives environmental audit findings and authorizes continued operations makes decisions. The financial institutions that extend credit lines while receiving sustainability risk reports make decisions. Forensic architecture demonstrates the power of this reconstructive move: the material traces of events can be assembled into cross-examinable temporal accounts that name who knew what, and when, and what they chose to do with that knowledge (Weizman, 2017). Applied to the infrastructure of the green transition, this approach produces a different kind of claim from the aggregate indictment of a system. It produces the claim that these specific actors, in these specific positions, with this specific knowledge, made these specific decisions. This is a claim that courts can hear, that investigative journalism can verify, that UN mechanisms can receive as formal submissions.

The second practice concerns the evidentiary hierarchy that accountability processes currently reproduce. Satellite observation is treated as epistemically superior to intergenerational memory. Corporate-formatted quantitative data is treated as superior to community testimony. These assumptions are not neutral; they are a sediment of the same colonial epistemology that structured the extraction economy (Mignolo & Walsh, 2018). An adequate methodology must refuse these subordinations procedurally, not just rhetorically. This means developing evidentiary practices that can hold a water guardian's decades of observation and a satellite interferometry reading of groundwater subsidence in the same argumentative frame, neither subordinated to the other, each serving as a check on the other's blind spots. What is absent from the record is itself a finding about power — the silences in the archive document the relations that produced them (Trouillot, 1995). The absence of well-specific extraction data from a corporation's public filings is not a gap to be filled; it is evidence of a disclosure architecture designed to prevent attribution. The absence of pre-extraction hydrological baselines from environmental impact assessments is not an oversight; it is a designed condition that forecloses measurement. Mapping the absences with the same analytical rigor applied to the data that is present is itself a form of accountability.

The third practice addresses scale, and it is here that the tension with the aggregation imperative must be resolved rather than dissolved. The problem with counter-platforms is not scale as such, but direction: they move from the infrastructure downward, treating the local harm site as an instance to be fed into the comprehensive map. The methodology that holds promise works in the opposite direction, moving from specific sites outward. It begins from the community's epistemic position — from the drying spring, the weakened pasture, the abandoned migration corridor — and traces outward through the corporate and regulatory documentation that surrounds this specific harm: the permit record, the monitoring data, the environmental assessment, the corporate disclosure, the ownership chain, the financial instruments. Place-based epistemologies are not a refusal of scale; they are a claim about the direction of travel (Escobar, 2008). The territorial knowledge of communities in extraction zones is not a local curiosity to be aggregated into a global picture. It is the irreducible political claim from which accountability must move outward.

What this approach cannot claim is comprehensiveness. It cannot map the entire battery-mineral supply chain simultaneously, cannot produce the planetary overview that the problem's scale appears to demand. This limitation is real and must be named rather than minimized. The infrastructure operates at planetary scale; accountability mechanisms capable of reaching it operate, at best, at national scale. This gap is the paper's unresolved problem, and naming it honestly is the methodological move this section can make without overclaiming. The practices described above — knowledge-moment reconstruction, refusal of the evidentiary hierarchy, site-grounded scale — cannot close that gap on their own. They can, however, produce a form of account that is more likely to be legally receivable, more difficult for the system to absorb as a single-node intervention, and more faithful to the full range of knowledge that exists about these harms. The aggregation platform promises comprehensiveness and delivers a different kind of invisibility — one in which all the data is present and the accountability claim still cannot be made. These practices promise less and require more: they require the intellectual and political commitment to hold different kinds of knowledge without subordinating one to another, to reconstruct decision at the juncture of knowledge rather than condemning the system from its aggregate outputs, and to work from the community's territorial claim outward rather than from the infrastructure downward.

## 8. Conclusion: The Scale Problem

This paper has argued that the green energy transition does not constitute a break from the infrastructure of extractive capitalism. It constitutes that infrastructure's renovation: the same legal architectures, territorial arrangements, and corporate structures that enabled the fossil fuel economy now power the battery-mineral economy, extended and re-legitimized by the moral emergency of the climate crisis. The permission structure has not been dismantled; it has been given a new cover story, and the cover story is more powerful than any its predecessor enjoyed, because it carries the weight of planetary necessity. The double concealment this paper identified — the functional invisibility that infrastructure always carries combined with the green legitimacy that forecloses political challenge — is not a rhetorical trick. It is a structural achievement. The infrastructure is designed to look like a solution to the problem that it perpetuates.

The scale problem is the problem that remains, and it deserves to be named with full precision rather than dissolved into optimism about methodology or accountability. The infrastructure of the green transition operates at planetary scale: the supply chains run from the Andean altiplano to Antwerp, from the Congo to Cologne, from the rare-earth mines of Inner Mongolia to the semiconductor fabrication plants of Taiwan and the assembly lines of Central Europe. The value flows across these chains without meaningful friction; the harm accumulates at extraction sites and dispersed communities without meaningful recognition. Accountability mechanisms, by contrast, operate at national scale at best, and more commonly at the scale of a single regulatory decision, a single permit, a single corporate entity. International human rights frameworks exist but are advisory. Climate litigation is proliferating but is addressed to states rather than to the corporate networks that make the infrastructure function. The bilateral investment treaties that govern the relationships between corporate investors and resource-rich states frequently prohibit precisely the regulatory action that would interrupt extraction — and the arbitration panels that adjudicate these disputes are staffed by the same legal profession that designs the investment structures being contested. The gap between the scale of the harm and the scale of the accountability mechanisms is not an implementation failure. It is maintained by the same actors who benefit from it.

Single-site interventions matter. The community that blocks an access road, the lawyer who files the tribunal claim, the investigative journalist who traces the beneficial ownership chain: these acts have real consequences and deserve full solidarity. The caution the paper registers is about category error, not about futility. A permit cancellation changes the legal fact of one extraction operation. It does not change the permit system that produced the decision. A litigation victory naming a corporation liable for a specific harm does not change the corporate architecture designed to absorb and contain liability while passing costs to subsidiaries. A documentary investigation that exposes one supply chain link does not change the disclosure architecture designed to ensure that no comprehensive view is ever legally required. The infrastructure absorbs single-register challenges because it was designed to absorb them.

The question this paper has been building toward cannot be answered here. It is this: how do we make a functioning system legible as a site of violence? The visibility problem in the green transition is not the visibility problem of slow violence, where gradual harm defeats the temporal architecture of legal and journalistic recognition. It is harder than that. The infrastructure is fast, efficient, and institutionally celebrated. Its outputs are the batteries that store renewable energy. Its legal architecture is the framework of development law that the world's most progressive financial institutions use to fund it. Its corporate actors appear at climate summits and sign the Paris commitments. What is required — the intellectual work, the juridical imagination, the political commitment to hold the community's knowledge of the drying spring and the corporate board's knowledge of the audit finding in the same frame of accountability — is not a methodology in the sense of a tool to be deployed. It is a reorientation of what accountability for the climate transition is for. The crisis is real. The urgency is real. The infrastructure that the urgency is being used to protect is also real. Making that legible is not a research task with a completion date. It is the problem that those who work at the intersection of ecology, capital, and accountability have inherited, and that the green transition has made simultaneously more urgent and harder than it has ever been before.
