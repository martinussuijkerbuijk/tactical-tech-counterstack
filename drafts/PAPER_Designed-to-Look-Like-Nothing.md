## Abstract

Environmental violence rarely announces itself. The explosion is the exception. Most harm to landscapes, ecologies, and communities accumulates through processes so gradual, so distributed across time and jurisdiction, so thoroughly normalized by the institutional arrangements that permit them, that causation becomes nearly impossible to attribute and accountability nearly impossible to reach. This paper proposes an **ecology of violence** framework — a conceptual architecture for analyzing four registers of environmental harm (fast, slow, structural, and extractive) as co-constitutive elements of a single system. Drawing on and extending foundational work in slow violence theory (Nixon, 2011), structural violence scholarship (Galtung, 1969; Farmer, 2004), political economy of extraction (Harvey, 2003; Moore, 2015), and the critical geography of uneven exposure (Pulido, 2000; Bullard, 1990; Coulthard, 2014), we argue that the four registers are not parallel phenomena but mutually sustaining ones: each reproduces the conditions for the others. The ecology of violence is legible only when mapped as a system. And it is addressable — legally, politically, epistemically — only at the scale and complexity at which it actually operates.

## 1. The Visibility Problem

A refinery explosion makes the news. Police arrest an activist at an encampment and the footage travels. A facility catches fire and there are photographs, timestamps, a crime scene. This is what we have learned to call violence: sudden, localized, legible, generating the kind of evidence that legal systems were built to receive.

Most environmental harm works the other way. An aquifer depleted over twenty years. Soil contamination accumulating across generations. An ecosystem collapsing incrementally as extraction intensifies — each permit renewal legal, each inspection passed, the harm distributed so widely across time and space that no single act causes it and no single perpetrator owns it. Rob Nixon gave this phenomenon its canonical name: slow violence — "a violence that occurs gradually and out of sight, a violence of delayed destruction that is dispersed across time and space, an attritional violence that is typically not viewed as violence at all" (Nixon, 2011, p. 2). What Nixon named has proven enormously generative. It has sharpened the theoretical vocabulary of environmental humanities, reoriented critical geography toward temporal as well as spatial analysis, and helped communities articulate what they already knew: that the absence of an event is not the absence of harm.

Slow violence, even in Nixon's hands, tends to appear in contrast to fast violence — as if the two occupied separate registers, addressed to different readers, analyzable through different methods. A community facing aquifer depletion and a community facing a police raid are suffering differently, but they are often suffering at the hands of the same system, through mechanisms that depend on each other. This is where the existing literature has not fully gone. The problem with slow violence goes beyond its being slow. It is structured to remain invisible — and the structure is the point.

This paper argues that fast violence, slow violence, structural violence, and what we call extractive violence are not parallel phenomena but co-constitutive ones. They form a system — an ecology of violence — in which each register sustains and enables the others, and the whole is qualitatively different from the sum of its parts. Addressing this system requires a framework capable of holding all four registers simultaneously, mapping the dependencies between them, and asking not just what happened but how the conditions were arranged so that it could.

## 2. Four Registers and Their Genealogies

Before arguing for the ecology, it is worth respecting the intellectual work that produced each register.

**Fast violence** has always been the legible variety. It produces crime scenes, timestamps, witnesses, evidence that courts can receive. The literature on environmental enforcement tends to concentrate here: the raid on the illegal dumping operation, the arrest of the land defender, the documented incident of police brutality at a frontline protest. The documentation matters enormously — there is no dismissing its necessity. But as scholars from Agamben (1998) to Mbembe (2003) have observed, the visibility of fast violence performs an important ideological function: it constructs violence as exceptional, as rupture, as something that stands out against a background of normality. That background — the aquifer depleting, the contamination accumulating, the permit system that allows both — recedes precisely as fast violence draws the attention forward. The visible event naturalizes the invisible process.

**Slow violence** is Nixon's contribution, and the name cannot be improved on. His insistence that slow violence is "typically not viewed as violence at all" is both descriptive and diagnostic: the failure to see it is built into the temporal and spatial architecture of the legal and journalistic systems that would otherwise register it. The harm is too dispersed, too gradual, too legally authorized to trigger the recognition that the term *violence* requires. Nixon's particular focus on the Global South — on communities in the sacrifice zones of extraction, in the dumping grounds of wealthy nations' toxic production — locates slow violence geographically as well as temporally. It accumulates, as Bullard (1990) showed of American environmental racism decades earlier, in places and on bodies already positioned to absorb it.

**Structural violence** carries a longer genealogy. Johan Galtung's foundational formulation identified violence not only in acts but in conditions: "the cause of the difference between the potential and the actual" (Galtung, 1969, p. 168) — the preventable deaths, the blocked opportunities, the life foreclosed by social arrangement rather than by any single perpetrator's hand. Paul Farmer extended this into medical anthropology and global health, arguing that the structural conditions of poverty, racism, and colonial history produce mortality as surely as any weapon (Farmer, 2004). Applied to environmental harm, structural violence describes the legal and institutional arrangements that make slow harm possible: liability caps that pre-authorize damage at scale, environmental impact assessments written by consultants paid by the companies they assess, regulatory agencies staffed by former executives who return to industry when their public term expires. Each of these is a designed arrangement, not an oversight. The architecture is the point.

**Extractive violence** is the register that ties the others together — and the one that has been most scattered across disciplinary literatures without being named as a form of violence in its own right. David Harvey's concept of "accumulation by dispossession" (Harvey, 2003) identifies the mechanism: capital's requirement for ongoing primitive accumulation — the forcible conversion of common resources, social goods, and subsistence environments into private assets from which profit flows. Jason Moore's work situates this within the longue durée of capitalism's relationship to what he calls "cheap nature" — the systematic undervaluation of ecological systems and human labor in the periphery as a condition of profitability at the center (Moore, 2015). Arturo Escobar's analysis of territorial rights in Latin America shows how extraction requires not just the physical presence of mining or agricultural operations but the prior destruction of the legal and social frameworks through which communities exercised territorial sovereignty (Escobar, 2008). In this framework, extractive violence names the economic logic that makes all the other registers necessary.

Each of these four registers has its own genealogy, its own intellectual home, its own methods, its own political communities. Slow violence lives in environmental humanities and postcolonial studies. Structural violence lives in sociology, medical anthropology, and peace studies. Extractive violence lives in political economy, critical geography, and indigenous studies. Fast violence lives in journalism, law, and human rights documentation. These disciplinary silos are not coincidental — they reproduce the fragmentation of the very system they are each studying.

## 3. The Ecology Argument: On Co-constitutiveness

The term *ecology* is doing specific theoretical work here, and it is worth being precise about what that work is.

An ecological system is characterized by relationships that are both mutually sustaining and mutually regulating. Remove a keystone species and the system reorganizes — losing one element while reorganizing the dependencies of everything connected to it. Introduce a new pressure and the system responds systemically, not locally. The defining feature of an ecology is that the relationships between parts are constitutive of what the parts are, not merely descriptive of how they happen to interact.

This is the claim being made about the four registers of environmental violence. They are not separate phenomena that happen to coexist, or modalities that can be addressed in sequence, or categories across which cases can be sorted. The registers reproduce each other. Extractive logic requires structural cover. Structural cover requires slow harm to remain invisible and legally authorized. Slow harm requires selective fast enforcement to suppress the resistance that would otherwise interrupt it. And fast enforcement generates the spectacle of legibility — the visible, documentable event — that normalizes everything below it, restoring the appearance of a law-governed environment in which slow accumulation can continue unchallenged.

Consider what this means for a community contesting a mining permit. The challenge — mounted through a legal system — occurs within the structural violence register, where the terms of legal standing, the evidentiary standards, the admissibility rules, the jurisdictional boundaries, and the timelines have all been arranged in ways that systematically favor the extractive operation. When the challenge gains momentum, fast enforcement may arrive to protect the mine's infrastructure — arrests, injunctions, the criminalization of protest. Far from departing from the structural register, fast enforcement is its enforcement arm. Meanwhile, the harm to the water table, the soil, the community's health baseline continues to accumulate in the slow violence register, dispersed and legally authorized, each individual reading within permitted limits, the aggregate harm distributed across decades and bodies. The profit flows to the beneficial owner — a corporation registered across several jurisdictions — through the extractive register, where the legal architecture has been designed to keep value accumulation and liability exposure as far apart as possible.

No single intervention can disrupt this loop. A legal victory that cancels one permit changes the facts of one case without changing the permit system that produced the decision. An investigative report that exposes one subsidiary does not touch the beneficial ownership architecture that makes opaque ownership possible. A community that documents one contamination event does not produce the pattern evidence that indicts the sector. This is a diagnosis rather than a counsel of despair. The ecology is designed to absorb single-register challenges. It is disrupted, if at all, only when the interdependencies are made visible and addressed simultaneously.

There is a particular clarity to seeing this through the lens of the Atacama. The 1981 Water Code, enacted under Pinochet, privatized water rights in Chile and severed water from land — a foundational act of structural violence that created the legal conditions for extraction decades before the lithium economy emerged to exploit them. When SQM acquired lithium and potassium concessions in the Salar de Atacama in the 1990s, they were doing something legal within a permission structure that had been designed to make it legal. The aquifer depletion that followed is slow violence — real, measurable, documented in satellite imagery of brine pool expansion and in flamingo nesting surveys showing population decline correlated with water table drops. When Atacameño communities began to organize and file water rights claims with the regulatory authority, those claims were dismissed or indefinitely deferred — structural violence maintaining the conditions for extractive violence. When the 2019 uprising brought protests to the Atacama, the militarized response was fast violence — legible, photographed, internationally reported. And through all of it, the profit flowed: from brine pool to battery grade lithium carbonate, to the supply chains of Apple and Tesla and Volkswagen, in the extractive register that is the system's reason for existing.

Each register is doing real work. Together, they are one system.

## 4. The Design Argument: Opacity as Strategy

There is a tendency, even in critical scholarship, to describe the structural conditions that enable ecological harm as failures: regulatory failures, legal failures, failures of political will. This framing carries its own assumptions about what the structures were originally meant to serve. Nancy Peluso and Michael Watts, in *Violent Environments* (2001), pushed back against this by showing that violence in resource-extraction contexts is a constituent feature of market and legal norms rather than a deviation from them. The violence is in the arrangement, not merely around it.

What the ecology of violence framework adds is a sharper account of how opacity works across registers. Liability caps do not merely limit damages after the fact; they function prospectively to pre-authorize harm at scale, telling corporations in advance how much damage they are permitted to cause before the financial calculus shifts. Jurisdictional fragmentation — the structuring of corporate operations across multiple legal territories — serves as a deliberate strategy for ensuring that no single court can ever see the full picture of the ecology. The insertion of subsidiary corporations between the harm site and the beneficial owner, the rotation of executives between regulatory positions and industry boards, the commissioning of environmental assessments by firms financially dependent on the companies they assess — these are the harm's infrastructure. Removing any one of them would change the economics of extraction. Their persistence is actively maintained.

Saskia Sassen's concept of expulsions — the systemic displacement of populations, economies, and ecologies from the circuits of value that profit from their displacement — points toward the same conclusion through a different analytical route (Sassen, 2014). The expelled are the condition of possibility for that economy's profitability. Their expulsion is the value. What makes this so difficult to see — and what makes the ecology of violence framework necessary — is that the system has been designed so that no single act or actor is responsible for the aggregate outcome. As Galtung noted, structural violence does not require a subject who intends harm, only a structure that produces it (Galtung, 1969). The ecology extends this: the structure is arranged precisely to produce harm without attributable subjects.

Here lies the mechanism of deniability that threads through all four registers. Fast violence has subjects and events — it remains legible as violence even when contested. But its very visibility performs a function: it makes the ecology as a whole appear to be a law-governed system with occasional violations, rather than a violence-producing system with occasional legibility. Slow violence is designed to look like nature: aquifer depletion presents as drought, soil contamination as geological variation, ecosystem collapse as natural succession. Structural violence looks like policy: a liability cap appears as a risk management framework, a regulatory appointment as technical expertise. Extractive violence looks like economics: profit extraction appears as return on investment, dispossession as development.

The ecology's most powerful feature is not any single register but the way the registers work together to produce a system that appears, from within the conventional frameworks through which violence is recognized, to contain almost no violence at all.

## 5. The Politics of Temporal Scale

Among the most important implications of the ecology of violence framework is temporal. Each register operates at a different temporal scale, and the misalignment of these scales functions as a mechanism of accountability avoidance in its own right.

Fast violence is temporally legible because it produces events: discrete, dateable, locatable occurrences that match the time-signatures of legal and journalistic recognition. Law, in its most basic architecture, is built around events — an act, a harm, a moment of causation. Even tort law, which extended legal recognition to patterns of harm, tends to anchor accountability to identifiable moments of decision or knowledge. Journalism is built around events in an even more fundamental sense: the news cycle is the temporal unit of legibility.

Slow violence defeats both these temporal architectures. A twenty-year aquifer depletion has no event. Each month's extraction falls within permitted limits. The harm is only visible at the scale of the full arc — by which time, as the structural register has ensured, liability windows have often closed, relevant subsidiaries have been restructured, and the human bodies that bore the harm are already dead or dispersed.

Structural violence operates at an even longer temporal scale: the Water Code that privatizes water rights, the bilateral investment treaty that constrains environmental regulation, the liability cap inserted into legislation — these may have been enacted decades before the harm they enable. The actors who designed the structure may not be the actors who profit from it. The chain of causation runs across generations of decision, which makes legal attribution genuinely difficult as well as legally inconvenient.

Extractive violence, meanwhile, is the temporal constant: the reason the system was built and the reason it endures. It does not produce events, only conditions and flows — value moving from a territory to an owner through a corporate architecture designed to make that movement legally unattributable.

The ecology of violence framework argues that accountability requires holding all four temporal scales simultaneously. A court that can only recognize the event — the permit decision, the incident, the arrest — cannot see the ecology. A legal theory capable of attributing harm only to an act cannot reach the actors who designed the structure within which the acts occur. Eyal Weizman's forensic architecture methodology offers a compelling approach to this at the scale of individual cases: the reconstruction of material traces into cross-examinable temporal accounts of what happened and when (Weizman, 2017). The ecology of violence framework extends this ambition: accountability for systemic ecological harm requires reconstructing not a single event but the full temporal arc across all four registers, assembled at the scale at which the harm actually occurred.

The temporal misalignment between the speed of harm and the speed of accountability is not merely a practical limitation. It is a deliberate feature of the system's design. Any accountability framework that operates within those mismatches will find itself, by design, always arriving too late.

## 6. The Geography of Uneven Exposure

The ecology of violence framework must be spatially situated. The ecology does not operate uniformly across territories — it concentrates, as decades of environmental justice scholarship have demonstrated, in specific places and on specific bodies, the places already positioned by histories of colonialism, racial capitalism, and dispossession to absorb harm.

Pulido's analysis of environmental racism in Southern California showed that the spatial distribution of toxic facilities, pollution exposure, and regulatory non-enforcement follows the lines of racial geography produced by centuries of uneven development (Pulido, 2000). Bullard's documentation of environmental racism in the American South demonstrated that communities of color bear disproportionate toxic burdens not because they happen to live near hazardous facilities but because the facilities were sited in their communities by virtue of their political vulnerability (Bullard, 1990). Glen Coulthard's analysis of indigenous land politics in Canada shows how the structural register of extractive capitalism continues to function through the dispossession of indigenous territorial sovereignty — not in spite of formal recognition frameworks, but through them (Coulthard, 2014). The concession, the settlement, the benefit-sharing agreement: each can function as a mechanism of continued dispossession dressed in the language of recognition.

The ecology of violence framework inherits and extends these insights. The four registers do not operate in geographic abstraction — they operate in specific territories, on specific communities, through specific histories of power. The extractive logic that drives the system identifies territories where the structural conditions are most favorable: where regulatory capacity is weakest, where community political power is most constrained, where historical claims to territorial sovereignty have been most thoroughly erased or delegitimized. The structural register then works to maintain these conditions — maintaining them is part of the work the structural register does. This is not incidental to extraction but constitutive of it.

This geographic argument carries a further implication: the ecology of violence framework must grapple with the fact that the registers are often administered across national and jurisdictional boundaries, while the harm is borne locally. The corporate structure that profits is registered in one jurisdiction. The regulatory authority that issues permits operates in another. The financial instruments that capture value flow through a third. The community that bears the harm lives in a fourth. Jurisdictional fragmentation is simultaneously a feature of contemporary capitalism, a deliberate strategy of accountability evasion, and a reflection of genuine tensions in how sovereign authority is constituted across territory. Anna Tsing's analysis of the friction that arises when global capital encounters local place illuminates the encounters through which this geography of uneven exposure is reproduced and contested (Tsing, 2005). The Atacama is not exceptional: it is a particularly legible instance of a structure that repeats.

Fanon's analysis of the colonial condition in *The Wretched of the Earth* remains indispensable here. The spatial logic of colonialism — the sharp partitioning of territories between those that produce value and those that bear cost — was never simply a historical arrangement. It was an infrastructure, and many of its features have proven more durable than the political arrangements that formally ended colonial rule (Fanon, 1961/2004). The ecology of violence framework suggests that what we are analyzing in contemporary extraction zones is not a new phenomenon but an ongoing one — the same logic of territorial dispossession, the same structural silencing of territorial claims, now operating through corporate architectures and international investment frameworks rather than through direct colonial administration.

## 7. Toward a Framework for Accountability

What does the ecology of violence framework require of accountability efforts? The argument developed in the preceding sections has several direct implications.

The first is that single-register interventions, however successful, are insufficient on their own. A legal victory canceling one permit changes the facts of one case; it leaves untouched the permit system that produced the decision, the corporate architecture that will restructure around the setback, and the slow harm that has already accumulated. Legal action remains indispensable, and every victory matters — the point is that legal action, to reach the ecology rather than a single node in it, requires understanding the interdependencies between registers, and designing its strategy accordingly.

The second implication is epistemic. The ecology is legible only when all four registers can be mapped together, across time and space, at the scale at which they actually operate. The evidence required for accountability differs in kind from the evidence required for incident-based processes. Pattern evidence assembled across decades rather than documentation of a single event. Network analysis reaching from the harm site to the beneficial owner across multiple corporate structures and jurisdictions rather than the corporate entity that happens to hold the permit. Temporal reconstruction identifying knowledge moments — when did the actors with the power to change course first know what was happening, and what did they do with that knowledge? — rather than incident documentation alone. And analysis of structural mechanisms — which legal arrangements enabled which harms? — alongside the harms themselves.

The third implication is infrastructural. Assembling this evidence — across registers, across time, across jurisdictions, at scale — exceeds what any single investigator, legal team, or community can hold. The ecology of violence framework implies a corresponding accountability infrastructure: one capable of gathering and structuring evidence at scale, mapping the relational architecture of complicity, and assembling the temporal arc of harm as a coherent argument across multiple accountability forums. Star's foundational analysis of infrastructure studies reminds us that infrastructure is never neutral — it embeds political commitments about who can see what, who can access what, whose categories of harm are legible (Star, 1999). An accountability infrastructure built on commons logic rather than platform logic — open schemas, community data sovereignty, implementation without gatekeeping — becomes a counter-design to the opacity that the ecology depends on.

The fourth implication is political and perhaps the most consequential. The ecology's design — its systematic arrangement to look like nothing in particular, to distribute responsibility so widely that it belongs to nobody, to operate at temporal and spatial scales that defeat conventional accountability architectures — is itself an argument. Implicitly but powerfully, it argues that there is no systemic violence here, only local incidents, regulatory gaps, natural processes, and economic necessities. The ecology of violence framework answers this argument directly: the system is the violence, and the distribution of responsibility is itself a form of violence. Making the ecology legible is already a political act, one that places the burden of proof back onto those who designed the structure.

This last point connects to a dimension that runs through the entire framework but has not yet been named explicitly: the question of knowledge. Slow harm that accumulates invisibly is not simply undocumented. In most significant cases, it is *known* — known to the regulatory agencies that receive the monitoring reports, known to the corporations that commission the environmental assessments, known to the executives who receive the scientific warnings. The ecology of violence depends not only on the invisibility of slow harm to communities and courts but on the active management of knowledge within the institutional actors who benefit from the harm's continuation. Reconstructing the knowledge moments — when did they first know? what did they do with that knowledge? what did they choose not to do? — transforms the accountability argument from negligence to decision: from nobody's fault to these specific actors, in these specific roles, making these specific choices over this specific period of time.

## 8. Conclusion

The ecology of violence framework is a wager about what analytical work is necessary to address the full scale of environmental harm. Beginning from the observation that existing frameworks — slow violence, structural violence, extractive capitalism, fast enforcement — each illuminate something real but, studied in isolation, capture only part of what is happening, the contribution of this framework lies not in synthesizing these into a new typology but in insisting on the systemic quality of their interdependence.

The ecology is not a metaphor for the environment. It is a claim about the structure of power: that fast violence, slow violence, structural violence, and extractive violence function like an ecosystem — sustaining one another through the relationships between them, reproducing the conditions for their own continuation. This has theoretical consequences for how we study environmental harm, for the genealogies we draw on, for the temporal and geographic scales we hold in view. It has methodological consequences for what counts as evidence, for what patterns we look for, for how we reconstruct causation across decades and jurisdictions. And it has political consequences for what accountability requires, what infrastructures must be built to support it, and what it means to make a system legible that was designed to remain invisible.

Slow violence is not a problem of insufficient attention. Structural violence is not a problem of insufficient reform. Extractive violence is not a problem of insufficient regulation. They form a system. And the system is the problem.
