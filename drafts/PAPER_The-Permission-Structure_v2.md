## Abstract

The aquifer beneath Chile's Salar de Atacama appears on Sociedad Química y Minera's balance sheet as an intangible asset, classified in perpetuity, assessed annually for impairment. This is not a legal metaphor. It is a precise description of how a hydrological system on which Atacameño communities have depended for millennia was ontologically transformed by the 1981 Pinochet Water Code — converted from communal relation into tradable commodity — and has been maintained as such through four decades of extraction. This paper proposes an **infrastructure of violence** framework for analyzing how extractive capitalism constructs and maintains the legal, institutional, and spatial arrangements through which ecological harm is produced, authorized, and rendered invisible. Drawing on and extending work in slow violence theory (Nixon, 2011), structural violence scholarship (Galtung, 1969; Farmer, 2004), and the political economy of extraction (Harvey, 2003; Moore, 2015; Escobar, 2008), we argue that fast violence, slow harm, structural design, and extractive logic are not parallel registers but a hierarchy: extraction is the organizing logic; structural architecture is the infrastructure it builds and maintains; slow harm is the infrastructure's product; and fast violence is its enforcement mechanism when communities resist. The framework's methodological contribution extends Farmer's anthropology of structural violence through multi-modal data integration — satellite evidence, corporate disclosure analysis, community testimony, timeline reconstruction — proposing three accountability principles: absence as evidence, knowledge moments as accountability pivots, and temporal sovereignty in the evidence record. The Salar de Atacama serves throughout as a forensic case, not an illustration.

## 1. The Intangible Asset

In its 2024 annual report to the United States Securities and Exchange Commission, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile — SQM, the dominant lithium extractor in the Atacama desert — classifies water rights as intangible assets on its balance sheet. Those classified as indefinite are not amortized, the filing explains, "because they represent rights granted in perpetuity subject to annual impairment assessment." Water use rights obtained from Chile's Dirección General de Aguas for supply from rivers and wells near SQM's production facilities are recorded, per the 20-F, at acquisition cost.

The language is technical and deliberately ordinary. But read carefully, it describes something remarkable: a corporation has transformed a hydrological system — aquifer, river, spring, the water that runs through a salt flat in the Atacama Desert — into a line item. The water is now an asset with a cost basis, held in perpetuity, vulnerable to impairment. What the filing does not record, because accounting systems have no category for it, is what the water was before. For the Atacameño communities who have lived in the Salar de Atacama basin for millennia, water is not an asset and it is not tradable. It is a relation. It runs through the vegas — the high-altitude wetlands that sustain llama pastoralism and ceremony — and through the springs that determine where communities can live, where their ancestors are buried, and what their territory means. The ontological gap between "intangible asset in perpetuity" and "the water through which a community understands itself" is not a philosophical curiosity. It is the working surface of the infrastructure of violence this paper describes.

The Water Code that produced this transformation was enacted in 1981 by the military dictatorship of General Augusto Pinochet. Decreto Ley 1.122 privatized water rights and severed them from land tenure, creating a system in which water could be held, accumulated, and traded by private actors — including mining corporations — independently of any requirement to use it on adjacent land or in relation to the communities that depended on it. This was not a regulatory accident. It was a design executed by identifiable economists, signed by a general, and calibrated to create the conditions for extractive investment. By the time SQM listed its water rights as indefinitely held intangible assets in 2024, that design had been running for forty-three years, surviving democracy, protest, legal challenge, and partial amendment.

This paper is about that design — and about the kind of analytical framework required to see it clearly. The infrastructure of violence is not a metaphor. It is a claim: that extractive capitalism constructs and maintains, through legal instruments, corporate architectures, regulatory arrangements, and enforcement mechanisms, the specific conditions under which harm can be produced at scale while remaining legally invisible, causally unattributable, and beyond the reach of any single accountability forum. Seeing this clearly requires a framework that refuses the isolation of its components — that holds the 1981 Water Code and the 2019 military deployment, the aquifer depletion and the corporate intangible asset, the environmental tribunal ruling and the SEC disclosure architecture, as elements of one system.

## 2. What "Infrastructure" Does That "Structure" Cannot

The violence literature's most important tool for this kind of analysis is Galtung's structural violence. The concept is worth dwelling on carefully, because the infrastructure of violence framework extends it by pushing against some of its founding assumptions.

Galtung defined violence not only in acts but in conditions: "the cause of the difference between the potential and the actual" (Galtung, 1969, p. 168). A child who dies of a preventable illness in a society with the medical capacity to prevent it dies of structural violence; no individual caused her death, but the structure of the health system produced it as systematically as any weapon would. This is a foundational contribution to peace research and to the critique of poverty, racism, and war — and it remains indispensable. Structural violence names what personal and interpersonal accounts of harm miss: the conditions that produce harm before any individual acts.

But Galtung's formulation carries an assumption that becomes a liability when applied to ecological accountability: structural violence, in his account, has no obvious subject. It is built into the fabric of social relations. Nobody designed it to harm anyone in particular. It persists as a condition, like weather, distributed through institutions whose architects are forgotten or whose original purposes have drifted into forms of harm nobody intended. This is the account that fits global poverty abstractly. It does not fit the Atacama.

The 1981 Water Code had specific authors. The economists who designed it — schooled in Milton Friedman's framework, operating inside a military dictatorship that had eliminated political opposition — knew that they were dismantling communal water relations and concentrating water rights in private hands. The SQM-Corfo agreements that delegated state mineral rights to a publicly traded corporation were negotiated by identifiable actors with documented financial interests: Corfo derives revenue from SQM's sales, producing a structural incentive against restricting extraction that was legible at the point of design. The oversight architecture — DGA monitoring data held at spatial resolutions insufficient for independent audit; environmental impact assessments relying on corporate-generated baseline data; the nuclear energy commission (CCHEN) regulating lithium as a strategic material rather than as a water-intensive extractive activity — was not a series of uncoordinated oversights. It was a calibrated system. Each element has a function.

The term "infrastructure" is doing specific philosophical work here. Infrastructure is built. It requires architects, capital, maintenance, and political protection. It encodes specific decisions about whose needs matter and whose do not. Susan Star, analyzing infrastructures of knowledge, argued that they become invisible through use — that the work they do is concealed by their very functionality, so that they appear as neutral background condition rather than designed arrangement (Star, 1999). The violence infrastructure in the Atacama works in exactly this way: the Water Code is not experienced as violence by its beneficiaries; it is experienced as law, as property, as the natural state of things. The decision not to update it, not to restore communal water rights after the return of democracy in 1990, not to mandate hydrological disclosure at spatial resolutions that would enable independent audit — these are decisions made annually by actors with identifiable interests, experienced by those actors as prudent governance and by affected communities as the permanent foreclosure of their territorial sovereignty.

The shift from "structural violence" to "infrastructure of violence" is a shift from describing conditions to naming designs: from "the structure produces harm" to "the infrastructure was built to enable harm, is maintained because harm benefits its maintainers, and would require a different design to produce a different outcome." This is not a minor theoretical distinction. It changes who is responsible, what accountability requires, and what it would mean to make it otherwise.

## 3. Extraction and the Infrastructure It Requires

Harvey's accumulation by dispossession explains the economic mechanism: capital, to sustain its rates of return, requires ongoing access to new territories and commons to incorporate into circuits of value (Harvey, 2003). Moore extends this into the longue durée: the profitability of the global capitalist economy rests on the systematic undervaluation — what he calls the appropriation of "cheap nature" — of ecological systems, human bodies, and subsistence environments that are treated as free inputs rather than as the products of complex social and ecological relations (Moore, 2015). The lithium from the Salar de Atacama enters SQM's balance sheet at extraction cost. The aquifer depletion, the dried vegas, the foreclosed Atacameño futures: these are externalities in the accounting sense, which is to say they are the costs that the infrastructure has been designed to transfer elsewhere.

What neither Harvey nor Moore fully accounts for is the design work that makes extraction possible in any specific territory. Accumulation by dispossession requires dispossession — and dispossession requires architecture. The 1981 Water Code is not an instance of accumulation by dispossession; it is the infrastructure that makes accumulation by dispossession possible in the Atacama. It did not merely permit extraction; it foreclosed the legal instruments through which territorial sovereignty could be exercised against it. By transforming water from a communal relation into a balance-sheet intangible, it eliminated the legal grammar through which Atacameño communities might have blocked extraction before it began. When SQM acquired water rights in the 1990s, it was not overcoming community resistance; it was operating in a legal territory from which the instruments of resistance had already been removed by the 1981 design.

Escobar's analysis of territorial development in Latin America names this as the erasure of "prior territorial designs" — the dismantling of the legal, cosmological, and practical frameworks through which communities exercised sovereignty over their territories (Escobar, 2008). The 1981 Water Code accomplished this erasure with notable precision. And the 1993 Indigenous Law that accompanied Chile's democratic transition recognized Atacameño cultural identity without restoring hydrological sovereignty — what the technical reconstruction calls "a legal dualism: cultural recognition without hydrological restitution." Recognition without restitution is a structural refinement: it absorbs the language of indigenous rights while leaving the material conditions of extraction intact. Coulthard's analysis of Canadian indigenous politics shows this mechanism operating at continental scale: formal recognition frameworks can function as instruments of continued dispossession rather than as remedies for it, when recognition is extended to cultural identity while territorial sovereignty — the practical capacity to control what happens on and beneath the land — is withheld (Coulthard, 2014).

This is what makes extractive capitalism's infrastructure of violence distinct from a general critique of capitalist accumulation. Harvey and Moore argue, rightly, that capitalism produces harm. The infrastructure of violence framework argues something more specific: that extraction, in any given territory, requires a *particular architecture* of legal arrangements, institutional designs, and enforcement mechanisms — and that this architecture is not an accidental accompaniment to extraction but extraction's operating condition. The Atacama could not be extracted without the Water Code. The Water Code had to be designed, signed, and protected. The protection required the ongoing political subordination of indigenous territorial claims. The subordination required, at moments of resistance, fast enforcement. The design is not the context for extraction. It is extraction's precondition.

## 4. Slow Harm as Structural Product

Rob Nixon named something crucial: gradual, dispersed, legally authorized ecological harm is violence, even when no event marks it and no perpetrator can be identified. The aquifer depleting over twenty years. The flamingo nesting sites abandoned as water tables drop. The vegas drying. Nixon's insistence that slow violence is "typically not viewed as violence at all" (Nixon, 2011, p. 2) was both descriptive and diagnostic — the failure to recognize it is built into the temporal architectures of journalism and law, both of which require events.

The Atacama dossier confirms Nixon's diagnosis and then exceeds it. The aquifer is not merely invisible because journalism and law require events. It is invisible because the disclosure architecture has been calibrated to prevent visibility at the scale at which accountability would be possible. SQM does not publish well-by-well or aquifer-zone-specific extraction rates in its SEC filings. Aggregate figures satisfy securities regulators while preventing independent hydrological modeling of cumulative drawdown attributable to specific pumping locations. The DGA holds monitoring data that is not published at the spatial resolution required for independent audit. The environmental impact assessments that authorized extraction relied on corporate-generated baseline data — and, in several cases, were conducted before comprehensive pre-extraction baselines were established, so there is no calibrated starting point against which current conditions can be assessed. The environmental tribunal hears claims on timelines of two to five years while aquifer depletion occurs on seasonal scales.

Each of these design features has a function: to keep harm below the threshold at which legal recognition becomes possible. This is Nixon's slow violence — but it is not primarily a representational problem. The communities in the Atacama do not fail to see the drying vegas. The vega is in front of them. The water guardian observes the declining spring. The pastoralist watches the pasture turn to dust. What they cannot do is make that observation legally legible in a system that requires corporate-generated hydrological data, tribunal-compatible evidentiary formats, and causal attribution chains that the disclosure architecture has been specifically designed to prevent. The invisibility is not a failure of representation. It is an infrastructure achievement.

This is the departure from Nixon that the framework requires. Slow harm is not the counterpart to fast violence — the gradual against the sudden, the unrecognized against the legible. Slow harm is the output of the extraction infrastructure. It accumulates because the structural architecture has been designed to authorize and conceal it. The depletion is the point — not incidentally, but structurally. Without the ability to extract at volumes that exceed the aquifer's recharge rate, and without the legal architecture that prevents this being measured and attributed, the extraction economics do not work. The slow harm is the extracted value.

Galtung is right that structural violence has no obvious subject, that the harm flows from conditions rather than from individual perpetrators. But the infrastructure of violence framework insists: these conditions were designed, and their maintenance is an ongoing choice. The DGA's decision, year after year, not to mandate well-specific disclosure is a decision. The SEA's approval of environmental impact assessments using corporate-generated baselines is a decision. The Corfo-SQM financial alignment that structurally compromises independent oversight was negotiated. The knowledge that authorizes this silence — the 2007 environmental assessment identifying aquifer stress, received by DGA and SQM and followed by permit renewals without modification — is documented. The structure has subjects. They made choices. The infrastructure of violence framework makes those choices visible.

## 5. A Hierarchy, Not a List

The framework we are proposing differs from previous typologies of violence in one critical respect: it is not a list of parallel phenomena, and the relationship between its components runs in one direction.

Extractive capitalism is the organizing logic. It requires profitable access to territories and resources — and requires, therefore, a permissive environment within which extraction can occur without the full cost of the harm being attributed and recovered. That permissive environment is built. It is built through legal instruments that pre-authorize harm (liability caps, Water Codes, quota systems that set extraction ceilings at levels sufficient for profitable operation); through institutional arrangements that structurally compromise oversight (regulatory capture, financial alignment between state and extractor, monitoring systems that generate data at insufficient resolution); through corporate architectures that distribute accountability across jurisdictions (beneficial ownership chains that obscure who profits; subsidiary structures that absorb and contain liability; disclosure regimes that satisfy financial regulators while preventing ecological accountability). This is the structural infrastructure.

Slow harm is what the infrastructure enables. The aquifer depletion, the ecosystem disruption, the generation-spanning loss of water sovereignty — these are the products of the permission structure operating as designed. The harm is legal. Each extraction is within authorized limits. Each inspection is passed. Each permit is renewed. The slow accumulation is not a side effect of extraction; it is extraction's ecological form. And it is designed to remain below the threshold at which the legal systems governing it could recognize it as harm.

Fast violence enforces the structural conditions when communities resist them. The 2019 Estallido Social and its militarized response — documented by Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch: rubber bullets, eye injuries, torture, sexual violence — was not a departure from the legal order under which lithium extraction operates. It was the legal order's enforcement arm, deployed against a mass uprising that among its demands articulated the reversal of the 1981 Water Code. President Piñera's statement — "we are at war against a powerful enemy" — was not theatrical excess. It named the structural function: fast violence protects the permission structure when the permission structure is threatened. The lithium extraction operations continued without interruption throughout the state of emergency. The infrastructure was being enforced.

The direction of this dependency matters for accountability. Slow harm cannot be addressed without addressing the structural infrastructure that makes it legal and invisible. The structural infrastructure cannot be changed without confronting the extractive logic that builds and maintains it. And the extractive logic cannot be confronted politically without fast enforcement arriving to protect the investment. These are not four parallel problems requiring four parallel solutions. They are one system, and the accountability argument must address it as such.

This is also what distinguishes the infrastructure of violence framework from a general critique of capitalism. Harvey and Moore argue, correctly, that capitalist accumulation produces harm. The infrastructure of violence framework argues something more specific and more actionable: that extraction builds a particular architecture for its harm, that the architecture is identifiable in its components and its design, that its maintainers can be named, and that the accountability argument must be addressed to the architecture as a system rather than to any single element of it. A legal challenge to one permit does not change the permit system. A journalistic investigation of one subsidiary does not change the beneficial ownership architecture. A community that documents one contamination event does not produce the pattern evidence that indicts the sector. The framework is a diagnostic for why single-register accountability keeps failing — and a specification of what systemic accountability would require.

## 6. The Salar de Atacama as Forensic Case

The dossier assembled for this paper treats the Atacama not as an example of a framework but as the material from which the framework was built. What follows is the structural argument run through the evidence.

**The permission structure is designed:**

In 1981, Decreto Ley 1.122 transformed Chilean water into a tradable commodity, independent of land. This was not incidental to the economic program of Pinochet's dictatorship; it was continuous with it. The Chicago School-influenced economists who designed the Water Code understood that concentrating water rights in private hands was a precondition for the kind of large-scale extractive investment the model required. The design held through the return of democracy in 1990 and the recognition of indigenous peoples under Ley 19.253 in 1993 — cultural recognition extended without hydrological restitution. When SQM acquired lithium and potassium concessions from Corfo in the 1990s, it was entering a legal territory pre-positioned for its purpose. The Corfo-SQM lease agreement produced what the capital topology dossier calls a "state-corporate condominium": the Chilean state retained nominal ownership of lithium resources while delegating extraction to a publicly traded corporation — and, critically, derived revenue from that extraction (Corfo accounted for 24% of SQM's lithium cost of sales in 2024, and 62% in 2023). The state became financially dependent on the extraction it nominally regulated. Independent oversight of extraction volumes is, by this financial architecture, structurally impossible.

The SQM-Corfo oversight apparatus elaborates the permission structure further. The DGA issues water rights and holds monitoring data — but does not publish it at well-specific resolution. The SEA conducts environmental impact assessments — but using corporate-generated baseline data. The CCHEN regulates lithium as a strategic material, not as a water-intensive extractive activity. The Tribunal Ambiental hears community claims — on timelines of two to five years, while the aquifer changes seasonally. Each of these design features operates as what the capital topology report calls an "accountability waiver": its visible function is oversight, but its effective function is to absorb the appearance of regulation while preventing the substance of accountability.

The most recent extension of this infrastructure: in 2024, SQM and Codelco signed a partnership agreement extending the state-corporate extraction mandate to 2060. The condition precedent of indigenous community consultation creates a procedural recognition without decisional power — communities must respond within timelines controlled by actors with vastly greater institutional resources. Meanwhile, the seawater pipeline under construction is an infrastructure hedge against the 2022 Water Code reform: if groundwater concessions face future restriction, the pipeline maintains extraction intensity regardless. The permission structure adapts to reform by building around it.

**The slow harm accumulates:**

Brine extraction depletes a closed hydrological basin. Peer-reviewed hydrological studies document measurable groundwater declines in sectors that correlate spatially and temporally with intensive extraction zones. Flamingo nesting populations decline. Vegas dry. This is not disputed in the scientific literature.

What is disputed — or rather, what is made invisible by design — is the attribution chain. SQM's 2020 Sustainable Development Plan commits to reducing water consumption by 65% and brine extraction by 50% of authorized limits. Its 2024 CDP rating: B for water, B- for climate. Its IRMA standard score: 75, described in its 20-F as "the highest score ever awarded to a lithium company worldwide." These certification systems perform what the community testimony synthesis calls "epistemic capture": the corporation that depletes the aquifer also generates the data that certifies its own responsibility. The community's embodied knowledge — the spring that has weakened, the flamingo site that has been abandoned, the pasture that has turned to dust — is inadmissible in the same evidentiary register as the CDP rating.

Community claims to the Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta document the temporal asymmetry in legal form. The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños filed a claim regarding SQM Salar's environmental compliance program; the Tribunal rejected it in its entirety on 11 June 2024. The community must now mobilize for the next claim, within procedural timelines set by a system whose speed is calibrated to corporate, not ecological, rhythms.

**Fast violence enforces:**

In January 2024, a portion of the Atacameño community blockaded access roads to SQM facilities for four days to protest the Codelco partnership extending extraction to 2060. SQM's Form 20-F describes the blockaders as a "splinter group" — a rhetorical move that translates territorial political claim into operational risk and internal community disorder. The blockade is documented not as an expression of sovereignty but as an "operational disruption." This is how corporate disclosure regimes absorb community resistance: by translating it into the language of investor risk, stripping it of political content, and returning it to the calculus of securities management.

In October 2019, the Estallido Social — a mass uprising that included Atacameño communities demanding water reform — was met with military deployment and a state of emergency. Human rights organizations documented eye injuries, torture, and sexual violence. The lithium extraction operations in the Salar de Atacama continued without interruption throughout. The authorization chain that connects the 1981 Water Code to the 2019 military response runs through Julio Ponce Lerou, Pinochet's former son-in-law, who controlled SQM through his Pampa Group holdings through the 2010s. The corporation that holds water rights as indefinitely held intangible assets is genealogically connected to the military dictatorship that authored the Water Code that made those rights possible. This is not metaphor. It is a documented capital topology.

**On what communities know that the system cannot register:**

The community testimony layer assembled for this paper is explicit about its epistemic status: it is documentary scaffolding produced without consent-based relationships, constructed from corporate disclosures, regulatory filings, and tribunal records. It is a map of silences, not testimony itself. <!-- COMMUNITY REVIEW: this paragraph requires review before publication -->

What it records is a set of structural impossibilities that constitute the lived experience of the infrastructure from below: the impossibility of knowing (SQM's well-specific extraction data is undisclosed; the DGA does not publish aquifer monitoring at usable resolution); the impossibility of timing (community claims must fit procedural windows that do not correspond to hydrological seasons); the impossibility of naming (Atacameño claims must translate the relation to water into the language of cubic meters and impact zones to be heard by a tribunal); the impossibility of refusal (communities who reject the extraction logic are framed as operational risks or splinter groups; the legal architecture provides no mechanism for territorial refusal). These impossibilities are not natural. They are designed. And designing them required the same authorship, the same decisions, the same political protection as designing the Water Code itself.

## 7. Toward a Methodology of Accountability: Extending Farmer Through Data

Paul Farmer gave us a methodology as well as a concept. In his work on structural violence in Haiti and on tuberculosis in Peru, Farmer showed what it means to trace harm from its lived consequences — bodies sick, bodies imprisoned, lives foreclosed — backward through the social structures that produced those consequences, naming the chain of causation and making it both analytically rigorous and politically legible (Farmer, 2004). The method combines ethnographic attention to particular lives with epidemiological analysis of structural patterns; community testimony with institutional data; the specific case with the systemic account. Its purpose is to make visible what power has arranged to remain invisible: to show that what presents as individual misfortune — a community's mortality rate, a prisoner's preventable illness — is the traceable product of a structure whose designers can be named.

This methodology remains foundational. But the infrastructures of violence under examination here have features that require extending Farmer's approach. The harm is dispersed not only across social classes but across jurisdictions, languages, and specialized archives that were never designed to speak to each other: corporate securities law in the United States, environmental permit records in Chile, satellite telemetry from European Space Agency platforms, community testimony in Kunza and Spanish, tribunal procedural records in administrative formats. The opacity architecture was explicitly designed to prevent the integration that would make attribution possible: disclosure thresholds calibrated to satisfy securities regulators without enabling ecological accountability; monitoring data held at spatial resolutions insufficient for independent audit; corporate entities structured across multiple jurisdictions so that no single court can see the full picture.

Extending Farmer requires what might be called *multi-modal evidence integration at the scale of the infrastructure itself*. In the Atacama, this means holding simultaneously: the corporate disclosure record (SQM's Form 20-F provides capital topology, ownership chains, lease architecture, revenue flows, and legal proceedings — all documented against SEC evidentiary standards); satellite-derived land change analysis (brine pool expansion, vegetation decline at aquifer margins, temporal correlation with extraction intensity — evidence that operates independently of corporate disclosure); community testimony (the intergenerational memory of pre-extraction hydrological conditions, the embodied knowledge of drying vegas, the oral accounts of what the 1981 Water Code produced in lived terms — essential but currently absent from the dossier, which is a structural finding about the research process, not about the communities); and tribunal and regulatory records (the timeline of legal challenges, the structure of evidentiary requirements, the temporal asymmetry between community claims and adjudication pace). None of these evidence layers is sufficient alone. The SEC filing contains no ecological data. The satellite image contains no corporate actor. The community testimony carries no legal standing in the environmental tribunal. The tribunal record documents individual permit violations, not cumulative aquifer harm. The infrastructure of violence only becomes legible when these layers are integrated — and integration is precisely what the opacity architecture was designed to prevent.

Three methodological principles follow.

**Absence as evidence.** The gaps in the disclosure architecture are findings, not empty spaces to be filled by inference. SQM does not disclose well-by-well extraction rates. The DGA does not publish aquifer monitoring data at the resolution required for independent hydrological modeling. Apple and Tesla supply chain disclosures do not name the extraction site: lithium passes through multiple processing tiers — brine extractor, carbonate producer, cathode manufacturer, cell supplier, device assembler — and downstream brands disclose only direct suppliers, rendering the Atacama administratively invisible to end consumers. Trouillot, analyzing the production of historical silences, showed that what is absent from archives is itself a finding about the power relations that produced the archive (Trouillot, 1995). The opacity architecture of extractive capitalism is documented through its gaps as much as through its contents. Mapping what cannot be known — well-specific extraction volumes, supply chain contractual terms, pre-extraction baseline data — is itself an accountability act. It shows the infrastructure doing its concealment work.

**Knowledge moments as accountability pivots.** The infrastructure of violence is not merely a set of structural conditions. It is a sequence of decisions made by identifiable actors with identifiable knowledge at identifiable times. When did SQM first receive environmental assessments indicating aquifer stress? (A 2007 assessment identifying aquifer risk was received by both DGA and SQM; extraction permits were renewed without modification.) When did the DGA first receive monitoring data showing groundwater decline? When did the SEA approve expansion assessments despite warnings? Reconstructing knowledge moments transforms a structural analysis into an accountability argument. It shifts the legal question from *did the structure produce harm?* to *did specific actors, with specific knowledge, make decisions that continued the harm?* — which is a question that courts can hear. Farmer's anthropology establishes structural causation; knowledge-moment reconstruction establishes decision-based causation. The two together build the argument that courts, UN mechanisms, and investigative journalism each need in different forms.

**Temporal sovereignty in the evidence record.** The technical reconstruction reveals that the Climate Knowledge Base pipeline that would process this evidence encodes temporal assumptions that reproduce the infrastructure's own temporal asymmetry: its TimelineEngine prioritizes English-language date formats, corporate quarterly cycles, and regulatory annual timelines, while the seasonal cycles of the Atacama aquifer, the intergenerational cycles of community memory, and the Kunza temporal frameworks through which Atacameño communities understand their territory are entirely unrepresentable in the system's current architecture. An adequate methodology for documenting infrastructures of violence must hold multiple temporal registers simultaneously: the corporate quarterly cycle, the hydrological seasonal cycle, the regulatory annual cycle, the aquifer geological cycle, and the intergenerational cycle of community knowledge. A timeline that can represent the 1981 Water Code and the 2024 Codelco partnership in the same forensic argument as a drying vega observed by a community elder and a groundwater decline measured by satellite interferometry is not merely a technical achievement. It is a political one: it refuses the temporal architecture of corporate and legal systems that has been one of the infrastructure's primary accountability shields.

These three principles extend Farmer's methodology beyond what ethnographic immersion and structural analysis alone can accomplish. They require the integration of data practices — satellite processing, corporate disclosure analysis, entity network mapping, timeline reconstruction — with the community-centered epistemology that Farmer insists on: the primacy of testimony, the ethical requirement of reciprocity, the refusal to treat community knowledge as anecdotal against corporate data's authority. The methodological challenge is to use the tools of the infrastructure's own knowledge systems — SEC filings, satellite platforms, corporate registries — against the opacity architecture those systems were partly designed to maintain, while refusing to reproduce the colonial evidentiary hierarchy — written over oral, quantitative over relational, corporate over communal — that the infrastructure depends on.

## 8. Conclusion

The aquifer beneath the Salar de Atacama is classified as an intangible asset on SQM's balance sheet. It is held in perpetuity, assessed annually for impairment, recorded at acquisition cost. The language is ordinary. It does the work of decades.

The infrastructure of violence framework proposes that this accounting entry is not incidental to the extraction it represents. It is the endpoint of a design process that began in 1981, survived democracy and protest and partial legal reform, and is currently being extended to 2060 through a partnership agreement signed by a state-owned copper company and a Wall Street-listed lithium producer. The Water Code, the Corfo-SQM condominium, the disclosure architecture, the oversight calibration, the corporate genealogy running from Pinochet's son-in-law to the balance sheets of global pension funds — these are the infrastructure. They are what makes the intangible asset possible, and what makes the dried vega legally invisible.

The framework's analytical contribution is to refuse the isolation of these elements. Slow harm is not a parallel phenomenon to extraction; it is the infrastructure's product. Structural design is not a separate problem from extractive capitalism; it is the system extraction builds and maintains for itself. Fast violence is not an exception to the legal order; it is the legal order's enforcement response when the infrastructure is challenged. Seeing the permission structure as a designed system — seeing the 1981 Water Code and the 2019 military deployment as elements of one architecture — is what makes accountability possible in a form that can actually reach the harm.

The methodology we are proposing is not an attempt to prove that bad things happened. It is an attempt to make the permission structure visible: to reconstruct the knowledge moments, to integrate the satellite and corporate and community evidence at the scale of the infrastructure itself, to produce — in the temporal and jurisdictional registers that accountability forums can actually receive — an argument that is addressed to the system and not only to its visible symptoms.

The aquifer is still depleting. The seawater pipeline is under construction. The Codelco partnership has been signed. The accountability argument is still being made.
