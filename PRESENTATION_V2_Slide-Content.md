## SLIDE 2 — The Hook

**Headline:** What we lack is not theory. It is infrastructure.

**Core quote:**

> "We have excellent climate justice theory and brave frontline communities. What we lack is the infrastructure that connects investigation to law, and community testimony to court-ready evidence."

**Three key points:**

1. **The gap is not knowledge** — investigators find patterns; communities carry the evidence in their bodies; lawyers know which courts are listening; journalists know how to tell the story. None of this is missing.

2. **The gap is connection** — evidence collected in a community stays in a community. A corporate network traced by an investigator never appears on a map. A thirty-year timeline of regulatory failure never gets assembled into something a judge can hold.

3. **The design choice** — Not a product. Not a platform. A commons stack built on **protocol logic, not platform logic**. The infrastructure belongs to those who use it. Growth through adoption, not extraction.

---

**Speaker note:**

The distinction between protocol logic and platform logic is worth dwelling on. Platform logic creates dependencies — you need the platform to access the data, and the platform's interests shape what the data looks like. Protocol logic means open schemas, community data sovereignty, the ability to implement independently. It is the difference between a road and a toll booth.

## SLIDE 3 — Ecologies of Violence

**Headline:** Violence is not an event. It is a system.

**On the naming:** *Why "ecologies of violence"?*
The term is intentional, and it's worth addressing directly. "Ecology" here doesn't mean environmental — it means systemic and co-constitutive. Just as an ecosystem is defined by the relationships between its parts, an ecology of violence is defined by how its registers depend on and reproduce each other. The four registers are not separate phenomena. They are one system.

---

**The four registers:**

**Fast violence** — The visible tip.
Raids, evictions, facility seizures, the arrest of a land defender. Fast violence is legible as violence — it generates documentation, timestamps, photographic evidence, legal events. And because it is visible, it draws all the attention. This is not incidental. Its visibility *naturalizes everything below it*.

**Slow violence** — The accumulation.
Rob Nixon's term: "a violence that occurs gradually and out of sight, a violence of delayed destruction that is dispersed across time and space, an attritional violence that is typically not viewed as violence at all." An aquifer depleted over twenty years. Soil contamination accumulating across generations. No crime scene. No single perpetrator. **This is where accountability architectures almost always fail.**

**Structural violence** — The permission structure.
The legal and institutional arrangements that make slow violence possible and fast violence selective. Liability caps that pre-authorize harm at scale. Environmental impact assessments written by consultants paid by the companies they assess. Regulatory bodies staffed by former industry executives. **This is not corruption in the ordinary sense — it is architecture.**

**Extractive violence** — The economic logic.
The reason the structures exist. The reason slow harms are allowed to accumulate. The reason fast enforcement protects infrastructure rather than communities. Extraction requires structural cover. Structural cover makes slow harm possible. And when communities resist, fast enforcement arrives to protect the investment.

---

**The core insight:**

These four registers are co-constitutive. Remove any one and the system changes. You cannot address the aquifer depletion without addressing the permit structure that authorized it. You cannot address the permit structure without asking who profits from it. You cannot hold anyone accountable for profits without tracing the corporate architecture designed to make that attribution impossible.

**Ecologies of violence are only legible when you can see all four registers working together, at scale, across time.**

---

**Speaker note:**

If the audience pushes back on the term "ecologies" — and they may — the response is: the term names something specific. These four registers are not a list. They form a system with internal dependencies. "Ecology" is the right word for a system that reproduces itself through the relationships between its parts. The violence is ecological not because it affects ecosystems (though it does), but because it has the structure of an ecosystem: interdependent, self-sustaining, difficult to disrupt at any single point.

## SLIDE 4 — What Exists?

**Headline:** The landscape is rich. The integration is missing.

**What this slide does:** Positions the Counterstack against existing platforms — not to dismiss them, but to show exactly where the gap is. Most of these are valuable and would be *inputs* to the Counterstack.

---

**The existing landscape:**

**EJAtlas** *(ICTA-UAB)*
The most comprehensive registry of ecological conflicts — 3,000+ cases, globally distributed, community-contributed. Documents that conflicts exist, who is affected, what the outcome was. Essential. But retrospective and archival: it records what is already known, referenced rather than structured, oriented toward research and advocacy rather than legal accountability.

**Global Forest Watch** *(WRI)*
Near real-time deforestation monitoring via satellite. Exceptional on *what is happening* to forest cover. Weak on *who is responsible*. Monitoring without attribution.

**OCCRP Aleph** *(Organized Crime and Corruption Reporting Project)*
A powerful OSINT data platform — ingests leaked documents, corporate registries, sanctions lists, used by investigative journalists globally. Strong on financial flows and corporate networks. Not oriented toward ecological harm specifically, and not structured for legal evidence production.

**OpenCorporates**
The world's largest open database of company information. Traces subsidiary structures, beneficial ownership chains across jurisdictions. Raw infrastructure — no conflict layer, no harm layer, no temporal causation.

**Forensic Architecture** *(Goldsmiths, University of London)*
The methodological closest cousin. Combines satellite imagery, 3D spatial modeling, witness testimony, and architectural analysis to produce evidence for courts and public inquiries. Operates at very high depth on individual cases. Not designed for scale. No platform logic — each investigation is bespoke.

**Babel Street** *(commercial)*
A commercial OSINT intelligence platform aggregating social media, public records, location data — used by government agencies, law enforcement, and corporations for surveillance and intelligence gathering. Technically sophisticated. Oriented entirely toward surveillance, not accountability. Worth naming because it proves the technology exists at scale — and because the Counterstack is, in part, a counter-design to what Babel Street represents.

---

**What each platform does — and doesn't do:**

| Platform | Conflict mapping | Corporate networks | Satellite monitoring | Temporal causation | Forum-ready evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| EJAtlas | ✓ | partial | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Global Forest Watch | partial | ✗ | ✓ | partial | ✗ |
| OCCRP Aleph | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | partial |
| OpenCorporates | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Forensic Architecture | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | partial | ✓ |
| Babel Street | ✗ | partial | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| **Counterstack** | **✓** | **✓** | **✓** | **✓** | **✓** |

---

**Speaker note:**

Naming Babel Street serves a strategic purpose: it makes visible that the underlying technology — agentic OSINT at scale, AI-assisted entity recognition, cross-jurisdictional data aggregation — already exists and is commercially deployed. The question is not whether this is possible. The question is: *who does it serve?* Babel Street serves states and corporations. The Counterstack is the counter-design.

## SLIDE 5 — What's Missing?

**Headline:** What if EJAtlas were prospective, investigative, and evidence-driven?

**What this slide does:** Names the specific gap and frames the Counterstack as the answer — not a replacement for what exists, but the missing connective layer.

---

**Three absences:**

**1. Integration**
Each platform operates in isolation. A conflict documented in EJAtlas has no connection to the beneficial ownership chain in OpenCorporates, which has no connection to the deforestation alert in Global Forest Watch, which has no connection to the corporate network in OCCRP Aleph. The ecology of violence is designed to exploit these siloes. Accountability infrastructure that replicates them cannot disrupt them.

**2. Prospective vs. archival**
EJAtlas documents what is already known and already over. The infrastructure we need is *anticipatory* — able to detect patterns before harm is fully recognized, assemble evidence while it is still legally actionable, and support communities *during* a conflict rather than archiving it afterward. The Counterstack is designed to be used while the harm is happening.

**3. Evidence production vs. documentation**
Documentation says: *this happened*. Evidence production says: *this happened, here is who caused it, here is how we know, here is the forum that can hear the claim*. The shift from documentation to evidence is the shift from visibility to accountability. Every existing platform stops at documentation. The Counterstack begins where they stop.

---

**The reframe:**

> What if EJAtlas were not a registry of past conflicts, but an active investigative apparatus — gathering evidence before harm is recognized, structuring that evidence for legal use, and routing it toward the forums most likely to produce accountability?

That is the Counterstack.

## SLIDE 7 — ShadowCorpus (Demo)

**Headline:** Mapping the network that makes harm invisible — and traceable.

**What this slide does:** Shows the Network-of-Complicity capability in action. This is a visual demo slide — the key is the map, not the text.

---

**What the audience sees:**

A network visualization showing:

- **Core node:** SQM (Sociedad Química y Minera) — the primary lithium and potassium producer in the Atacama
- **State layer:** CORFO (Chilean economic development agency) as minority shareholder — sovereign ownership creates regulatory complexity
- **Ownership chain:** SQM majority controlled by Julio Ponce Lerou (Pinochet's former son-in-law) through a chain of holding companies; full beneficial ownership partially obscured through offshore structures
- **Supply chain layer:** SQM lithium → battery manufacturers (CATL, LG Chem, Panasonic) → end-use (Apple, Tesla, Samsung, Volkswagen)
- **Regulatory layer:** DGA (Dirección General de Aguas) — the water authority that issues extraction permits; personnel connections between DGA and extractive industry
- **Harm layer:** Atacameño (Lickanantay) communities — water rights holders whose claims have been systematically dismissed; Salar de Atacama ecosystem declining

**Dark matter nodes** (shown as dashed/flagged):
- Full beneficial ownership chain beyond Ponce Lerou's holding companies: *partially unresolved*
- Actual volume of brine extraction vs. permitted volumes: *self-reported, unverified*
- DGA-industry personnel connections post-2015: *requires further investigation*

---

**What this shows about the ecology:**

The network makes visible what each register contributes:
- **Extractive violence:** SQM → supply chains → tech companies → global profit
- **Structural violence:** Water Code privatization → DGA permit system → indigenous claims dismissed
- **Slow violence:** brine extraction → aquifer depletion → ecosystem collapse (visible in satellite data)
- **Fast violence:** 2019 protests → militarized response → activist detention

**No single node is responsible. The network is the accountability architecture — and the accountability evasion.**

---

**Speaker note:**

The point of showing dark matter is methodological honesty. We don't know everything. Showing what we don't know is as important as showing what we do — it tells investigators where to go next, and it tells courts that opacity was designed, not accidental. A network map that only shows confirmed connections is less truthful than one that also shows the gaps.

## SLIDE 8 — From Tool-Based to Fully Agentic (Demo)

**Headline:** The shift from manual investigation to agentic intelligence — and why it matters for justice.

**What this slide does:** Shows the qualitative difference between existing OSINT practice and an agentic pipeline — and argues that scale is not just a technical upgrade but a change in what accountability is possible.

---

**The current state of investigation:**

A skilled OSINT investigator working on a supply chain case can, over weeks or months, trace a beneficial ownership chain through corporate registries, cross-reference satellite imagery with facility permits, and connect a scientific warning to an executive's inbox. This is exceptional work. It is also:

- Limited to what one team can hold in its head
- Dependent on what sources happen to be available in accessible languages
- Unable to detect patterns across hundreds of similar cases simultaneously
- Starting from scratch on evidence that other teams have already assembled

**The agentic shift:**

An agentic pipeline does not replace the investigator. It operates at a different scale and feeds the investigator what they need. Specifically:

- Ingests evidence across thousands of documents simultaneously, in multiple languages
- Detects patterns that would require years of manual analysis — which corporate actors appear across multiple conflict sites, which regulatory exemptions recur across jurisdictions, which supply chain relationships connect apparently unrelated harms
- Flags anomalies: a subsidiary registered in a jurisdiction with no environmental liability treaty three months before a legal challenge; an environmental impact assessment citing sources that cannot be verified
- Produces structured, source-attributed claims that link every assertion to its origin document — the evidentiary standard that makes the difference between a story and a court-ready argument

---

**For the Atacama case specifically:**

A manual investigation of SQM's water use in the Atacama might take a team of three investigators six months. An agentic pipeline can:

- Ingest all DGA permit records for the Salar de Atacama (publicly available, Spanish)
- Cross-reference permitted extraction volumes against satellite-derived brine pool expansion data (Copernicus/Sentinel-2, freely available)
- Identify discrepancies between self-reported and observed extraction
- Connect those discrepancies to the dates of indigenous community water rights hearings
- Flag the gap as evidence of knowledge: the discrepancies existed before the hearings; the company had the data

**This shifts the investigation from anecdotal to systematic — and from a single case to a pattern argument across the entire lithium sector.**

---

**The justice argument:**

Corporations deploy legal teams, regulatory affairs departments, and public relations infrastructure at scale. Communities and their legal allies cannot. The asymmetry of resources is structural. An agentic pipeline is not a technical convenience — it is a **power rebalancing mechanism**, giving communities and their allies investigative capacity commensurate with the institutional power arrayed against them.

## SLIDE 9 — Evidence-Based Timeline: From Data to Knowledge

**Headline:** Forty years of slow violence, assembled into a legal argument.

**What this slide does:** Demonstrates the timeline capability using the Atacama case — showing how data across all four registers becomes a structured accountability argument. This is the most powerful demo slide.

---

**The problem the timeline solves:**

Legal accountability requires causation. Causation requires a chain: actor → decision → consequence. In ecological violence, that chain is buried across decades, jurisdictions, and institutional archives — each link individually deniable, the chain only visible at a scale no single investigator can hold.

The evidence-based timeline assembles the chain. Not as a chronology of events, but as a **structured argument about knowledge, decision, and consequence** across all four registers.

---

**The Atacama timeline:**

```
1981 — Structural violence established
       Pinochet's Water Code privatizes water rights
       Water separated from land rights; indigenous communities lose territorial water sovereignty
       Evidence: Legislative record, DGA founding documents
       Register: Structural

1985–2000 — Extractive violence begins
       SQM acquires lithium and potassium concessions in the Salar de Atacama
       Extraction begins; brine pumping increases annually
       Evidence: SQM concession records, DGA extraction permits
       Register: Extractive

2000–2010 — Slow violence accumulates
       Satellite data shows brine pool expansion; vegetation stress at aquifer margins
       Flamingo nesting site surveys show population decline correlated with water table drop
       Evidence: Copernicus/Landsat archive imagery, ecological monitoring data
       Register: Slow

2007 — Knowledge moment
       Environmental assessment commissioned by DGA identifies aquifer stress risk
       Assessment received by DGA and SQM; extraction permits renewed without modification
       Evidence: DGA archive documents, permit renewal records
       Register: Structural (knowledge + inaction)

2012–2018 — Community claims systematically dismissed
       Atacameño communities (Lickanantay) file water rights claims with DGA
       Claims denied or deferred; no independent hydrological study commissioned
       Evidence: DGA hearing records, community legal submissions
       Register: Structural / Fast (selective enforcement of process)

2016 — External scientific warning
       Independent researchers publish aquifer depletion findings in peer-reviewed literature
       SQM cited in findings; DGA notified
       Evidence: Published papers, DGA correspondence
       Register: Structural (knowledge confirmed; inaction continues)

2018–2020 — Supply chain pressure begins
       Apple, Tesla, BMW publish supply chain sustainability commitments
       SQM listed as lithium supplier; companies conduct audits
       Audit findings: partial disclosure, extraction volumes self-reported
       Evidence: Corporate sustainability reports, audit documentation
       Register: Extractive (economic pressure shapes disclosure)

2019 — Fast violence: Suppression of resistance
       Chilean social uprising (Estallido Social) includes Atacameño protests against water extraction
       Militarized response; activist detentions; state of emergency declared
       Evidence: Human rights commission reports, news documentation
       Register: Fast

2021–present — Legal and regulatory pressure
       Chilean Constitutional Convention drafts rights-of-nature provisions (ultimately rejected)
       Environmental Tribunal cases filed by indigenous communities
       SQM operating under modified permit conditions; volume questions unresolved
       Evidence: Tribunal records, permit modifications, satellite monitoring ongoing
       Register: Structural (reform attempted; structural conditions persist)
```

---

**What the timeline proves:**

This is not a story about one company's bad behaviour. This is a forty-year ecology of violence:

- **Structural violence** created the conditions (1981 Water Code)
- **Extractive violence** exploited them (SQM concessions, supply chain demand)
- **Slow violence** accumulated while institutions looked away (aquifer depletion, ecosystem collapse)
- **Fast violence** suppressed resistance when communities organized (2019)
- And the **knowledge moments** — 2007, 2016 — show that the harm was known, the decisions were made, and the structural system protected the extraction

**For a Colombian or Chilean court:** knowledge + inaction + harm = negligence
**For a UN Loss and Damage mechanism:** who profited, who paid the cost = reparations case
**For an investigative journalist:** forty years of structural choices = the story
**For a policy body:** the permit system pre-authorized the harm = systemic reform needed

## SLIDE 10 — Further Reflections

**Headline:** What this opens — and what it requires.

**What this slide does:** Closes the presentation with the research agenda. Invites the audience into the conversation. Honest about what is built, what is in development, and what requires partnership.

---

**What the Counterstack already demonstrates:**

- A methodology for mapping ecologies of violence at scale
- Three integrated investigative capabilities — agentic pipeline, network visualization, evidence timeline — working on real case material (the Atacama)
- A positioning in the landscape that is genuinely distinct from EJAtlas, Forensic Architecture, and commercial platforms like Babel Street
- An evidence-based argument that accountability infrastructure is not a technical problem but a political and methodological one

---

**Open questions that shape the research agenda:**

**On methodology:**
- How do we maintain epistemic honesty at scale? The agentic pipeline produces evidence at a speed that outpaces human verification. What are the quality gates?
- How do we represent uncertainty without undermining the legal utility of evidence? Dark matter is honest; it is also potentially weaponizable by defence attorneys.

**On governance:**
- Who governs the schemas? Protocol logic requires someone to maintain the open standards. Who decides when the evidence model changes?
- How do we ensure community data sovereignty in practice, not just in principle? What consent frameworks are adequate when communities are in different legal systems?

**On the ecology of accountability:**
- The Counterstack produces evidence for the forums where accountability is *currently* possible. But which forums? Which jurisdictions are expanding standing? Which are contracting?
- How does the infrastructure stay relevant as the legal landscape shifts?

**On partnerships:**
- Which communities and legal partners are ready to test this against real cases?
- What does the consortium need from the Counterstack, and what can it offer in return?

---

**The closing argument:**

> Fast violence draws the eye.
> Slow violence accumulates out of sight.
> Structural violence holds the permission in place.
> Extractive violence is the reason the system exists.
>
> **The ecology of violence is only legible when mapped together, at scale, across time.**
>
> That is what we are building.
> This is the research agenda we are proposing.
> The question is whether you want to build it with us.
