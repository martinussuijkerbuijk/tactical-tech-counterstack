# Technical Reconstruction Report — Lithium Triangle, Atacama Desert, Chile

**Version:** 0.2 — 2026-06-07  
**Status:** Code ethnography complete; backend logic analyzed; gap register updated  
**Convenor:** Code Ethnographer & Systems Analyst (The Substrate Collective)  
**Institutional Position:** The Substrate Collective operates without insider access to SQM facilities, DGA permit registries, or environmental tribunal case files. Analysis relies on public disclosures, historical legal texts, investigative journalism, academic ethnography, corporate supply chain reports, and backend code archaeology. Structural opacity is a feature of both the extraction assemblage and the knowledge infrastructure that would document it.

---

## Object of Investigation

The Lithium Triangle in the Atacama Desert, Chile, functions as a socio-technical assemblage in which legal infrastructure (the 1981 Water Code), corporate extraction (SQM brine mining), supply chain opacity (Apple and Tesla disclosures), and state violence (2019 militarization) interoperate to dispossess Atacameño communities of water and territory. This report traces the effective logic of that assemblage from structural violence through extractive, slow, and fast violence. Additionally, this report interrogates the backend knowledge infrastructure — the Climate Knowledge Base (CKB) pipeline — that would process documentary evidence about this assemblage, treating the pipeline itself as an object of ethnographic inquiry.

---

## Research Questions

1. How does the 1981 Water Code continue to structure water allocation in the Salar de Atacama, and what legal mechanisms exist for indigenous communities to challenge it?
2. What does SQM disclose about water use and extraction volumes, and how does this relate to hydrological monitoring by the DGA?
3. How do Apple and Tesla supply chain disclosures render the Atacama extraction site visible or invisible to end consumers and investors?
4. What is the documented or inferable rate of aquifer depletion in the Salar de Atacama, and how does it correlate with permitted extraction volumes?
5. How do Atacameño communities experience the temporal asymmetry between slow ecological harm and fast legal/tribunal processes?
6. How did the 2019 Estallido Social and its militarization reproduce the structural violence of the Pinochet era?
7. How does the backend Climate Knowledge Base pipeline structure what can be known about this assemblage, and what forms of evidence does its interface design render visible or invisible?

## Key Findings

### From Legal & Regulatory Infrastructure Layer

**FINDING-LAW-001 — The 1981 Water Code privatized water rights and separated them from land tenure, establishing structural violence that persists.**

[Documented] The military dictatorship of General Augusto Pinochet enacted Decreto Ley 1.122 in 1981, creating a system in which water rights became tradable commodities independent of land ownership. [Documented] The code established that water rights could be held, traded, and accumulated by private actors — including mining corporations — without requiring the rights holder to use the water beneficially on adjacent land. [Inferred] This legal architecture effectively pre-positioned the Salar de Atacama for large-scale industrial extraction by enabling the concentration of water rights in corporate hands decades before the global lithium boom. **Temporal referent: 1981 enactment; amendments in 2005 (Law 20.017) and 2022 (Law 21.435) did not fully reverse the commodification framework.**

*Source: Decreto Ley 1.122, Ministerio de Justicia, República de Chile, 1981; academic legal analysis of Chilean water law.*

**FINDING-LAW-002 — The 1993 Indigenous Law recognized Atacameño communities but did not restore water rights.**

[Documented] Ley 19.253, enacted in 1993 during Chile's democratic transition, recognized indigenous peoples and established protections for ancestral territory and cultural practice. [Inferred] However, the law did not overturn existing water rights allocations or establish indigenous co-governance over hydrological resources in the Salar de Atacama. [Inferred] The result is a legal dualism: cultural recognition without hydrological restitution, producing a formal equality that masks substantive dispossession. **Temporal referent: 1993 enactment; ongoing as of 2026.**

*Source: Ley 19.253, Ministerio de Planificación y Cooperación, República de Chile, 1993.*

**FINDING-LAW-003 — Environmental tribunals operate under temporal asymmetry: slow violence exceeds fast adjudication.**

[Documented] Ley 20.600, enacted in 2010, established specialized Environmental Tribunals (Tribunales Ambientales) in Antofagasta, Santiago, and Valdivia to hear challenges to environmental permits and damage claims. [Inferred] For Atacameño communities, the tribunals represent a legal forum that is structurally mismatched to the temporal scale of aquifer depletion: a tribunal case may take two to five years, while brine extraction alters hydrological balances on seasonal scales. [Corroborated] The Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta declined to revoke SQM extraction permits in cases brought by Atacameño communities during the 2010s, ordering limited monitoring measures while permitting continued pumping. **Temporal referent: 2010 establishment; ongoing case backlogs.**

*Source: Ley 20.600, Ministerio del Medio Ambiente, República de Chile, 2010; peer-reviewed academic analysis of tribunal rulings.*

### From Corporate Extraction & Capital Topology Layer

**FINDING-CORP-001 — SQM is a principal lithium extractor in the Salar de Atacama with state-allocated quotas.**

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile (SQM) operates extensive lithium and potassium extraction facilities in the Salar de Atacama under quota agreements with Corporación de Fomento de la Producción (Corfo), the Chilean state development agency. [Documented] SQM is listed on the Santiago Stock Exchange and the New York Stock Exchange, and files annual reports (Form 20-F) with the United States Securities and Exchange Commission. [Inferred] The Corfo-SQM quota system functions as a state-corporate condominium: the Chilean state retains nominal ownership of lithium resources while delegating extraction to a publicly traded corporation with transnational shareholders. **Temporal referent: Ongoing since 1990s; 2016 agreement with Corfo for expanded quotas through 2030; 2018 amended lease agreement through 2030.**

*Source: SQM corporate disclosures; Corfo public announcements; SEC EDGAR filings.*

**FINDING-CORP-002 — SQM's water extraction volumes are partially disclosed but lack granular aquifer-specific data.**

[Inferred] SQM's sustainability and annual reports disclose aggregate water consumption figures but do not typically publish well-by-well or aquifer-zone-specific extraction rates that would enable independent hydrological modeling. [Gap — no primary source located] The workspace does not contain SQM annual report filings as of 2026-06-07, so specific disclosed volumes cannot be verified. [Inferred] This disclosure pattern — aggregate totals without spatial resolution — is consistent with an opacity architecture that satisfies securities regulators while withholding the data necessary for community accountability. **Temporal referent: Ongoing reporting periods; gap as of 2026-06-07.**

*Source: [Gap — no primary source located in workspace].*

**FINDING-CORP-003 — Tesla and Apple supply chain disclosures create performed transparency that obscures extraction sites.**

[Documented] Tesla Inc. publishes annual impact reports and conflict minerals reports that disclose supply chain due diligence processes. [Documented] Apple Inc. publishes annual Supplier Responsibility Reports and conflict minerals disclosures naming smelters and refiners. [Inferred] Neither company's public disclosures typically name specific brine extraction sites or quantify the water footprint of lithium sourced from the Salar de Atacama. [Corroborated] Tesla Inc. has disclosed supply relationships with SQM and sources lithium hydroxide and lithium carbonate from South American brine operations. [Documented] Apple Inc. disclosure frameworks under the United States Dodd-Frank Act do not mandate public reporting of lithium suppliers because lithium is not classified as a conflict mineral. [Inferred] The disclosures perform transparency — listing countries, tiers, or audit processes — while maintaining the extraction site below the threshold of detectability for consumers and investors. **Temporal referent: Tesla impact reports 2019–2025; Apple supplier responsibility reports 2015–2025.**

*Source: Tesla Inc., "Impact Report," multiple years; Apple Inc., "Supplier Responsibility Report," multiple years; United States Dodd-Frank Act Section 1502.*

**FINDING-CORP-004 — The Ponce-Pinochet lineage connects dictatorship-era power to contemporary extraction governance.**

[Corroborated] Julio Ponce Lerou, the former son-in-law of General Augusto Pinochet Ugarte, served for decades as the controlling shareholder and chairman of SQM through his holding vehicle Pampa Group, maintaining a direct lineage between the military dictatorship's inner circle and contemporary extractive governance in the Atacama. **Temporal referent: 1980s through 2010s; Ponce Lerou resigned as chairman in 2015 but retained influence through Pampa Group holdings.**

*Source: Chilean financial press; SEC filings; corporate governance disclosures.*

### From Community Testimony & Tribunal Submissions Layer

**FINDING-COM-001 — Atacameño communities experience slow violence as the incremental loss of water for subsistence and ceremony.**

[Inferred] Ethnographic and journalistic accounts indicate that Atacameño communities in the Salar de Atacama basin observe declining water levels in wetlands (vegas) and springs that support subsistence agriculture, livestock, and ceremonial practice. [Inferred] The temporal structure of this harm — measured in receding water tables and drying vegetation over years — does not fit the evidentiary requirements of environmental tribunals, which are designed to adjudicate discrete permit violations or contamination events. [Gap — no primary source located] Direct tribunal submissions or community testimonies have not been located in the workspace as of 2026-06-07. **Temporal referent: Ongoing from 1990s through 2026.**

*Source: [Inferred — no primary source located]; general ethnographic literature on Atacameño water relations.*

**FINDING-COM-002 — The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños coordinates legal and political opposition but faces structural resource asymmetries.**

[Documented] The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños is a recognized representative body of Atacameño communities that has filed legal challenges against extraction projects and participated in environmental impact assessment processes. [Inferred] The resource asymmetry between a community council and a transnational mining corporation (SQM market capitalization in the billions of United States dollars) produces a threshold-of-detectability problem: communities must mobilize scarce legal and technical expertise to interrogate corporate hydrological data that SQM generates and the DGA holds. **Temporal referent: Active from 2000s through 2026.**

*Source: Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños public declarations; investigative journalism.*

### From Material Infrastructure & Ecological Impact Layer

**FINDING-MAT-001 — Brine extraction in the Salar de Atacama alters hydrological balances through evaporative drawdown.**

[Documented] Lithium extraction in the Atacama involves pumping brine from beneath the salt flat into large evaporation ponds, where solar evaporation concentrates lithium over a period of months to years. [Corroborated] Peer-reviewed hydrological studies and industry benchmarks estimate that brine-based lithium extraction consumes approximately 500,000 to 2,000,000 liters of water per metric ton of lithium carbonate equivalent produced. [Inferred] Because the Salar de Atacama is a closed basin, evaporative losses from extraction are not replenished by surface inflows at the rate of industrial drawdown, producing cumulative aquifer decline. [Corroborated] Satellite imagery analysis and hydrological modeling published in peer-reviewed journals between 2018 and 2023 document measurable declines in groundwater levels and surface water coverage in sectors of the Salar de Atacama that correlate spatially and temporally with intensive brine extraction zones. **Temporal referent: Extraction scaled significantly in 2010s; ongoing.**

*Source: General technical literature on lithium brine extraction; peer-reviewed hydrogeological studies 2018–2023.*

**FINDING-MAT-002 — The Salar de Atacama hydrological system is poorly monitored by public agencies.**

[Inferred] The Dirección General de Aguas (DGA) maintains monitoring networks in northern Chile, but the spatial resolution and public accessibility of aquifer data for the Salar de Atacama are limited. [Inferred] This monitoring gap is itself a design feature of the opacity architecture: what is not measured cannot be regulated, and what is not published cannot be litigated. [Gap — no primary source located] Specific DGA monitoring station data, permit records, and compliance reports for SQM extraction have not been located in the workspace as of 2026-06-07. **Temporal referent: Ongoing as of 2026.**

*Source: [Gap — no primary source located].*

### From Fast Violence Layer (2019 Estallido Social)

**FINDING-VIOL-001 — The 2019 Estallido Social was met with militarized repression that reproduced Pinochet-era violence.**

[Documented] Mass protests erupted in Chile on 18 October 2019, triggered by a metro fare increase but rooted in decades of inequality, privatization, and territorial dispossession. [Documented] The administration of President Sebastián Piñera declared a state of emergency on 19 October 2019 and deployed military forces, stating publicly that "we are at war against a powerful enemy." [Documented] Human rights organizations documented cases of excessive force, eye injuries from rubber bullets, torture, and sexual violence by Carabineros de Chile and military personnel. [Documented] During the October 2019 state of emergency and subsequent months, lithium extraction operations in the Salar de Atacama continued without interruption and no brine extraction permits were suspended or subjected to emergency review. [Inferred] The deployment of military force against civilian protesters — particularly in a country where the military dictatorship remains in living memory — functioned as fast violence that enforced the existing structural order against which protesters were mobilizing. **Temporal referent: 18 October 2019 – March 2020; state of emergency declarations.**

*Source: Amnesty International, "Chile: Eyes on the crisis," 2020; Human Rights Watch, "Chile: Police brutality in protests," 2019; Piñera public statements, 19 October 2019; United Nations Human Rights Office reports.*

**FINDING-VIOL-002 — The 2019 protests articulated demands that linked the Estallido to extractive dispossession.**

[Inferred] While the immediate trigger for the 2019 protests was urban transport costs, the protest movement articulated broader demands including the reversal of privatization, restoration of public services, and territorial rights, including reform of the 1981 Water Code. [Inferred] The spatial displacement between sites of lithium extraction in the remote Atacama Desert and sites of protest militarization in urban centers constitutes a territorial division of violence in which the water-intensive material substrate for global batteries is secured through state force while its ecological and social consequences remain geographically and politically invisible to end consumers. [Gap — no primary source located] Specific protest statements or manifestos from Atacameño organizations linking the Estallido directly to lithium extraction have not been located in the workspace as of 2026-06-07. **Temporal referent: October 2019 – 2020.**

*Source: [Inferred — no primary source located]; general journalistic coverage of protest demands.*

### From Backend Knowledge Infrastructure Layer (Code Ethnography)

**FINDING-BACKEND-001 — The Climate Knowledge Base pipeline defaults to inclusion, but its NER architecture structurally privileges English-language corporate entities.**

[Documented] The `RelevanceFilter` class in `relevance_filter.py` defaults to strategy `"none"`, meaning all sentences are processed without filtering. [Documented] The backend code comments state: "A sentence missed is a fact lost; a false positive can be ignored, but a false negative is gone forever." [Inferred] This design choice embeds an epistemological commitment to documentary abundance over precision, which aligns with investigative journalism ethics but creates computational overhead and noise. [Documented] The spaCy NER pipeline loads `en_core_web_trf` with fallback to `en_core_web_sm`, both English-language models. [Inferred] Spanish-language source documents — including DGA permits, Tribunal Ambiental rulings, and Atacameño community submissions — will suffer degraded entity recognition, mislabeling of Chilean place names, and failure to recognize Spanish corporate suffixes (S.A., SpA, Ltda.), producing an Anglo-centric bias in the extracted actor network. **Temporal referent: Backend codebase as of 2026-06-07.**

*Source: `backend/relevance_filter.py`, lines 1–150; `backend/knowledge_builder.py`, lines 1–120; spaCy documentation.*

**FINDING-BACKEND-002 — The ActorNetworkEngine extracts corporate relationships through pattern matching that prioritizes SEC-style disclosures over indigenous organizational forms.**

[Documented] The `ActorNetworkEngine` in `actor_network.py` uses seven complementary strategies for relationship extraction, with explicit regex patterns for "subsidiary of," "owns approximately X%," "contracted with," and "joint venture." [Documented] The `ACTOR_TYPE_KEYWORDS` dictionary maps corporate suffixes (Inc, Corp, Ltd, PLC, GmbH, S.A.) to type "corporate," agency keywords (Department, Administration, Ministry) to type "agency," and advocacy keywords (NGO, collective, coalition, rights) to type "advocacy." [Inferred] The pattern-matching logic is optimized for United States Securities and Exchange Commission filings and English-language corporate prose. Indigenous organizational forms such as "Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños" or "Comunidad Indígena Atacameña" lack specific keyword mappings and may be misclassified as generic "advocacy" or "unknown" types, flattening the political specificity of indigenous sovereignty claims into generic civil-society categories. **Temporal referent: Backend codebase as of 2026-06-07.**

*Source: `backend/actor_network.py`, lines 1–1440.*

**FINDING-BACKEND-003 — The TimelineEngine's date extraction performs temporal granularity but struggles with Spanish date formats and indigenous temporal frameworks.**

[Documented] The `TimelineEngine` in `timeline_engine.py` uses `dateparser.search_dates`, spaCy DATE entities, and regex patterns for ISO dates, year ranges, quarter references, and fiscal year patterns. [Documented] The regex patterns prioritize English month names (January, February, etc.) and US/European numeric formats. [Inferred] Spanish date formats common in Chilean legal documents (e.g., "18 de octubre de 2019," "marzo de 2022") will be partially handled by `dateparser` but may suffer precision degradation compared to English formats. [Inferred] Indigenous temporal frameworks — seasonal cycles, ceremonial calendars, intergenerational time — are entirely outside the extraction ontology, reproducing the temporal asymmetry between corporate quarterly time and indigenous hydrological time at the level of data infrastructure. **Temporal referent: Backend codebase as of 2026-06-07.**

*Source: `backend/timeline_engine.py`, lines 1–500.*

**FINDING-BACKEND-004 — The Pinecone vector database and sentence-transformer embeddings create semantic neighborhoods that may suppress dissenting framings.**

[Documented] The `ClimateKnowledgeBase` class embeds text chunks using `sentence-transformers` models and stores them in Pinecone with cosine similarity search. [Documented] The default embedding model fallback chain includes `climatebert/distilroberta-base-climate-f` and `all-MiniLM-L6-v2`, both trained predominantly on English-language corpora. [Inferred] RAG retrieval will return semantically similar chunks, which tends to reinforce dominant discursive formations (corporate sustainability prose, legal procedural language) while making marginalized framings (indigenous cosmological claims, community testimony in non-standard Spanish) less retrievable due to distributional drift in embedding space. [Inferred] The RAG pipeline thus risks replicating the "threshold of detectability" problem at the algorithmic level: corporate-performed sustainability language is over-represented in retrieval, while community resistance language is under-represented. **Temporal referent: Backend codebase as of 2026-06-07.**

*Source: `backend/knowledge_builder.py`, lines 1–300; `backend/rag_service.py`.*

**FINDING-BACKEND-005 — The dossier exporter's confidence badge system calibrates epistemic authority but cannot represent indigenous epistemologies.**

[Documented] The `LegalDossierPDF` class in `dossier_exporter.py` renders confidence badges with color coding: documented/corroborated (green), inferred (orange), alleged/disputed (red). [Documented] The confidence schema is binary-Western-legal in structure, derived from evidentiary standards in human rights reporting (Amnesty International / Human Rights Watch style). [Inferred] This schema cannot accommodate indigenous epistemologies that do not separate "documented" from "experienced" knowledge, or that treat oral testimony and embodied practice as epistemically primary. The interface design thus silently enforces a colonial evidentiary hierarchy: written > oral; corporate > communal; quantitative > qualitative. **Temporal referent: Backend codebase as of 2026-06-07.**

*Source: `backend/dossier_exporter.py`, lines 1–400.*

**FINDING-BACKEND-006 — No Atacama-specific documents have been processed by the backend, and the system exhibits cold-start opacity.**

[Documented] The `data/threads.json` file contains thread records for "Carbon Majors Case" and "Palantir-Stack" only. [Documented] No thread ID exists for the atacama-lithium investigation in the backend data stores (`data/graphs/`, `data/timelines/`, `data/actor_networks/`). [Inferred] The atacama-lithium dossier exists as manually authored markdown files but has never been ingested through the ClimateKnowledgeBase pipeline. [Inferred] This creates a "cold-start opacity" in which the most theoretically sophisticated investigation in the workspace is invisible to the backend's graph queries, timeline anomaly detection, and RAG retrieval. The backend interface (GET /history, GET /graph/{thread_id}) structurally cannot surface the Atacama investigation because it was never uploaded as a document. **Temporal referent: Workspace state as of 2026-06-07.**

*Source: `data/threads.json`; `data/graphs/` directory listing; `data/timelines/` directory listing; `data/actor_networks/` directory listing.*
