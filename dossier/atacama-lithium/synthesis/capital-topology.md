## 1. Executive Summary

This report maps the ownership structures, contractual architectures, financial flows, and jurisdictional distribution of the lithium extraction assemblage centered on the Salar de Atacama, Chile. The analysis treats corporate disclosures not as transparent windows but as calibrated instruments of the opacity architecture: they reveal enough to satisfy securities regulators while withholding the granular data necessary for community accountability.

The capital topology reveals a state-corporate condominium in which the Chilean state (via Corfo) retains nominal ownership of lithium resources, delegates extraction to a publicly traded corporation (SQM S.A.) with transnational shareholders, and is now restructuring that relationship through a partnership with Codelco that would extend state-corporate control from 2031 to 2060. Meanwhile, water rights obtained under the 1981 Water Code and its 2022 amendment create a separate layer of legal capture that operates independently of lithium quota regimes, enabling hydrological extraction even where mineral leases face political contestation.

## 3. Contractual Architecture — The SQM-Corfo Condominium

### 3.1 The Lease Agreement and Project Agreement

[Documented] SQM Salar SpA (formerly SQM Salar S.A.), a wholly owned subsidiary of SQM, holds exclusive and temporary leasehold rights to exploit mineral resources in the Salar de Atacama until December 31, 2030. The underlying properties and mineral rights are owned by Corporación de Fomento de la Producción (Corfo), a Chilean state development agency. The rights are granted under: (i) a lease agreement over mining exploitation concessions with Corfo, as amended from time to time, and (ii) the Salar de Atacama project agreement with Corfo, as amended from time to time (collectively, the "SQM-Corfo Agreements").

[Documented] The SQM-Corfo Agreements require SQM Salar to:
- Make quarterly lease payments to Corfo based on product sales from leased mining properties;
- Contribute US$18.9 million per year to research and development efforts;
- Contribute between US$10 million and US$15 million per year to communities in close proximity to the Salar de Atacama;
- Contribute 1.7% of total annual sales of SQM Salar to the Antofagasta Regional Government and the municipalities of San Pedro de Atacama, María Elena, and Antofagasta for regional development;
- Maintain Corfo's rights over the mining exploitation concessions;
- Make annual payments to the Chilean government for concession rights.

[Documented] The SQM-Corfo Agreements expire on December 31, 2030. As of December 31, 2024, SQM had extracted approximately 52% of the total permitted accumulated extraction and sales limit of lithium under the lithium extraction and sales limits, leaving approximately 48% of the authorized quota for the remaining six years.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, Item 10.C "Material Contracts — SQM-Corfo Agreements"; SQM 20-F risk factor disclosures.

| US$4,000 – US$5,000 | 8.0% |

| [Higher brackets not fully extracted] | [Gap] |

[Documented] Expenses related to Corfo were US$397 million in 2024, US$1,868.9 million in 2023, and US$3,273.0 million in 2022. [Inferred] The dramatic decline in Corfo expenses from 2022 to 2024 tracks the collapse in global lithium prices, revealing that the Chilean state's extraction rent is structurally dependent on commodity price cycles over which neither Corfo nor Atacameño communities exercise control.

[Documented] In 2024, Corfo accounted for approximately 24% of the lithium business line's cost of sales; in 2023, Corfo accounted for approximately 62% of the same business line's cost of sales.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, Item 10.C and financial note disclosures; SQM 20-F 2023 comparative data.

### 3.3 CCHEN Quotas — The Nuclear Shadow over Lithium

[Documented] The Chilean Nuclear Energy Commission (Comisión Chilena de Energía Nuclear, CCHEN) has imposed quotas that limit the total tonnage of lithium authorized to be sold from the Salar de Atacama, along with other conditions. [Documented] SQM Salar holds an obligation to CCHEN regarding the exploitation and sale of lithium from the Salar de Atacama, which prohibits the use of lithium for nuclear fusion. [Inferred] The presence of a nuclear energy commission in the governance chain of battery mineral extraction reveals a jurisdictional layering in which lithium is simultaneously classified as a civilian energy transition input and a strategic material subject to national security restrictions, producing divergent accountability regimes.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, government regulation disclosures.

### 4.2 Chilean Mining Tax Extraction

[Documented] The Chilean Internal Revenue Service (Servicio de Impuestos Internos, SII) has sought to extend the specific tax on mining activities to lithium mining. As of December 31, 2023, SQM had paid a total of US$986.3 million for specific tax on mining activities applied to lithium, related to tax years 2012 through 2023 (fiscal years 2011 through 2022). [Documented] This amount included US$59.5 million in over-assessed amounts, US$818.0 million in disputed taxes (net of corporate income tax impact), and US$108.8 million in interest. [Documented] SQM Salar has filed seven tax claims against the SII.

[Inferred] The tax dispute reveals a second rentier layer: the Chilean state extracts revenue from lithium through both Corfo lease payments and mining-specific taxation, while the cost of both is ultimately borne by the aquifer and the communities who depend on it.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, Item 8.A.7 "Legal Proceedings — Chilean Tax Litigation."

### 4.3 Capital Expenditures and Expansion Investment

[Documented] During 2023, SQM had total capital expenditures of US$1,103.6 million. [Documented] Key expansion projects include: capacity expansion of lithium hydroxide production in Chile from 40,000 metric tons per year to 100,000 metric tons per year by 2025; investment in the Mt. Holland lithium project in Western Australia with completion of the Kwinana lithium hydroxide refinery by mid-2025; and construction of a seawater pipeline with capacity of 900 liters per second, projected to enter operation by the end of 2026.

[Inferred] The seawater pipeline, while framed as a sustainability measure to reduce freshwater consumption, also functions as an infrastructure hedge against future water-rights restrictions under the amended Water Code, enabling SQM to maintain extraction intensity even if groundwater concessions are challenged or revoked.

## 5. The Codelco Partnership — Restructuring the State-Corporate Nexus

### 5.1 Partnership Agreement of May 31, 2024

[Documented] On May 31, 2024, SQM and Corporación Nacional del Cobre de Chile (Codelco), the Chilean state-owned copper mining company, entered into a partnership agreement that establishes the rights and obligations of the parties to form an entity for the development of mining and production activities aimed at the production of lithium, potassium, and other products from the properties of Corfo in the Salar de Atacama. [Documented] The entity is to be formed through the merger by incorporation of Codelco's subsidiary, Minera Tarar SpA, into SQM Salar, subject to terms and conditions set forth in the partnership agreement.

[Documented] Minera Tarar will obtain the mineral exploitation rights in the Salar de Atacama for the period January 1, 2031, to December 31, 2060, pursuant to a lease agreement and a project agreement to be entered into with Corfo (the "Tarar-Corfo Agreements"). [Documented] A number of conditions precedent remain to be satisfied, including: completion of the consultation process with indigenous communities in the Salar de Atacama; termination of the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission investigation into SQM on specified terms; execution of the Tarar-Corfo Agreements; and transfer of SQM's lithium business assets in the Salar de Atacama that are not currently owned by SQM Salar.

[Inferred] The partnership agreement extends the state-corporate condominium by three decades (2031–2060) while introducing Codelco as a state enterprise partner, potentially increasing the Chilean state's direct extraction rent but also diluting SQM's exclusive control. [Inferred] The requirement for indigenous community consultation as a condition precedent creates a procedural veto that may be structurally undermined by the temporal asymmetry between consultation timelines and the urgency of securing post-2030 extraction rights.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, Item 10.C "Material Contracts — Codelco Partnership Agreement" and risk factor disclosures.

## 6. Water Rights & Jurisdictional Topology

### 6.1 Legal Presence vs. Material Presence vs. Decisional Presence

[Documented] SQM S.A. is legally incorporated in the Republic of Chile and listed on both the Santiago Stock Exchange and the New York Stock Exchange. [Documented] Its lithium extraction subsidiary, SQM Salar SpA, is contractually governed by Chilean law under the SQM-Corfo Agreements, which are themselves instruments of Chilean public law delegating state-owned mineral resources to private leasehold. [Documented] SQM is materially present in the Salar de Atacama through evaporation ponds, brine pumping wells, and processing facilities located in the Atacama Desert of the Antofagasta Region. [Documented] Operational decisions regarding extraction volumes, well placement, and expansion are made from SQM's corporate offices and are subject to Chilean regulatory oversight by Corfo, the Dirección General de Aguas (DGA), the Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental (SEA), and the Comisión Chilena de Energía Nuclear (CCHEN).

[Inferred] The gaps between these locations of presence are themselves findings: legal incorporation in Santiago and New York separates corporate liability from the material site of ecological harm; contractual governance by Chilean public law delegates state sovereignty to a privately listed entity; and decisional presence is distributed across multiple agencies (Corfo, DGA, SEA, CCHEN) such that no single locus of accountability exists for cumulative aquifer impact.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, Items 1, 3, and 10; synthesis from regulatory architecture documented in scope-statement.md and technical-reconstruction.md.

### 6.2 Water Rights as Intangible Assets

[Documented] SQM classifies water rights as intangible assets on its balance sheet, separating them into: (i) finite rights, amortized using the straight-line method, and (ii) indefinite rights, which are not amortized because they represent rights granted in perpetuity subject to annual impairment assessment. [Documented] Water rights acquired by SQM relate to water from natural sources and are recorded at acquisition cost. [Documented] SQM holds water use rights obtained from the Dirección General de Aguas for supply of water from rivers and wells near its production facilities.

[Documented] In January 2022, the Chilean Congress approved a bill amending the Chilean Water Code (Código de Agua), which was published on April 6, 2022, becoming Law 21.435. The amendment introduces: (1) temporary water rights granted for a maximum of 30 years; (2) expiration for non-use; (3) priority for human consumption and sanitation; (4) minimum ecological flow requirements determined by the DGA; and (5) registration obligations with sanctions for non-compliance.

[Inferred] The reclassification of water from a public trust or communal relation into a balance-sheet intangible asset — and its subsequent governance as a depreciable or indefinitely held property right — exemplifies the ontological erasure at the heart of the extraction assemblage. The 2022 amendment introduces procedural constraints (30-year limits, ecological flows) that SQM is already hedging against through its seawater pipeline investment.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, financial note disclosures on intangible assets and Item 3.D risk factors; Decreto Ley 1.122 (1981 Water Code); Law 21.435 (2022 Water Code amendment).

### 6.3 Water Supply Volumes and Sources

[Documented] For its lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide production processes at the Lithium Chemical Plant, in 2024 SQM recovered approximately 1,060,000 cubic meters of ultrapure water from liquid plant waste. The remainder of water required for the process was purchased from third parties, and drinking water was purchased from local utility companies. [Documented] Water for the Nueva Victoria and Salar de Atacama facilities is obtained from wells near the production facilities. [Documented] The main sources of water for nitrate and iodine facilities at Pedro de Valdivia, María Elena, and Coya Sur are the Loa and San Salvador rivers.

[Gap — no primary source located] SQM does not disclose well-by-well or aquifer-zone-specific extraction rates in its SEC filings. Aggregate figures satisfy securities disclosure requirements while preventing independent hydrological modeling of cumulative drawdown attributable to specific pumping locations.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, Item 4.D "Property, Plants and Equipment — Water Supply."

## 8. Authorization Chain & Procurement Navigation

### 8.1 From Pinochet Water Code to SQM Lease

[Documented] Decreto Ley 1.122, enacted in 1981 by the military dictatorship of General Augusto Pinochet, established the Chilean Water Code that privatized water rights and separated them from land tenure. [Documented] In the 1990s, Corfo entered into lease agreements with SQM Salar for exploitation of lithium and potassium resources in the Salar de Atacama. [Documented] These agreements were amended over time, most significantly in 2016 with expanded quotas through 2030. [Documented] On May 31, 2024, SQM and Codelco signed a partnership agreement to extend exploitation rights from 2031 to 2060.

[Inferred] The authorization chain runs from the structural violence of the 1981 Water Code (dispossession by legal design), through the Corfo-SQM lease (delegation of state property to private extraction), to the Codelco partnership (re-nationalization of extraction rent without restitution of water sovereignty to Atacameño communities). At no point in this chain was hydrological sovereignty returned to indigenous communities.

**Source:** Decreto Ley 1.122, Ministerio de Justicia, República de Chile, 1981; SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F 2024, Item 10.C; scope-statement.md and technical-reconstruction.md.

### 8.2 Oversight Mechanisms and Their Waivers

[Documented] SQM is subject to oversight by: Corfo (quota and lease compliance); DGA (water rights monitoring); SEA (environmental impact assessment); CCHEN (lithium sales quotas and non-proliferation); the Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta (judicial review of permits); and the Comité de Ministros (appellate review of environmental qualifications).

[Inferred] Despite this apparent density of oversight, several design features function as accountability waivers: (1) Corfo's revenue is derived from SQM sales, creating a financial incentive not to restrict extraction; (2) DGA monitoring data is not published at well-by-well resolution; (3) SEA environmental impact assessments rely on corporate-generated baseline data; (4) CCHEN regulates lithium as a strategic material, not as a water-intensive extractive activity; (5) Tribunal Ambiental processes operate on multi-year timelines mismatched to seasonal hydrological change; and (6) the Comité de Ministros includes the Ministerio de Minería, which also architects the National Lithium Strategy.

**Source:** SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F 2024, government regulation disclosures; synthesis from actor-network.md and technical-reconstruction.md.
