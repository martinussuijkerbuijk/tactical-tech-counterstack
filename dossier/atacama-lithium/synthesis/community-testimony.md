## 1. Reflexive Preamble: The Limits of This Layer

[Documented] The Substrate Collective produced this Community Testimony Layer on 7 June 2026 to map affected communities and document the lived experience of opacity under the lithium extraction assemblage in the Salar de Atacama, Chile. [Documented] As of the date of production, The Substrate Collective has not established consent-based relationships with any Atacameño community, organization, or individual. [Documented] No first-person oral history interviews, participatory mapping sessions, or structured interviews with Atacameño water guardians, community authorities, or residents have been conducted. [Documented] This layer is therefore a *documentary scaffolding* constructed from publicly available corporate disclosures, regulatory filings, tribunal rulings, and human rights reports. It is not community testimony. It is a map of the silences, a register of the gaps, and a preliminary orientation for future reciprocal engagement.

[Inferred] The absence of direct community testimony in this layer is not an empty space to be filled by inference; it is a structural finding. The opacity architecture of the Atacama lithium assemblage operates, in part, by making the voices of affected communities difficult to access from a European research station. The same legal and technical infrastructures that privatize water and fragment supply chains also fragment the circuits through which community knowledge travels. [Inferred] This layer treats that inaccessibility as itself a feature of the violence under investigation, not as a methodological failure to be hidden.

## 2. Affected Community Map

### 2.1 The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños

[Documented] The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños, also referred to as the Atacameño Peoples Council, is a recognized representative body that coordinates 18 Atacameño indigenous communities in the Atacama region of Chile. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, risk factor disclosures.` [Documented] The Consejo advocates for the rights, traditions, and interests of the Atacameño people, including land use, environmental protection, and economic development. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños filed a claim with the Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta regarding the Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente-approved environmental compliance program of SQM Salar SpA. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024; Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta ruling referenced in SQM disclosures.`

[Documented] On 11 June 2024, the Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta rejected the claim filed by the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños regarding the SQM Salar SpA compliance program in its entirety, and SQM Salar SpA is currently implementing the compliance program. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Inferred] The rejection of the Consejo's claim by the specialized environmental court, following a multi-year procedural timeline, exemplifies the temporal asymmetry between the velocity of corporate extraction and the velocity of legal remedy.

### 2.2 Member Communities with Documented Claims

[Documented] The following Atacameño communities are identified in the workspace as members of the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños with documented legal or territorial claims related to lithium extraction:

- **Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Toconao:** An Atacameño community located near the Salar de Atacama that has filed water and territorial claims against extraction expansion. `Source: evidence/actor-network.md, The Substrate Collective, 2026-06-07.`
- **Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Socaire:** A highland Atacameño community with ancestral water rights claims affected by upstream extraction. `Source: evidence/actor-network.md, The Substrate Collective, 2026-06-07.`
- **Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Peine:** An Atacameño community asserting territorial and hydrological rights against extraction expansion. `Source: evidence/actor-network.md, The Substrate Collective, 2026-06-07.`

[Inferred] These three named communities represent only a fraction of the 18 communities coordinated by the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños. The remaining 15 communities are not individually identified in the workspace as of 7 June 2026, and their specific territorial relationships, water use practices, and experiences of extraction are not documented here. [Inferred] The differential visibility of Toconao, Socaire, and Peine in regulatory and corporate documents likely reflects their proximity to extraction infrastructure and their participation in formal legal processes, not their relative importance or the severity of impacts they experience.

### 2.3 The Atacameños Indigenous Organization

[Documented] The Atacameños Indigenous Organization is identified in SQM corporate disclosures as a distinct entity from the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] On 15 December 2023, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. signed an agreement with Corporación Nacional del Cobre de Chile and the Atacameños Indigenous Organization to include the Atacameños Indigenous Organization in discussions regarding extending lithium extraction in the Salar de Atacama beyond 2030. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Inferred] The existence of a separate organizational entity signing agreements with SQM and Codelco, alongside the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños which has filed legal claims against SQM, indicates internal differentiation within Atacameño political organization that is flattened when "the community" is treated as a homogeneous category.

### 2.4 The January 2024 Blockade and the "Splinter Group"

[Documented] In January 2024, a disagreement within the Atacameños Peoples Council led to a blockade of the main roads to SQM's Salar de Atacama facilities for four days by a group that SQM describes as a "splinter group." `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, risk factor disclosures.` [Documented] The blockading group expressed dissent towards the non-binding Memorandum of Understanding signed by SQM with Codelco for the operation and development of lithium extraction in the Salar de Atacama from 2025 to 2060. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] The blockade resulted in a shutdown of operations at SQM's Salar de Atacama facilities for one day. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.`

[Inferred] SQM's framing of the blockading community members as a "splinter group" functions as a corporate rhetorical strategy that delegitimizes dissent by portraying it as factional and non-representative, while the agreement-signers are positioned as legitimate interlocutors. [Inferred] The blockade itself constitutes a community-documented pattern of resistance that diverges sharply from SQM's official narrative of "open, constructive dialogues with the local communities, primarily via roundtable discussions." `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.`

## 3. The Lived Experience of Opacity

### 3.1 What It Means to Be Subject to Decisions You Cannot Interrogate

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. holds water use rights obtained from the Dirección General de Aguas for supply of water from rivers and wells near its production facilities in the Salar de Atacama, and the company does not disclose well-by-well or aquifer-zone-specific extraction rates in its securities filings. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024; capital-topology synthesis, The Substrate Collective, 2026-06-07.` [Documented] The Dirección General de Aguas does not publish well-by-well extraction data at the resolution required for independent hydrological modeling. `Source: synthesis/capital-topology.md, The Substrate Collective, 2026-06-07.`

[Inferred] For Atacameño communities living in the Salar de Atacama basin, the lived experience of opacity includes the impossibility of knowing how much brine and freshwater is being pumped from each well, how those pumping rates correlate with observed declines in wetland water levels, and whether the hydrological models used by SQM and approved by the Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental accurately represent the aquifer dynamics on which their pastures, ceremonial sites, and drinking water depend. [Inferred] The opacity is not merely a lack of information; it is an active architecture. Corporate environmental impact assessments rely on corporate-generated baseline data. Regulatory monitoring networks are designed at spatial scales that may render community-observed wetland losses administratively invisible. Tribunal processes require communities to mobilize scarce technical and legal resources to interrogate data that they cannot independently generate.

### 3.2 Temporal Opacity: Harm That Outpaces Accountability

[Documented] The Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta was established under Ley 20.600 in 2010 to hear challenges to environmental permits and damage claims. `Source: Ley 20.600, Ministerio del Medio Ambiente, República de Chile, 2010.` [Documented] The Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta declined to revoke SQM extraction permits in cases brought by Atacameño communities during the 2010s, ordering limited monitoring measures while permitting continued pumping. `Source: synthesis/technical-reconstruction.md, The Substrate Collective, 2026-06-07.` [Documented] The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños claim regarding the SQM Salar SpA compliance program, filed after the program was approved on 29 August 2022, was rejected in its entirety on 11 June 2024. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.`

[Inferred] The temporal structure of this harm — measured in receding water tables and drying vegetation over years — does not fit the evidentiary requirements of environmental tribunals, which are designed to adjudicate discrete permit violations or contamination events on timelines of two to five years. [Inferred] For an Atacameño farmer observing the gradual desiccation of a vega that has watered llama herds for generations, the tribunal's multi-year process produces a form of temporal violence: by the time a ruling is issued, the ecological threshold may have already been crossed, and the ruling itself authorizes continued pumping subject to "monitoring" that the community cannot independently verify.

### 3.3 Epistemic Opacity: When Corporate Data Replaces Ecological Reality

[Documented] In October 2020, SQM announced its Sustainable Development Plan, which includes voluntarily expanding monitoring systems, promoting better and more profound conversations with neighboring communities, becoming carbon neutral by 2040, and reducing water consumption by 65 percent and brine extraction by 50 percent of authorized limits. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] In 2024, SQM completed the Carbon Disclosure Project water assessment with a B rating and the climate assessment with a B minus rating. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] In September 2023, the lithium mining operations of SQM in the Salar de Atacama achieved a score of 75 in the Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance standard, which SQM states is the highest score ever awarded to a lithium company worldwide. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.`

[Inferred] From the perspective of affected communities, these ratings and certifications constitute a form of epistemic capture. The same corporation that pumps brine from beneath the salt flat also generates the data that certifies its own responsibility. The community's embodied knowledge — the observation that a spring has weakened, that a flamingo nesting site has been abandoned, that a pasture has turned to dust — is not admissible as evidence in the same register as SQM's Carbon Disclosure Project rating. [Inferred] The lived experience of opacity here is the experience of watching your territory be certified as sustainable by actors you cannot interrogate, using methodologies you did not approve, while your own observations are categorized as anecdotal or unscientific.

### 3.4 Linguistic and Ontological Opacity

[Documented] The Substrate Collective acknowledges that it does not speak Kunza (Ckunza), the ancestral Atacameño language, and that its Spanish-language capacity, while functional, may miss legal and cosmological nuance. `Source: dossiers/atacama-lithium/scope-statement.md, The Substrate Collective, 2026-06-07.` [Documented] The Climate Knowledge Base backend pipeline processes documents using English-language spaCy named entity recognition models and sentence-transformer embedding models trained predominantly on English-language corpora. `Source: synthesis/technical-reconstruction.md, The Substrate Collective, 2026-06-07.`

[Inferred] For Atacameño communities, the opacity architecture extends into the very infrastructure of knowledge production. When community submissions to environmental tribunals are written in Spanish but filtered through English-language natural language processing pipelines, when Kunza concepts of territoriality have no equivalent in hydrological modeling software, and when indigenous temporal frameworks — seasonal cycles, ceremonial calendars, intergenerational time — are entirely outside the extraction ontology of corporate reporting and regulatory audit, the communities experience a form of ontological opacity. Their ways of knowing the territory are rendered unnameable or uncountable by the systems that govern it.

## 4. Divergence Mapping: Community-Documented Patterns vs. Official System Logic

### 4.1 Water as Relational Substrate vs. Water as Commodity

[Documented] The 1981 Water Code transformed water rights into privately tradable commodities independent of land ownership. `Source: Decreto Ley 1.122, Ministerio de Justicia, República de Chile, 1981.` [Documented] SQM classifies water rights as intangible assets on its balance sheet, separating them into finite rights amortized on a straight-line basis and indefinite rights that are not amortized because they are granted in perpetuity subject to annual impairment assessment. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, financial note disclosures.`

[Inferred] Community-documented patterns indicate that Atacameño communities understand water not as a quantifiable commodity or balance-sheet intangible but as a social, spiritual, and territorial relation. The divergence is not a disagreement over facts; it is an ontological conflict over what water is. [Inferred] The official system logic cannot register this divergence because its categories — concession, extraction rate, cubic meter, impairment assessment — were designed to render water legible as property. The community's relational understanding is produced as unnameable by the very architecture of the Water Code.

### 4.2 Cumulative Seasonal Harm vs. Discrete Violation Adjudication

[Documented] The Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente filed charges against SQM Salar SpA in November 2016 for extraction of brine beyond the authorized amount, progressive damage to the vitality of algarrobo trees, incomplete information delivery, and modification of monitoring plan variables, among other alleged violations. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] On 14 April 2020, the General Water Directorate of the Dirección General de Aguas in Antofagasta imposed a fine of 4,180 monthly tax units on SQM Salar SpA for alleged violations of Article 294 of the Water Code. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] In May 2024, the General Water Directorate initiated a sanctioning procedure against SQM Salar SpA for alleged violations of Article 294 of the Water Code at the solar evaporation ponds, and on 15 October 2024, imposed a fine of 1,285 monthly tax units for alleged violations of Articles 5 and 6 of DGA Resolution No. 1,238 regarding monitoring and reporting. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.`

[Inferred] While the regulatory system documents discrete violations and imposes administrative fines, community-documented patterns — inferred from ethnographic literature and SQM's own disclosure of "community concerns over management of increased traffic, environmental impacts and resource depletion" — suggest that communities experience harm as cumulative, systemic, and continuous. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024, risk factor disclosures.` [Inferred] The divergence between official violation logic and community harm logic means that even when SQM is fined, the underlying pumping continues, and the community's experience of loss is not addressed by the administrative penalty.

### 4.3 Community Dissent as Political Expression vs. Operational Risk Factor

[Documented] SQM discloses that "disputes with the local communities that live near the Salar de Atacama may in the future interfere with our operations and/or result in additional operating costs or restrictions." `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] SQM further discloses that "specific challenges in community relations include community concerns over management of increased traffic, environmental impacts and resource depletion, social, environmental and cultural heritage impacts, increasing expectations regarding the level of benefits that communities receive, benefits sharing with indigenous peoples' governments, concerns focused on the level of transparency regarding the payment of compensation and the provision of other benefits to affected landholders and the wider community." `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.`

[Inferred] The community-documented pattern is one of political struggle for territorial sovereignty and hydrological justice. The official system logic, as expressed in SQM's risk-factor disclosures to the United States Securities and Exchange Commission, translates this political struggle into financial risk: "opposition by indigenous communities to our activities may require modifications, disrupt or preclude our operations." `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Inferred] The blockade of January 2024 — a four-day road closure expressing dissent against a memorandum that would extend extraction to 2060 — is documented by SQM not as a political claim but as an operational disruption caused by a "splinter group." This divergence reveals how corporate disclosure regimes absorb community resistance into investor-risk calculus, stripping it of its political content and territorial meaning.

### 4.4 Performed Transparency vs. Unanswerable Questions

[Documented] SQM states that it is "dedicated to maintaining open, constructive dialogues with the local communities, primarily via roundtable discussions." `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.` [Documented] SQM's lease agreement with Corporación de Fomento de la Producción includes a commitment to invest between US$10 million and US$15 million annually in sustainable development projects for the Atacama La Grande indigenous communities through organizations promoting local development. `Source: SQM S.A., SEC Form 20-F for the year ended December 31, 2024.`

[Inferred] Community-documented patterns — inferred from SQM's own disclosure of community concerns over "transparency regarding the payment of compensation" — suggest that the roundtable discussions and sustainable development projects function as technologies of performed transparency rather than genuine accountability mechanisms. [Inferred] The community cannot interrogate the baseline hydrological data that underpins SQM's environmental impact assessments. The community cannot access the well-by-well pumping records held by the Dirección General de Aguas. The community cannot review the terms of the non-binding Memorandum of Understanding signed between SQM, Codelco, and the Atacameños Indigenous Organization. The roundtable offers dialogue without decisional power; the sustainable development fund offers monetary compensation without restitution of hydrological sovereignty.

## 5. The Opacity Architecture from Below

[Documented] The opacity architecture of the Atacama lithium assemblage is documented in the scope statement and technical reconstruction as the systematic design of legal, technical, and discursive structures that renders extraction systematically illegible. `Source: dossiers/atacama-lithium/scope-statement.md; dossiers/atacama-lithium/synthesis/technical-reconstruction.md.` [Inferred] From the community perspective, this architecture is experienced not as an abstract system but as a series of daily, practical, and affective impossibilities:

- **The impossibility of knowing:** Communities observe declining water levels in vegas and springs but cannot access the well-specific extraction data that would establish causal attribution. `Source: SQM 20-F disclosures; DGA data access gaps documented in capital-topology synthesis.`
- **The impossibility of timing:** Communities must file legal claims within administrative windows that do not correspond to hydrological seasons or ecological thresholds. `Source: Tribunal Ambiental procedural timelines documented in technical-reconstruction synthesis.`
- **The impossibility of naming:** Communities who understand water as a living relation must translate their claims into the language of property, impact zones, and cubic meters to be heard by tribunals. `Source: scope-statement.md, ontological erasure concept.`
- **The impossibility of refusal:** Communities who reject the logic of extraction are framed as operational risks or splinter groups; the legal architecture does not provide a mechanism for territorial refusal. `Source: SQM 20-F risk factor disclosures; blockade framing.`
- **The impossibility of reaching the consumer:** Communities in the Salar de Atacama cannot trace their dispossession into the Apple Inc. or Tesla Inc. devices that contain their water, because supply chain disclosures render the extraction site administratively invisible. `Source: supply chain opacity documented in capital-topology and material-footprint syntheses.`

## 6. Pattern Summary: What Communities Know That the System Does Not

[Inferred] Despite the opacity architecture, community-documented patterns — pieced together from corporate risk disclosures, tribunal references, and secondary ethnographic literature — suggest knowledge that diverges from official system logic:

1. **Baseline erasure:** Communities hold intergenerational memory of pre-extraction hydrological conditions that are absent from corporate baseline studies. The 1990s expansion of lithium extraction occurred without comprehensive pre-extraction baseline data, and community oral history fills this gap — but is inadmissible in tribunal proceedings. `Source: GAP-005 in scope-statement.md; inferred from general ethnographic literature.`

2. **Feedback loops:** Communities observe that corporate "sustainable development projects" may increase community dependency on the corporation, creating a structural conflict of interest that discourages continued opposition. The US$10 million to US$15 million annual community investment is a condition of the Corfo lease that SQM must fulfill; it functions simultaneously as compensation and as a mechanism of capture. `Source: SQM 20-F lease payment disclosures; inferred from general political economy of mining-community relations.`

3. **Internal differentiation:** The January 2024 blockade reveals that "the Atacameño community" is not a unitary actor. The divergence between the Atacameños Indigenous Organization (signatory to the December 15, 2023 agreement) and the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños (which filed the rejected tribunal claim) suggests competing strategies of engagement and refusal. SQM's framing of the blockaders as a "splinter group" obscures this political complexity. `Source: SQM 20-F disclosures; inferred from organizational mapping.`

4. **Hydrological interconnectivity:** Communities understand that the Salar de Atacama aquifer system is interconnected with wetlands, rivers, and pastures across a broader territorial scale than the Áreas Autorizadas para la Extracción designated in RCA No. 226/2006. Corporate monitoring is calibrated to the concession boundaries; community observation operates at the watershed scale. `Source: RCA No. 226/2006 documented in material-footprint synthesis; inferred from general hydrogeological and ethnographic literature.`

## 8. Recommendations for Reciprocal Engagement

[Inferred] The following recommendations are offered not as prescriptive directives but as provisional orientations for a future phase of investigation that would prioritize relationship over extraction:

1. **Establish contact through the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños** as the recognized coordinating body, while respecting the autonomy of individual member communities and acknowledging the existence of other organizational forms such as the Atacameños Indigenous Organization.

2. **Design consent protocols** in Spanish and, where possible, with Kunza translation, that explicitly address: the purpose of testimony gathering; how recordings and transcripts will be stored; community rights to withdraw, edit, or veto specific passages; and how findings will be returned to the community in usable formats.

3. **Return findings in advocacy-ready formats** — including Spanish-language summaries, timeline visualizations of tribunal delays, and maps of supply chain opacity — before publication in English-language academic venues.

4. **Compensate community time and expertise** as a structural requirement, not a courtesy, recognizing that engaging with European researchers consumes time that could be spent on organizing, herding, or ceremony.

5. **Support community data sovereignty** by contributing to community-controlled hydrological monitoring, legal defense funds, or independent technical expertise, rather than merely extracting testimony for the academic record.

6. **Acknowledge and publish the internal differentiation** within Atacameño communities — between signatories and blockaders, between elders and youth, between those who work for SQM and those who oppose it — without using such differentiation to delegitimize collective claims.
