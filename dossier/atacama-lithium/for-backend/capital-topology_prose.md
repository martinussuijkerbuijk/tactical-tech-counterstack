# Capital Topology of the Atacama Lithium Extraction Assemblage

Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. (SQM) is a publicly traded corporation legally incorporated in the Republic of Chile and listed on both the Santiago Stock Exchange and the New York Stock Exchange. [Documented] As of December 31, 2024, SQM had 1,115 shareholders, and its largest disclosed shareholder was Inversiones TLC SpA, which held 21.90 percent of outstanding shares. [Documented] Sociedad de Inversiones Pampa Calichera S.A. held 15.23 percent of outstanding shares as of December 31, 2024. [Documented] The Bank of New York Mellon, acting as depositary for American Depositary Receipts, held 14.91 percent of outstanding shares as of December 31, 2024. [Documented] Potasios de Chile S.A. held 6.36 percent of outstanding shares as of December 31, 2024. [Documented] Banco de Chile, holding shares on behalf of State Street Corporation, held 3.92 percent of outstanding shares as of December 31, 2024. [Documented] AFP Habitat S.A., a Chilean pension fund administrator, held 3.69 percent of outstanding shares as of December 31, 2024. [Documented] Global Mining Spa held 3.08 percent of outstanding shares as of December 31, 2024. [Documented] Banco Santander, holding shares on behalf of foreign investors, held 2.73 percent of outstanding shares as of December 31, 2024. [Documented] Tianqi Lithium Corporation, a Chinese lithium processor, held approximately 22.16 percent of SQM's shares as of March 31, 2025. [Documented]

[Inferred] The overlap between the 21.90 percent holding reported for Inversiones TLC SpA and the 22.16 percent holding reported for Tianqi Lithium Corporation suggests either fragmented nominee arrangements, a subsequent acquisition not yet reflected in year-end filings, or overlapping beneficial interests that obscure ultimate control. [Inferred] The presence of Chilean pension fund administrators AFP Habitat S.A. and AFP Provida S.A. among major shareholders implicates workers' retirement capital in the financial returns of brine extraction, creating a structural alignment between pension beneficiaries and extraction intensity that is not disclosed to individual account holders.

Tianqi Lithium Corporation is simultaneously a competitor in the global lithium chemicals market and a major shareholder of SQM, creating a dual position that enables governance influence without operational liability for aquifer depletion. [Documented] Under a 2018 agreement with the Fiscalía Nacional Económica of Chile, Tianqi Lithium Corporation agreed not to appoint directors to lithium-related decision-making bodies of SQM unless nominated by independent directors, and to inform the Fiscalía Nacional Económica of any lithium market agreements with Albemarle Corporation and/or SQM prior to execution. [Documented] SQM estimates that Tianqi Lithium Corporation holds approximately 6 percent of the global lithium chemicals market share, compared to SQM's estimated 17 percent and Albemarle Corporation's estimated 14 percent, as of 2024. [Documented]

SQM Salar SpA, a wholly owned subsidiary of SQM, holds exclusive leasehold rights to exploit mineral resources in the Salar de Atacama until December 31, 2030, pursuant to the SQM-Corfo Agreements with Corporación de Fomento de la Producción (Corfo), a Chilean state development agency. [Documented] The SQM-Corfo Agreements consist of a lease agreement over mining exploitation concessions and the Salar de Atacama project agreement, both as amended from time to time. [Documented] Under the SQM-Corfo Agreements, SQM Salar must make quarterly lease payments to Corfo based on product sales from leased mining properties, contribute US$18.9 million per year to research and development efforts, contribute between US$10 million and US$15 million per year to communities near the Salar de Atacama, and contribute 1.7 percent of total annual sales of SQM Salar to the Antofagasta Regional Government and the municipalities of San Pedro de Atacama, María Elena, and Antofagasta for regional development. [Documented]

The lease payment rate to Corfo is structured as a progressive percentage of the sales price of lithium carbonate equivalent. [Documented] For sales prices between US$0 and US$4,000 per metric ton of lithium carbonate, the lease payment rate is 6.8 percent. [Documented] For sales prices between US$4,000 and US$5,000 per metric ton, the rate is 8.0 percent. [Documented] For sales prices between US$5,000 and US$6,000 per metric ton, the rate is 10.0 percent. [Documented] Expenses related to Corfo totaled US$397 million in 2024, US$1,868.9 million in 2023, and US$3,273.0 million in 2022. [Documented] In 2024, Corfo accounted for approximately 24 percent of the lithium business line's cost of sales. [Documented] In 2023, Corfo accounted for approximately 62 percent of the same business line's cost of sales. [Documented]

As of December 31, 2024, SQM had extracted approximately 52 percent of the total permitted accumulated extraction and sales limit of lithium under the SQM-Corfo Agreements, leaving approximately 48 percent of the authorized quota for the remaining six years until the December 31, 2030 expiration date. [Documented] As of December 31, 2023, SQM had extracted approximately 43 percent of the total permitted limit, with seven years remaining. [Documented]

The Comisión Chilena de Energía Nuclear (CCHEN), the Chilean Nuclear Energy Commission, imposes quotas that limit the total tonnage of lithium authorized to be sold from the Salar de Atacama, and SQM Salar holds an obligation to CCHEN that prohibits the use of lithium for nuclear fusion. [Documented] [Inferred] The involvement of a nuclear energy commission in battery mineral governance reveals a jurisdictional layering in which lithium is simultaneously classified as a civilian energy transition input and a strategic material subject to national security restrictions, producing divergent accountability regimes that do not address hydrological impact.

SQM reported total consolidated sales of US$4,528.8 million in 2024, US$7,476.6 million in 2023, and US$10,710.6 million in 2022. [Documented] Revenues from lithium and its derivatives totaled US$2,241.3 million in 2024, representing 49.5 percent of total revenues. [Documented] Revenues from lithium and its derivatives totaled US$5,180.1 million in 2023, representing 69.4 percent of total revenues. [Documented] Revenues from lithium and its derivatives totaled US$8,152.9 million in 2022. [Documented] Revenues related to products originating from the Salar de Atacama represented 55 percent of consolidated revenues in 2024 and 73 percent of consolidated revenues in 2023. [Documented] SQM's lithium sales volumes were approximately 204.9 thousand metric tons in 2024, a 20.5 percent increase over 2023, but average prices were approximately 64.1 percent lower than in 2023. [Documented]

The Chilean Internal Revenue Service (Servicio de Impuestos Internos, SII) has sought to extend the specific tax on mining activities to lithium mining. [Documented] As of December 31, 2023, SQM had paid a total of US$986.3 million for specific tax on mining activities applied to lithium, covering tax years 2012 through 2023. [Documented] This amount included US$59.5 million in over-assessed amounts, US$818.0 million in disputed taxes net of corporate income tax impact, and US$108.8 million in interest. [Documented] SQM Salar has filed seven tax claims against the Servicio de Impuestos Internos. [Documented]

On May 31, 2024, SQM and Corporación Nacional del Cobre de Chile (Codelco), the Chilean state-owned copper mining company, entered into a partnership agreement to form a new entity for lithium, potassium, and other mineral production from Corfo properties in the Salar de Atacama. [Documented] The entity is to be formed through the merger by incorporation of Codelco's subsidiary, Minera Tarar SpA, into SQM Salar. [Documented] Minera Tarar will obtain mineral exploitation rights for the period January 1, 2031, through December 31, 2060, pursuant to lease and project agreements to be entered into with Corfo. [Documented] Conditions precedent include completion of consultation with indigenous communities in the Salar de Atacama, termination of the United States Securities and Exchange Commission investigation into SQM on specified terms, execution of the Tarar-Corfo Agreements, and transfer of SQM lithium business assets not currently owned by SQM Salar. [Documented]

[Inferred] The Codelco-SQM partnership extends the state-corporate condominium by thirty years while introducing a state-owned enterprise as direct partner, potentially increasing the Chilean state's extraction rent but diluting SQM's exclusive control without restoring indigenous hydrological sovereignty. [Inferred] The indigenous community consultation requirement functions as a procedural condition that may be undermined by temporal asymmetry between consultation timelines and the corporate urgency to secure post-2030 extraction rights.

SQM classifies water rights as intangible assets on its balance sheet, recording them at acquisition cost and separating them into finite rights amortized on a straight-line basis and indefinite rights that are not amortized because they are granted in perpetuity subject to annual impairment assessment. [Documented] SQM holds water use rights obtained from the Dirección General de Aguas for supply of water from rivers and wells near its production facilities. [Documented] Water for the Nueva Victoria and Salar de Atacama facilities is obtained from wells near the production facilities. [Documented] For its lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide production at the Lithium Chemical Plant, in 2024 SQM recovered approximately 1,060,000 cubic meters of ultrapure water from liquid plant waste, with additional water purchased from third parties and local utility companies. [Documented]

In January 2022, the Chilean Congress approved a bill amending the Chilean Water Code, which was published on April 6, 2022, becoming Law 21.435. [Documented] The amendment establishes that water rights will have a temporary nature with a maximum grant period of 30 years, will be subject to expiration for non-use, must give priority to human consumption and sanitation, must maintain minimum ecological flows as determined by the Dirección General de Aguas, and must be registered in the Real Estate Registry and the Public Water Cadaster of the Dirección General de Aguas. [Documented] [Inferred] SQM's investment in a seawater pipeline with capacity of 900 liters per second, projected to enter operation by the end of 2026, functions as an infrastructure hedge against future groundwater concession restrictions under the amended Water Code.

SQM S.A. is legally incorporated in the Republic of Chile and contractually governed by Chilean law under the SQM-Corfo Agreements. [Documented] SQM is materially present in the Salar de Atacama through evaporation ponds, brine pumping wells, and lithium processing facilities in the Antofagasta Region. [Documented] Operational decisions are made from corporate offices and are subject to oversight by Corfo, the Dirección General de Aguas, the Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental, and the Comisión Chilena de Energía Nuclear. [Documented] [Inferred] The separation between legal incorporation in Santiago and New York, contractual governance by Chilean public law, material presence in the Atacama Desert, and decisional presence distributed across multiple state agencies creates a jurisdictional fragmentation in which no single locus of accountability exists for cumulative aquifer depletion.

Apple Inc., headquartered in Cupertino, California, United States, publishes an annual Supplier List and Supplier Responsibility Progress Reports. [Documented] Tesla Inc., headquartered in Austin, Texas, United States, publishes annual Impact Reports and conflict minerals disclosures. [Gap — no primary source located] Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. public disclosures available in the workspace do not name SQM as a direct or indirect supplier of lithium from the Salar de Atacama. [Inferred] The absence of SQM from downstream supply chain disclosures does not demonstrate absence of commercial relationship; it demonstrates that lithium passes through multiple processing tiers, and downstream brands disclose only direct suppliers or conflict minerals smelters, rendering the extraction site administratively invisible to consumers and investors.

[Inferred] The oversight architecture governing SQM extraction contains multiple accountability waivers: Corfo derives revenue from SQM sales, creating a financial incentive not to restrict extraction; the Dirección General de Aguas does not publish well-by-well extraction data at the resolution required for independent hydrological modeling; the Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental relies on corporate-generated baseline data for environmental impact assessments; the Comisión Chilena de Energía Nuclear regulates lithium as a strategic material rather than as a water-intensive extractive activity; and the Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta operates on multi-year timelines that are structurally mismatched to seasonal hydrological change. [Inferred] These design features collectively ensure that what is not measured cannot be regulated, and what is not published cannot be litigated, maintaining the Salar de Atacama below the threshold of detectability for enforceable accountability.
