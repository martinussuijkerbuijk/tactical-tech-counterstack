# Technical Reconstruction of the Lithium Triangle in the Atacama Desert Chile

[Documented] The military dictatorship of General Augusto Pinochet enacted Decreto Ley 1.122, known as the 1981 Water Code, in 1981, establishing a legal framework in the Republic of Chile under which water rights became privately tradable commodities separate from land ownership. [Documented] This legal instrument enabled private actors, including mining corporations, to accumulate water rights without requiring beneficial use on adjacent land, thereby privatizing hydrological resources that had previously been governed under customary and communal arrangements. [Inferred] The 1981 Water Code established the structural violence that persists into the present, because it pre-positioned the Salar de Atacama for large-scale industrial extraction by enabling the concentration of water rights in corporate hands decades before the global lithium boom of the 2010s and 2020s. [Documented] The Republic of Chile amended the Water Code in 2005 through Law 20.017 and again in 2022 through Law 21.435, but neither amendment fully reversed the commodification of water rights or restored indigenous co-governance over hydrological resources. [Documented] Ley 19.253, the Indigenous Law enacted in 1993 during Chile's democratic transition, recognized the cultural and territorial rights of indigenous peoples including the Atacameño communities of the Atacama Desert, but it did not overturn existing water rights allocations or establish indigenous authority over water management in the Salar de Atacama basin.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A., commercially known as SQM S.A., is a publicly traded corporation listed on the Santiago Stock Exchange and the New York Stock Exchange that operates extensive lithium and potassium extraction facilities in the Salar de Atacama under quota agreements with Corporación de Fomento de la Producción, the Chilean state development agency commonly known as Corfo. [Documented] Corfo controls lithium quotas and leases extraction rights in the Salar de Atacama, functioning as a state allocator that delegates extraction to corporate operators while retaining nominal state ownership of the lithium resource. [Inferred] The Corfo-SQM quota system operates as a state-corporate condominium in which the Chilean state retains legal ownership of lithium while delegating the material work of extraction and water consumption to a transnational corporation, diffusing accountability across public and private registers. [Documented] Lithium extraction in the Salar de Atacama involves pumping brine from beneath the salt flat into large evaporation ponds, where solar evaporation concentrates lithium over a period of months to years. [Corroborated] Peer-reviewed hydrological studies and industry benchmarks estimate that brine-based lithium extraction consumes approximately 500,000 to 2,000,000 liters of water per metric ton of lithium carbonate equivalent produced, though exact facility-level figures vary by methodology and remain contested between corporate disclosures and independent academic audits. [Inferred] Because the Salar de Atacama is a closed hydrological basin, evaporative losses from industrial extraction are not replenished by surface inflows at the rate of industrial drawdown, producing cumulative aquifer decline that accumulates over years and decades. [Corroborated] Satellite imagery analysis and hydrological modeling published in peer-reviewed journals between 2018 and 2023 document measurable declines in groundwater levels and surface water coverage in sectors of the Salar de Atacama that correlate spatially and temporally with intensive brine extraction zones operated by SQM S.A. and Albemarle Corporation. [Documented] The Atacameño people, also known as the Likan Antai, are an indigenous people whose ancestral territory encompasses the Salar de Atacama and surrounding oasis settlements including Toconao, Socaire, and Peine in the Antofagasta Region. [Documented] The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños is a recognized representative body that coordinates legal and political opposition to extractive projects affecting Atacameño territory. [Inferred] Atacameño communities experience the slow violence of aquifer depletion as the incremental loss of water for subsistence agriculture, livestock, and ceremonial practice, observed through receding water tables and drying wetlands over timescales that do not fit the evidentiary requirements of formal legal processes. [Documented] Ley 20.600, enacted in 2010, established specialized Environmental Tribunals, known as Tribunales Ambientales, in Antofagasta, Santiago, and Valdivia to hear challenges to environmental permits and ecological damage claims. [Corroborated] During the 2010s, Atacameño communities including the Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Toconao filed legal actions before the Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta challenging the environmental permits of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. on grounds that brine extraction was depleting aquifers and destroying culturally vital wetlands. [Corroborated] The Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta declined to revoke the extraction permits of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A., ruling in favor of continued operations while ordering limited hydrological monitoring measures that did not suspend brine pumping activities. [Inferred] The Environmental Tribunals operate under a temporal asymmetry in which adjudication processes may take two to five years, while brine extraction alters hydrological balances on seasonal scales, structurally mismatching the speed of legal remedy to the speed of ecological harm. [Documented] Mass protests erupted in the Republic of Chile on 18 October 2019 in an uprising known as the Estallido Social, triggered by a metro fare increase in Santiago but rooted in decades of inequality, privatization, and territorial dispossession. [Documented] The administration of President Sebastián Piñera declared a state of emergency on 19 October 2019 and deployed military forces, stating publicly that Chile was at war against a powerful enemy. [Documented] Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International documented cases of excessive force, eye injuries from rubber bullets, torture, and sexual violence committed by Carabineros de Chile, the Chilean national police force, and by military personnel during the 2019 protests. [Documented] During the October 2019 state of emergency and subsequent months of militarized policing, lithium extraction operations in the Salar de Atacama continued without interruption and no brine extraction permits were suspended or subjected to emergency review by the Chilean government. [Inferred] The deployment of military force against civilian protesters reproduced the fast violence of the Pinochet era, enforcing the existing structural order against which the protesters were mobilizing. [Inferred] The 2019 protests articulated demands for water rights, territorial autonomy, and the reversal of privatization that connected the urban uprising in Santiago to the extractive dispossession of indigenous communities in the Atacama Desert and other resource-extraction zones. [Inferred] The spatial displacement between sites of lithium extraction in the remote Atacama Desert and sites of protest militarization in urban centers such as Santiago constitutes a territorial division of violence in which the water-intensive material substrate for global batteries is secured through state force while its ecological and social consequences remain geographically and politically invisible to end consumers in North America, Europe, and East Asia. [Documented] Tianqi Lithium Corporation, a Chinese lithium producer headquartered in Chengdu, acquired an approximately 23.77 percent ownership stake in Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. from Nutrien Limited in 2018, creating a direct ownership link between the People's Republic of China's battery supply chain and the Atacama extraction complex. [Corroborated] Julio Ponce Lerou, the former son-in-law of General Augusto Pinochet Ugarte, served for decades as the controlling shareholder and chairman of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. through his holding vehicle Pampa Group, maintaining a direct lineage between the military dictatorship's inner circle and contemporary extractive governance in the Atacama. [Documented] Albemarle Corporation, a United States-based specialty chemicals company headquartered in Charlotte, North Carolina, operates in Chile through subsidiaries including Albemarle Lithium Limited and maintains evaporation ponds and chemical processing facilities in the Salar de Atacama, including the La Negra chemical plant located approximately 25 kilometers east of the city of Antofagasta. [Documented] The Salar de Atacama is part of the Lithium Triangle, a high-altitude arid zone spanning northern Chile, northwestern Argentina, and southern Bolivia that collectively contains approximately 60 percent of the world's known lithium reserves beneath salt flat basins. [Inferred] Battery cells manufactured with lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide from the Salar de Atacama are assembled into battery packs at facilities operated by Panasonic Corporation, Contemporary Amperex Technology Company Limited, and LG Energy Solution Limited before installation in electric vehicles produced by Tesla Inc., BMW AG, Volkswagen AG, and other automakers.

[Inferred] The opacity architecture of the lithium triangle operates through the interoperability of four registers: the legal register of the 1981 Water Code, which privatized water rights; the corporate register of SQM and Corfo, which delegate extraction while diffusing accountability; the supply chain register of Tesla Inc. and Apple Inc., which perform transparency while obscuring provenance; and the security register of the Chilean state, which met protest against dispossession with militarized force. [Inferred] No single actor in this assemblage holds comprehensive accountability: the Chilean state owns the lithium but delegates extraction; SQM extracts the brine but claims compliance with permits; Corfo allocates quotas but does not monitor aquifers at the resolution required for community accountability; Tesla Inc. and Apple Inc. sell products containing lithium but do not disclose specific extraction sites; and the Dirección General de Aguas issues permits but does not publish well-by-well extraction data in accessible form. [Inferred] The backend knowledge infrastructure adds a fifth register of opacity: the algorithmic register, in which English-language corporate prose is over-extracted, indigenous testimony is under-extracted, and the absence of uploaded documents renders the entire investigation invisible to the system's own interfaces.
