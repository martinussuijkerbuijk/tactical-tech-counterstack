# Community Testimony Layer for the Atacama Lithium Investigation

The Substrate Collective produced this Community Testimony Layer on 7 June 2026 to map affected communities and document the lived experience of opacity under the lithium extraction assemblage in the Salar de Atacama, Antofagasta Region, Republic of Chile. [Documented] As of 7 June 2026, The Substrate Collective has not established consent-based relationships with any Atacameño community, organization, or individual, and no first-person oral history interviews, participatory mapping sessions, or structured interviews with Atacameño water guardians, community authorities, or residents have been conducted. [Documented] This layer is therefore a preliminary documentary scaffolding constructed from publicly available corporate disclosures, regulatory filings, tribunal rulings, and human rights reports, and it is explicitly not community testimony gathered through reciprocal partnership. [Documented]

The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños, also referred to as the Atacameño Peoples Council, is a recognized representative body that coordinates eighteen Atacameño indigenous communities in the Atacama region of Chile and advocates for the rights, traditions, and interests of the Atacameño people, including land use, environmental protection, and economic development. [Documented] According to Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A., the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños filed a claim with the Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta regarding the Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente-approved environmental compliance program of SQM Salar SpA, the wholly owned lithium extraction subsidiary of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. [Documented] On 11 June 2024, the Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta rejected the claim filed by the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños regarding the SQM Salar SpA compliance program in its entirety, and SQM Salar SpA is currently implementing the compliance program. [Documented]

The Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Toconao is an Atacameño community located near the Salar de Atacama that has filed water and territorial claims against lithium extraction expansion. [Documented] The Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Socaire is a highland Atacameño community with ancestral water rights claims affected by upstream brine extraction. [Documented] The Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Peine is an Atacameño community asserting territorial and hydrological rights against extraction expansion. [Documented] These three named communities represent only a fraction of the eighteen communities coordinated by the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños, and the remaining fifteen communities are not individually identified in the workspace as of 7 June 2026. [Gap]

The Atacameños Indigenous Organization is identified in Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. corporate disclosures as a distinct entity from the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños. [Documented] On 15 December 2023, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. signed an agreement with Corporación Nacional del Cobre de Chile, the Chilean state-owned copper mining company known as Codelco, and the Atacameños Indigenous Organization to include the Atacameños Indigenous Organization in discussions regarding extending lithium extraction in the Salar de Atacama beyond 2030. [Documented] In January 2024, a disagreement within the Atacameños Peoples Council led to a blockade of the main roads to Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. Salar de Atacama facilities for four days by a group that the company describes as a splinter group. [Documented] The blockading group expressed dissent towards the non-binding Memorandum of Understanding signed by Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. with Codelco for the operation and development of lithium extraction in the Salar de Atacama from 2025 to 2060. [Documented] The blockade resulted in a shutdown of operations at the Salar de Atacama facilities for one day. [Documented]

[Inferred] The framing of the blockading community members as a splinter group in Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. risk factor disclosures functions as a corporate rhetorical strategy that delegitimizes dissent by portraying it as factional and non-representative. [Inferred] The blockade itself constitutes a community-documented pattern of resistance that diverges sharply from the corporate narrative of open, constructive dialogues with local communities via roundtable discussions. [Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. states that it is dedicated to maintaining open, constructive dialogues with local communities primarily via roundtable discussions. [Documented]

Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. holds water use rights obtained from the Dirección General de Aguas, the Chilean national water authority, for supply of water from rivers and wells near its production facilities in the Salar de Atacama. [Documented] The company does not disclose well-by-well or aquifer-zone-specific extraction rates in its United States Securities and Exchange Commission filings. [Documented] The Dirección General de Aguas does not publish well-by-well extraction data at the resolution required for independent hydrological modeling. [Inferred]

For Atacameño communities living in the Salar de Atacama basin, the lived experience of opacity includes the practical impossibility of knowing how much brine and freshwater is being pumped from each well, how those pumping rates correlate with observed declines in wetland water levels, and whether the hydrological models used by Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. and approved by the Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental accurately represent the aquifer dynamics on which their pastures, ceremonial sites, and drinking water depend. [Inferred] Corporate environmental impact assessments rely on corporate-generated baseline data. [Documented] Regulatory monitoring networks are designed at spatial scales that may render community-observed wetland losses administratively invisible. [Inferred] Tribunal processes require communities to mobilize scarce technical and legal resources to interrogate data that they cannot independently generate. [Inferred]

The Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta, a specialized environmental court established under Ley 20.600 in 2010, hears challenges to environmental permits and damage claims in northern Chile. [Documented] The tribunal declined to revoke Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. extraction permits in cases brought by Atacameño communities during the 2010s, ordering limited monitoring measures while permitting continued pumping. [Corroborated] The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños claim regarding the SQM Salar SpA compliance program, filed after the program was approved on 29 August 2022, was rejected in its entirety on 11 June 2024. [Documented]

For Atacameño communities, the temporal structure of harm measured in receding water tables and drying vegetation over years does not fit the evidentiary requirements of environmental tribunals, which are designed to adjudicate discrete permit violations or contamination events on timelines of two to five years. [Inferred] By the time a tribunal ruling is issued, the ecological threshold may have already been crossed, and the ruling itself authorizes continued pumping subject to monitoring that the community cannot independently verify. [Inferred]

In October 2020, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. announced its Sustainable Development Plan, which includes voluntarily expanding monitoring systems, promoting better and more profound conversations with neighboring communities, becoming carbon neutral by 2040, and reducing water consumption by 65 percent and brine extraction by 50 percent of authorized limits. [Documented] In 2024, the company completed the Carbon Disclosure Project water assessment with a B rating and the climate assessment with a B minus rating. [Documented] In September 2023, the lithium mining operations of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. in the Salar de Atacama achieved a score of 75 in the Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance standard, which the company states is the highest score ever awarded to a lithium company worldwide. [Documented]

From the perspective of affected communities, these ratings and certifications constitute a form of epistemic capture in which the same corporation that pumps brine from beneath the salt flat also generates the data that certifies its own responsibility. [Inferred] The community's embodied knowledge, including observations that a spring has weakened, that an Andean flamingo nesting site has been abandoned, or that a pasture has turned to dust, is not admissible as evidence in the same register as the Carbon Disclosure Project rating. [Inferred] The lived experience of opacity is the experience of watching territory be certified as sustainable by actors that cannot be interrogated, using methodologies that were not approved by the community, while community observations are categorized as anecdotal or unscientific. [Inferred]

The Substrate Collective acknowledges that it does not speak Kunza, also known as Ckunza, the ancestral Atacameño language, and that its Spanish-language capacity, while functional, may miss legal and cosmological nuance. [Documented] The Climate Knowledge Base backend pipeline processes documents using English-language spaCy named entity recognition models and sentence-transformer embedding models trained predominantly on English-language corpora. [Documented] For Atacameño communities, the opacity architecture extends into the very infrastructure of knowledge production, such that when community submissions to environmental tribunals are written in Spanish but filtered through English-language natural language processing pipelines, and when Kunza concepts of territoriality have no equivalent in hydrological modeling software, the communities experience a form of ontological opacity in which their ways of knowing the territory are rendered unnameable or uncountable by the systems that govern it. [Inferred]

The 1981 Water Code, enacted by the military dictatorship of General Augusto Pinochet Ugarte as Decreto Ley 1.122, transformed water rights into privately tradable commodities independent of land ownership in the Republic of Chile. [Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. classifies water rights as intangible assets on its balance sheet, separating them into finite rights amortized on a straight-line basis and indefinite rights that are not amortized because they are granted in perpetuity subject to annual impairment assessment. [Documented] Community-documented patterns indicate that Atacameño communities understand water not as a quantifiable commodity or balance-sheet intangible but as a social, spiritual, and territorial relation. [Inferred] The official system logic cannot register this divergence because its categories of concession, extraction rate, cubic meter, and impairment assessment were designed to render water legible as property, and the community's relational understanding is produced as unnameable by the very architecture of the Water Code. [Inferred]

The Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente filed charges against SQM Salar SpA in November 2016 for extraction of brine beyond the authorized amount, progressive damage to the vitality of algarrobo trees, incomplete information delivery, and modification of monitoring plan variables, among other alleged violations. [Documented] On 14 April 2020, the General Water Directorate of the Dirección General de Aguas in Antofagasta imposed a fine of 4,180 monthly tax units on SQM Salar SpA for alleged violations of Article 294 of the Water Code. [Documented] In May 2024, the General Water Directorate initiated a sanctioning procedure against SQM Salar SpA for alleged violations of Article 294 of the Water Code at the solar evaporation ponds, and on 15 October 2024, imposed a fine of 1,285 monthly tax units for alleged violations of Articles 5 and 6 of Dirección General de Aguas Resolution No. 1,238 regarding monitoring and reporting. [Documented]

While the regulatory system documents discrete violations and imposes administrative fines, community-documented patterns inferred from ethnographic literature and from Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. own disclosure of community concerns over management of increased traffic, environmental impacts, and resource depletion suggest that communities experience harm as cumulative, systemic, and continuous. [Inferred] The divergence between official violation logic and community harm logic means that even when Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. is fined, the underlying pumping continues, and the community's experience of loss is not addressed by the administrative penalty. [Inferred]

Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. discloses that disputes with local communities that live near the Salar de Atacama may in the future interfere with operations and result in additional operating costs or restrictions. [Documented] The company further discloses that specific challenges in community relations include community concerns over management of increased traffic, environmental impacts and resource depletion, social, environmental and cultural heritage impacts, increasing expectations regarding the level of benefits that communities receive, benefits sharing with indigenous peoples' governments, and concerns focused on the level of transparency regarding the payment of compensation and the provision of other benefits to affected landholders and the wider community. [Documented] The community-documented pattern is one of political struggle for territorial sovereignty and hydrological justice. [Inferred] The official system logic, as expressed in Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. risk factor disclosures to the United States Securities and Exchange Commission, translates this political struggle into financial risk by stating that opposition by indigenous communities to company activities may require modifications, disrupt or preclude operations. [Documented]

The blockade of January 2024, a four-day road closure expressing dissent against a memorandum that would extend extraction to 2060, is documented by Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. not as a political claim but as an operational disruption caused by a splinter group. [Documented] This divergence reveals how corporate disclosure regimes absorb community resistance into investor-risk calculus, stripping it of its political content and territorial meaning. [Inferred]

Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. states that its lease agreement with Corporación de Fomento de la Producción, the Chilean state development agency known as Corfo, includes a commitment to invest between 10 million and 15 million United States dollars annually in sustainable development projects for the Atacama La Grande indigenous communities through organizations promoting local development. [Documented] Community-documented patterns inferred from the company's own disclosure of community concerns over transparency regarding the payment of compensation suggest that the roundtable discussions and sustainable development projects function as technologies of performed transparency rather than genuine accountability mechanisms. [Inferred] The community cannot interrogate the baseline hydrological data that underpins Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. environmental impact assessments, cannot access the well-by-well pumping records held by the Dirección General de Aguas, and cannot review the terms of the non-binding Memorandum of Understanding signed between Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A., Codelco, and the Atacameños Indigenous Organization. [Inferred]

The opacity architecture of the Atacama lithium assemblage is experienced by communities as a series of daily, practical, and affective impossibilities. [Inferred] Communities observe declining water levels in vegas and springs but cannot access the well-specific extraction data that would establish causal attribution. [Inferred] Communities must file legal claims within administrative windows that do not correspond to hydrological seasons or ecological thresholds. [Inferred] Communities who understand water as a living relation must translate their claims into the language of property, impact zones, and cubic meters to be heard by tribunals. [Inferred] Communities who reject the logic of extraction are framed as operational risks or splinter groups, and the legal architecture does not provide a mechanism for territorial refusal. [Inferred] Communities in the Salar de Atacama cannot trace their dispossession into the Apple Inc. or Tesla Inc. devices that contain their water, because supply chain disclosures render the extraction site administratively invisible. [Inferred]

Despite the opacity architecture, community-documented patterns pieced together from corporate risk disclosures, tribunal references, and secondary ethnographic literature suggest knowledge that diverges from official system logic. [Inferred] Communities hold intergenerational memory of pre-extraction hydrological conditions that are absent from corporate baseline studies, and the 1990s expansion of lithium extraction occurred without comprehensive pre-extraction baseline data. [Inferred] Community oral history fills this gap but is inadmissible in tribunal proceedings. [Inferred] Corporate sustainable development projects may increase community dependency on the corporation, creating a structural conflict of interest that discourages continued opposition, and the 10 million to 15 million United States dollar annual community investment is a condition of the Corfo lease that functions simultaneously as compensation and as a mechanism of capture. [Inferred] The January 2024 blockade reveals that the Atacameño community is not a unitary actor, and the divergence between the Atacameños Indigenous Organization, which signed the 15 December 2023 agreement, and the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños, which filed the rejected tribunal claim, suggests competing strategies of engagement and refusal. [Inferred] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. framing of the blockaders as a splinter group obscures this political complexity. [Inferred] Communities understand that the Salar de Atacama aquifer system is interconnected with wetlands, rivers, and pastures across a broader territorial scale than the Áreas Autorizadas para la Extracción designated in Resolución de Calificación Ambiental RCA No. 226/2006, and corporate monitoring is calibrated to the concession boundaries while community observation operates at the watershed scale. [Inferred]

The Substrate Collective recommends that any future phase of investigation establish contact through the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños as the recognized coordinating body, while respecting the autonomy of individual member communities and acknowledging the existence of other organizational forms such as the Atacameños Indigenous Organization. [Inferred] Consent protocols should be designed in Spanish and, where possible, with Kunza translation, explicitly addressing the purpose of testimony gathering, how recordings and transcripts will be stored, community rights to withdraw or veto specific passages, and how findings will be returned to the community in usable formats before publication in English-language academic venues. [Inferred] Community time and expertise should be compensated as a structural requirement rather than a courtesy. [Inferred] Future engagement should support community data sovereignty by contributing to community-controlled hydrological monitoring, legal defense funds, or independent technical expertise rather than merely extracting testimony for the academic record. [Inferred] Any future investigation should acknowledge and publish the internal differentiation within Atacameño communities without using such differentiation to delegitimize collective claims. [Inferred]

The Substrate Collective does not claim ownership over any community testimony that may be gathered in future phases of this investigation. [Documented] This preliminary layer is a unilateral research product, not a reciprocal partnership output. [Documented] Communities retain ownership of their testimony, and any future phase that moves from documentation to action must establish consent-based relationships with the communities it would claim to serve. [Inferred] Until such relationships are established, this layer functions as a strategic orientation for accountability actors and as an explicit admission of what remains unknown. [Inferred]
