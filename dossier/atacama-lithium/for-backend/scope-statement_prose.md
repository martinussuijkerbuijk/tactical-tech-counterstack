# Scope Statement for the Atacama Lithium Investigation

This investigation examines the lithium extraction assemblage in the Salar de Atacama, Chile, across the period from 1981 to June 2026, focusing on financial and corporate governance structures, regulatory permits and environmental tribunal records, technical and ecological extraction systems, and supply chain disclosures linked to global technology corporations and indigenous community resistance. [Documented] The Substrate Collective scoped this investigation on 7 June 2026.

The analysis covers the period from 1981 to 7 June 2026, with particular attention to the post-dictatorship expansion of mineral concessions from 1990 to 2010, the integration of Chilean lithium into global battery supply chains from 2015 to 2026, and the 2019 Estallido Social and its aftermath. [Documented]

Primary actors include Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. (SQM), Tianqi Lithium Corporation, Nutrien Ltd., Corporación Nacional del Cobre de Chile (Codelco), Apple Inc., Tesla Inc., the Dirección General de Aguas of Chile (DGA), the Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental of Chile (SEA), the Tribunales Ambientales of Chile, the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños, and the Carabineros de Chile. [Documented]

This investigation is conducted by The Substrate Collective from a European university position with public funding, with access to corporate securities filings, open regulatory databases, academic literature, and investigative journalism, and with the limitations of lacking subpoena power, field presence in the Atacama Desert, and direct access to classified or proprietary supply chain contracts. [Documented]

This investigation defines green extractivism as the discursive and material framing of mineral extraction as a necessary and virtuous contribution to planetary energy transition, which simultaneously renders extraction zones as sacrificeable territories and their inhabitants as governable externalities. [Documented]

This investigation defines hydrological sovereignty as the capacity of a community to govern the water relations within its territory according to its own cosmological and practical understandings, free from subordination to state concession regimes or corporate property claims. [Documented]

This investigation defines slow violence as violence that occurs gradually and invisibly, distributed across time and space, such that its perpetrators, beneficiaries, and victims are separated by vast temporal and geographical distances, drawing on Rob Nixon's 2011 theoretical formulation. [Documented]

This investigation defines temporal asymmetry as the structural mismatch between the velocity at which a technical-economic system produces harm and the velocity at which regulatory, legal, or investigative accountability can respond. [Documented]

This investigation defines performed sustainability as the strategic production of environmental disclosures, carbon-neutrality claims, and responsible-sourcing reports that function to secure social license, satisfy investor environmental, social, and governance criteria, and preempt binding regulation while extraction intensifies. [Documented]

This investigation defines threshold of detectability in its hydrological sense as the calibration of monitoring wells, baseline studies, corporate environmental impact assessments, and regulatory audit protocols such that certain hydrological changes remain below the officially detectable threshold, rendering them administratively nonexistent. [Documented]

This investigation defines ontological erasure as the translation of relational territorial beings into categories legible to extractive governance, such that Atacameño communities, wetlands, and Andean flamingo nesting sites are rendered as brine reserves, evaporation ponds, resources, and impact zones. [Documented]

This investigation defines the structural-to-fast violence continuum as the seamless connection between the legal architecture of dispossession, its maintenance through bureaucratic dismissal, and its enforcement through militarized suppression. [Documented]

This investigation defines distributed presence as the condition under which a territory, its ecology, and its dispossession are simultaneously present in distant commodities, financial instruments, and consumer devices, yet materially absent from the regimes of visibility that govern accountability. [Documented]

This investigation defines opacity architecture as the systematic design of legal, technical, and discursive structures that renders the material conditions and political relations of extraction systematically illegible to affected communities, regulators, and investigators. [Documented]

This investigation defines performed transparency as the strategic production of selective visibility that creates an appearance of openness while preserving core operational opacity, substituting the spectacle of openness for the possibility of accountability. [Documented]

The Atacama lithium assemblage naturalizes the cosmological claim that planetary energy transition requires the sacrifice of specific territories and indigenous lifeworlds, presupposing that the green future of the global North is structurally dependent on the sacrifice of the global South's ecologies. [Inferred]

The Atacama lithium assemblage naturalizes the cosmological claim that water is a quantifiable commodity rather than a living relational substrate, treating water as measurable, exchangeable, and substitutable through the Dirección General de Aguas concession regime and corporate water-balance spreadsheets. [Inferred]

The Atacama lithium assemblage naturalizes the cosmological claim that corporate environmental self-monitoring is epistemically sufficient to govern planetary boundaries, substituting corporate data for ecological reality through Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile environmental impact assessments and Apple Inc. supplier audits. [Inferred]

The Atacama lithium assemblage naturalizes the cosmological claim that state violence is a legitimate technical correction to communities who refuse the calculus of sacrifice, treating communities who reject concession logic as operational risks to be managed by security forces. [Inferred]

The Atacama lithium assemblage naturalizes the cosmological claim that the spatial and jurisdictional distribution of extraction across global supply chains is an inevitable feature of modern commerce rather than an architecture of deliberate evasion, treating confidentiality clauses and proprietary data as market necessities rather than technologies of dispossession. [Inferred]

Layer 1 of this investigation tracks financial and corporate governance structures including revenue streams, ownership structures, stock-exchange disclosures, beneficial ownership, joint ventures, lithium segment margins, debt financing, and the entry of Chinese state-affiliated capital into Chilean lithium. [Documented]

Layer 1 draws on Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile annual reports and 20-F filings with the United States Securities and Exchange Commission, Santiago Stock Exchange disclosures, Tianqi Lithium Corporation annual reports, Nutrien Ltd. regulatory filings, and Corporación Nacional del Cobre de Chile corporate disclosures. [Documented]

Layer 2 of this investigation tracks regulatory permits and tribunal records including water concessions, brine extraction permits, environmental qualification resolutions, Tribunal Ambiental rulings, ministerial decrees, and the legal architecture of the 1981 Water Code and subsequent amendments. [Documented]

Layer 2 draws on Dirección General de Aguas concession registries, Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental project databases, Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta rulings, Comité de Ministros decisions, and Chilean official gazette records. [Documented]

Layer 3 of this investigation tracks technical and ecological systems including brine extraction technologies, evaporation pond design and expansion, lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide processing flows, aquifer hydrology, water-balance modeling, environmental monitoring well networks, and the material metabolism of the extraction site. [Documented]

Layer 3 draws on Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile environmental impact assessments, Dirección General de Aguas hydrological monitoring reports, independent academic hydrogeological studies, satellite imagery, and corporate technical disclosures. [Documented]

Layer 4 of this investigation tracks supply chain disclosures and community testimony including downstream supply chain disclosures from Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc., Atacameño community submissions to environmental tribunals and administrative processes, testimonies of water loss and territorial dispossession, and the militarization of protest in 2019 and beyond. [Documented]

Layer 4 draws on Apple Inc. Supplier Responsibility Progress Reports, Tesla Inc. Impact Reports, Atacameño community submissions to Tribunal Ambiental, investigative journalism, United Nations Special Rapporteur communications, and human rights organization reports. [Documented]

Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile is the principal lithium and potassium extractor in the Salar de Atacama and operates evaporation ponds, brine pumping infrastructure, and lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide processing plants. [Documented]

Tianqi Lithium Corporation is a Chinese lithium processor and minority owner of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile, exercising governance influence over Atacama extraction strategy. [Documented]

Apple Inc. is a major consumer of lithium-ion batteries that publishes Supplier Responsibility reports with supply chain due-diligence claims. [Documented]

Tesla Inc. is an electric vehicle manufacturer and major lithium demand driver that publishes Impact Reports with environmental claims. [Documented]

The Dirección General de Aguas is the Chilean national water authority that administers water concessions and monitors hydrological extraction under the 1981 Water Code. [Documented]

The Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental is the Chilean agency that administers the environmental impact assessment system and issues Resoluciones de Calificación Ambiental for extraction projects. [Documented]

The Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta is a specialized environmental court with jurisdiction over northern Chile that hears Atacameño community challenges to lithium project approvals. [Documented]

The Carabineros de Chile is the Chilean national police force that was deployed to suppress protests during the 2019 Estallido Social and subsequent mining-region mobilizations. [Documented]

The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños is the coordinating body representing Atacameño communities in legal and political struggles against lithium extraction. [Documented]

The Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Toconao is an Atacameño community located near the Salar de Atacama that has filed water and territorial claims against extraction expansion. [Documented]

The Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Socaire is a highland Atacameño community with ancestral water rights claims affected by upstream extraction. [Documented]

The Comunidad Indígena Atacameña de Peine is an Atacameño community asserting territorial and hydrological rights against extraction expansion. [Documented]

This investigation assumes that corporate annual reports, securities filings, regulatory registries, tribunal rulings, and supply chain disclosures are adequate proxies for operational reality, an assumption that excludes embodied indigenous knowledge and proprietary corporate data and is therefore continuously interrogated. [Documented]

This investigation assumes that analyzing Atacameño territories through categories of aquifer, concession, and impact zone risks replicating the ontological erasure that the investigation critiques, and therefore reserves space for community-submitted concepts and refuses to reduce them to the four-layer scaffold. [Documented]

This investigation acknowledges that its findings will lag behind Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile's continuous pumping operations, and theorizes this lag as a structural feature of accountability under conditions of temporal asymmetry rather than a mere inconvenience. [Documented]

This investigation acknowledges that its four-layer scaffold fragments an integrated socio-ecological system, reflecting a political choice rooted in disciplinary training, and remains open to introducing hydrological, cosmological, and affective schematics as the investigation proceeds. [Documented]

This investigation acknowledges that extracting data from Atacameño struggles for a European academic dossier risks reproducing the extractive logic under investigation, and therefore evaluates every finding against whether it strengthens community claims-making or merely accumulates in the institution. [Documented]

This investigation is shaped by its European university location and public funding, which provide access to United States Securities and Exchange Commission filings, European Union corporate transparency databases, and academic journals, but which do not provide subpoena power, field presence in the Atacama Desert, or direct access to classified supply chain contracts. [Documented]

This investigation is shaped by linguistic limitations including functional Spanish-language capacity and the absence of Kunza or Ckunza language competence, which may result in the under-representation of indigenous epistemes and non-Spanish community protocols. [Documented]

This investigation rejects the claim of neutrality, asserting instead an explicit normative commitment to democratic accountability, indigenous territorial sovereignty, and the redistribution of epistemic power. [Documented]

This investigation warns against the risk of green fetishism, insisting that technical description of lithium supply chains must be consistently re-anchored in political analysis of who is dispossessed of water and who profits from the battery. [Documented]

This investigation warns against the substitution of corporate sustainability reports for accountability, treating Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. disclosure regimes as objects of critique rather than proxies for justice. [Documented]

This investigation warns that tracing Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. supply chains implicates Northern consumers and European publics in Atacama dispossession, making the mapping an explicit claim about complicity and responsibility. [Documented]
