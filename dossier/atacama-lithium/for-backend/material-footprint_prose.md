# Material Footprint of Lithium Extraction in the Salar de Atacama

[Documented] On 7 June 2026, the Substrate Collective conducted a material footprint investigation into the lithium extraction assemblage in the Salar de Atacama, Antofagasta Region, Republic of Chile, tracing supply chains from subsurface brine pumping through corporate chemical processing to battery manufacturing for consumer electronics and electric vehicles.

[Documented] In 1981, the military dictatorship of General Augusto Pinochet Ugarte enacted Decreto Ley 1.122, known as the 1981 Water Code, which transformed water from a public good into a privately tradable commodity by establishing transferable water rights independent of land ownership in the Republic of Chile.

[Documented] The 1981 Water Code allowed the Chilean state to grant water use rights in perpetuity without mandatory extraction limits or forfeiture for non-use, enabling private corporations to accumulate monopoly control over hydrological basins while displacing indigenous Atacameño communities who had managed water through customary use for millennia.

[Documented] In January 2022, the Chilean Congress approved a bill amending the Chilean Water Code, which was published on 6 April 2022 and became applicable law, introducing a maximum 30-year term for new water rights, priority for human consumption and sanitation, minimum ecological flow requirements, and expiration for non-use, though existing rights grandfathered under the 1981 code remain largely intact.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A., commercially known as SQM S.A., is a Chilean chemical and mining company headquartered in Santiago, Chile, listed on the Santiago Stock Exchange and the New York Stock Exchange, and is one of the world's largest producers of lithium, iodine, and specialty nitrates.

[Documented] SQM Salar SpA, a subsidiary of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A., holds exclusive and temporary leasehold rights to exploit mineral resources in the Salar de Atacama until 31 December 2030 pursuant to lease agreements with Corporación de Fomento de la Producción, the Chilean state economic development agency commonly known as Corfo.

[Documented] Corporación de Fomento de la Producción owns the mineral and brine concessions in the Salar de Atacama and has leased extraction rights to SQM Salar SpA and Albemarle Corporation, a United States-based specialty chemicals company headquartered in Charlotte, North Carolina.

[Documented] The SQM-Corfo Agreements, as amended in January 2018, establish a total accumulated production and sales limit of up to 349,553 metric tons of lithium carbonate equivalent for Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. operations in the Salar de Atacama.

[Documented] As of 31 December 2023, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. had extracted approximately 43 percent of the total permitted accumulated extraction and sales limit of lithium under the SQM-Corfo Agreements.

[Documented] For the year ended 31 December 2024, revenues related to products originating from the Salar de Atacama represented 55 percent of the consolidated revenues of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A., consisting of revenues from the potassium business line and the lithium and derivatives business line.

[Documented] Approximately 49 percent of the total consolidated revenues of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. for the year ended 31 December 2024 were represented by lithium products.

[Documented] On 31 May 2024, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. and Corporación Nacional del Cobre de Chile, the Chilean state-owned copper mining company known as Codelco, entered into a partnership agreement establishing the rights and obligations of the parties to form an entity for the development of mining and production activities aimed at the production of lithium, potassium, and other products from the properties of Corporación de Fomento de la Producción in the Salar de Atacama during the period from 2025 to 2060.

[Documented] On 15 December 2023, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. signed an agreement with Corporación Nacional del Cobre de Chile and the Atacameños Indigenous Organization to include the Atacameños Indigenous Organization in discussions regarding extending lithium extraction rights in the Salar de Atacama.

[Documented] The lease agreement between Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. and Corporación de Fomento de la Producción includes a commitment to invest between 10 million and 15 million United States dollars annually in sustainable development projects for the Atacama La Grande indigenous communities through organizations promoting local development.

[Documented] The Salar de Atacama is located approximately 210 kilometers east of Antofagasta in the Atacama Desert of northern Chile and is a salt-encrusted depression containing an underground deposit of brines contained in porous sodium chloride rock fed by an underground inflow from the Andes mountains.

[Documented] The brines of the Salar de Atacama are estimated to cover a surface of approximately 2,800 square kilometers and contain commercially exploitable deposits of potassium, lithium, sulfates, and boron.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. pumps brines from depths of 15 to 150 meters below the surface through a field of wells distributed across the Salar de Atacama.

[Documented] In order to produce lithium carbonate, lithium hydroxide, potassium chloride, and other products, brines from the Salar de Atacama are pumped to solar evaporation ponds where evaporation of the water contained in the brine in a sequential process of precipitation and evaporation results in potassium-enriched salts and lithium-concentrated brines.

[Documented] In the first stages of the evaporation process, sodium chloride salts known as halite precipitate, followed by potassium chloride salts together with sodium chloride known as sylvinite, which are used to produce fertilizer products.

[Documented] The concentrated lithium chloride solution obtained during the evaporation process contains approximately 4 to 5 percent lithium, and this solution is then transported by truck approximately 190 kilometers southeast to the Lithium Chemical Plant located near the city of Antofagasta.

[Documented] At the Lithium Chemical Plant near Antofagasta, the lithium chloride solution is further purified and treated with sodium carbonate to produce lithium carbonate, which is dried, compacted if necessary, and packaged for shipment to customers.

[Documented] The production capacity of the lithium carbonate facility at the Lithium Chemical Plant near Antofagasta was 210,000 metric tons per year at the end of 2024, and Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. plans to increase this capacity to 240,000 metric tons per year by the end of 2026 or early 2027.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. produces lithium hydroxide at the Lithium Chemical Plant near Antofagasta, where three plants together had a total production capacity of 40,000 metric tons per year as of the end of 2024, and the company is in the process of increasing this capacity to 100,000 metric tons per year by the end of 2025.

[Documented] In the lithium hydroxide production process, lithium carbonate is reacted with a lime solution to produce lithium hydroxide brine and calcium carbonate salt, and the brine is then evaporated in a multi-effect evaporator and crystallized to produce lithium hydroxide which is dried and packaged for shipment to customers.

[Documented] During the brine concentration process, it is possible to obtain lithium sulfate as an additional raw material, and the wet lithium sulfate is treated at the SOP S/C plant producing dry lithium sulfate as a finished product, which is currently sent to the refining plant and different tolling facilities in the People's Republic of China to be converted into lithium hydroxide and lithium carbonate.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. operates a lithium hydroxide refining plant in the People's Republic of China with a design annual capacity of 20,000 metric tons that began operations at the beginning of 2023, and the company additionally maintains tolling contracts with tolling facilities in China for an additional annual capacity of 20,000 metric tons.

[Documented] All of the potassium chloride-related plants in the Salar de Atacama currently have a nominal production capacity of up to 2.6 million metric tons per year, and the remainder of the potassium chloride produced at the Salar de Atacama is shipped to the port in Tocopilla in either crystallized or granular form and then sold as a commodity fertilizer to third parties.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. operates port facilities at Tocopilla, Chile, for the shipment of products and the delivery of raw materials pursuant to maritime concessions granted by the respective administrative authorities.

[Documented] With total sales of approximately 204.9 thousand metric tons of lithium carbonate and hydroxide in 2024, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. believes its market share of lithium chemicals was approximately 17 percent globally in 2024.

[Documented] During 2024, demand for lithium chemicals increased by approximately 25 percent, exceeding 1.2 million metric tons globally, and batteries represented the main application for lithium with approximately 90 percent of total demand, including batteries for electric vehicles which represented approximately 70 percent of total demand in 2024.

[Documented] In 2024, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. sold its lithium products in 43 countries to approximately 218 customers, and 93 percent of its sales of lithium were in Asia.

[Documented] Two customers accounted for at least 10 percent of the lithium and lithium derivatives sales of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. in 2024, representing approximately 28 percent of lithium revenues, and the ten largest customers together accounted for approximately 60 percent of lithium revenues.

[Documented] One supplier, Corporación de Fomento de la Producción, accounted for approximately 24 percent of the cost of sales of the lithium and derivatives business line of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. in 2024.

[Documented] Average lithium prices decreased from 30,467 United States dollars per metric ton in 2023 to 10,936 United States dollars per metric ton during the year ended 31 December 2024.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. believes its capital expenditures for 2025, including maintenance, could reach approximately 1.1 billion United States dollars, of which approximately 550 million United States dollars is allocated to the SQM Lithium Chile Division for continued capacity expansions at the Lithium Chemical Plant and the Salar de Atacama facilities, sustainability initiatives, and increased efficiencies.

[Documented] Water for the Nueva Victoria and Salar de Atacama facilities of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. is obtained from wells near the production facilities.

[Documented] For the lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide production processes at the Lithium Chemical Plant near Antofagasta, in 2024 Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. recovered approximately 1,060,000 cubic meters of ultrapure water from liquid plant waste, and the rest of the water required for the process was purchased from third parties.

[Documented] The main sources of water for the nitrate and iodine facilities of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. at Pedro de Valdivia, María Elena, and Coya Sur are the Loa and San Salvador rivers.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. holds water use rights obtained from the Chilean Water Authority, known as the Dirección General de Aguas, for the supply of water from rivers and wells near its production facilities.

[Documented] Since 2017, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. has been connected to the central electricity grid in Chile, and its electricity needs are primarily covered by Power Purchase Agreements entered into with Empresa Eléctrica Cochrane SpA, an affiliate of AES Corporation, on 31 December 2012.

[Documented] In October 2020, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. announced its Sustainable Development Plan, which includes voluntarily expanding monitoring systems, promoting conversations with neighboring communities, becoming carbon neutral by 2040, and reducing water consumption by 65 percent and brine extraction by 50 percent of authorized limits.

[Documented] In September 2023, the lithium mining operations of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. in the Salar de Atacama achieved a score of 75 in the Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance standard, which the company states is the highest score ever awarded to a lithium company worldwide.

[Documented] In 2024, Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. completed the Carbon Disclosure Project water assessment with a B rating and the climate assessment with a B minus rating.

[Documented] On 13 December 2017, the Environmental Court of Antofagasta ordered a temporary and partial closure of operations of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. in the Salar de Atacama.

[Documented] In September 2021, SQM Salar S.A. proposed to the Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente a new environmental compliance plan, which was approved by the Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente and is subject to review by the Environmental Court of Antofagasta after a claim was filed by an indigenous association.

[Corroborated] The Tribunal Ambiental de Antofagasta, a specialized environmental court established under Ley 20.600 in 2010, has declined to revoke the extraction permits of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A., ruling in favor of continued operations while ordering limited hydrological monitoring measures that did not suspend brine pumping activities.

[Documented] The Atacameño Peoples Council, known as the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños, represents 18 Atacameño indigenous communities and advocates for the rights, traditions, and interests of the Atacameño people, including land use, environmental protection, and economic development in the Atacama region of Chile.

[Documented] On 18 October 2019, mass protests erupted across the Republic of Chile in response to a public transit fare increase, rapidly escalating into a nationwide social movement known as the Estallido Social demanding structural reforms to pensions, education, health care, and the 1981 Water Code.

[Documented] On 19 October 2019, President Sebastián Piñera Echenique declared a state of emergency and deployed military forces into Santiago and other major cities, stating publicly that Chile was at war against a powerful enemy.

[Documented] Amnesty International documented that Carabineros de Chile, the Chilean national police force, used excessive force during the October 2019 protests, including firing rubber-coated metal projectiles that caused eye injuries and permanent blindness in at least 34 individuals.

[Documented] During the October 2019 state of emergency and subsequent months of militarized policing, lithium extraction operations in the Salar de Atacama continued without interruption and no brine extraction permits were suspended or subjected to emergency review by the Chilean government.

[Inferred] The spatial displacement between sites of lithium extraction in the remote Atacama Desert and sites of protest militarization in urban centers such as Santiago constitutes a territorial division of violence in which the water-intensive material substrate for global batteries is secured through state force while its ecological and social consequences remain geographically and politically invisible to end consumers in North America, Europe, and East Asia.

[Documented] Apple Inc., a consumer electronics corporation headquartered in Cupertino, California, publishes an annual Supplier Responsibility Report and supplier list that traces its supply chain for materials used in iPhones, MacBooks, and other devices, though current disclosure frameworks under the United States Dodd-Frank Act do not mandate public reporting of lithium suppliers because lithium is not classified as a conflict mineral.

[Corroborated] Tesla Inc., an electric vehicle and energy corporation headquartered in Austin, Texas, has disclosed supply relationships with Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. and sources lithium hydroxide and lithium carbonate from South American brine operations for battery production at Tesla Gigafactory facilities.

[Corroborated] Tesla Inc. Chief Executive Officer Elon Musk publicly discussed shifting toward lithium iron phosphate battery chemistries and direct lithium extraction technologies in 2022 and 2023, yet Tesla Inc. continued to source refined lithium products from Chilean brine operations during this period according to corporate disclosures and sector reporting.

[Inferred] The supply chain disclosures published by Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. treat lithium as an abstract commodity input rather than a geologically specific substance extracted from the Salar de Atacama, thereby obscuring the hydrological violence and indigenous dispossession embedded in each kilowatt-hour of battery capacity sold to consumers.

[Documented] Tianqi Lithium Corporation, a Chinese lithium producer headquartered in Chengdu, acquired an approximately 23.77 percent ownership stake in Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. from Nutrien Limited in 2018, creating a direct ownership link between the People's Republic of China's battery supply chain and the Atacama extraction complex.

[Documented] Julio Ponce Lerou, the former son-in-law of General Augusto Pinochet Ugarte, served for decades as the controlling shareholder and chairman of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. through his holding vehicle Pampa Group, maintaining a direct lineage between the military dictatorship's inner circle and contemporary extractive governance in the Atacama.

[Documented] The Salar de Atacama is part of the Lithium Triangle, a high-altitude arid zone spanning northern Chile, northwestern Argentina, and southern Bolivia that collectively contains approximately 60 percent of the world's known lithium reserves beneath salt flat basins.

[Inferred] Battery cells manufactured with lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide from the Salar de Atacama are assembled into battery packs at facilities operated by Panasonic Corporation, Contemporary Amperex Technology Company Limited, and LG Energy Solution Limited before installation in electric vehicles produced by Tesla Inc., BMW AG, Volkswagen AG, and other automakers.

[Documented] Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. is constructing a seawater pipeline with a capacity of 900 liters per second that is expected to enter into operation by the end of 2026, and the company states that the use of seawater for future or current operations could increase operating costs while seawater projects could face timing issues and permits uncertainty.

[Documented] The Tente en el Aire iodine plant module 4, with a capacity of 6,000 metric tons of iodide per year, had its construction completed by the end of 2024, and this additional volume will require additional water consumption to be provided by the new seawater pipeline.

[Corroborated] Peer-reviewed hydrological studies and industry benchmarks estimate that brine-based lithium extraction in the Atacama consumes approximately 500,000 to 2,000,000 liters of water per metric ton of lithium carbonate equivalent produced, though exact facility-level figures vary by methodology and remain contested between corporate disclosures and independent academic audits.

[Inferred] The subsurface brine and freshwater extracted by Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. and Albemarle Corporation originate from the same regional aquifer systems that sustain fragile high-altitude wetland ecosystems known as vegas and bofedales, which provide forage for camelid herds and habitat for migratory bird species and have been stewarded by Atacameño communities for generations.

[Inferred] The documented aquifer depletion in the Salar de Atacama constitutes a form of slow violence, defined by scholar Rob Nixon as incremental environmental degradation that inflicts harm across generational time scales exceeding the audit cycles of regulatory agencies and the reporting horizons of corporate sustainability disclosures.

[Documented] The environmental permit Resolución de Calificación Ambiental RCA No. 226/2006, issued on 19 October 2006 by the Comisión Regional del Medio Ambiente, authorizes Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. to extract brines via pumping wells from two designated areas in the western and southwestern portions of the Salar de Atacama concession zones, which the company refers to as Áreas Autorizadas para la Extracción.

[Documented] The Áreas Autorizadas para la Extracción zones are divided into the AAE-SOP northern portion covering 10,512 hectares, equivalent to 29.27 percent of the total authorized extraction area, and the AAE-MOP southern portion covering 25,399 hectares, equivalent to 70.73 percent of the total authorized extraction area.

[Documented] The water that Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. uses for mineral production in the Salar de Atacama is obtained from wells located in the alluvial aquifer on the eastern edge of the Salar de Atacama, for which the company holds groundwater use rights and the corresponding environmental authorization under RCA No. 226/2006.

[Documented] Under the authorized maximum net brine production rates defined in RCA 226/2006 and the voluntary reduction plan announced by Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. in 2020, future pumping is characterized by a reduction from 1,166 liters per second to 822 liters per second during the remaining six-year life of mine, under which a total of approximately 175 million cubic meters of brine will be extracted from the producing wells, corresponding to 0.22 million metric tons of lithium.

[Documented] In November 2016, the Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente filed charges against SQM Salar SpA for extraction of brine beyond the authorized amount, progressive damage to the vitality of algarrobo trees, incomplete information delivery, and modification of monitoring plan variables, among other alleged violations.

[Documented] SQM Salar SpA submitted a compliance program to the Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente, which was approved on 29 August 2022, and the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños filed a claim regarding this program with the Environmental Court of Antofagasta.

[Documented] On 11 June 2024, the Environmental Court of Antofagasta agreed to reject the claim filed by the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños regarding the SQM Salar SpA compliance program in its entirety, and SQM Salar SpA is currently implementing the compliance program.

[Documented] Through a resolution issued on 14 April 2020, the General Water Directorate, which is the regional office of the Dirección General de Aguas in Antofagasta, imposed a fine of 4,180 monthly tax units on SQM Salar SpA for alleged violations of Article 294 of the Water Code, and this resolution was appealed with the outcome still pending as of the date of the 2024 annual report.

[Documented] In May 2024, the General Water Directorate of the Antofagasta Region initiated a sanctioning procedure against SQM Salar SpA for alleged violations of Article 294 of the Water Code at the solar evaporation ponds of the Atacama Salt Flat operation, and Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. presented defenses rejecting the alleged non-compliance with the resolution from the General Water Directorate still pending.

[Documented] Through a resolution issued on 15 October 2024, the General Water Directorate imposed a fine of 1,285 monthly tax units on SQM Salar SpA for alleged violations of Articles 5 and 6 of Dirección General de Aguas Resolution No. 1,238 regarding the monitoring and reporting system for effective extractions at the groundwater extraction facilities, and this resolution was appealed with the outcome still pending.

[Documented] In July 2024, a criminal complaint was filed for alleged environmental non-compliance in the Atacama Salt Flat, which may be investigated under Article 308 of the Criminal Code of the Republic of Chile, and the complaint is being handled by the Calama Public Prosecutor's Office based on information presented in an exceptional review request for environmental qualification resolutions filed by Albemarle Limited.

[Documented] As of 31 December 2024, six years remain on the term of the SQM-Corfo Agreements.

[Documented] Law No. 21,455, published in the Official Gazette of the Republic of Chile on 21 June 2022, establishes a legal framework for climate change and amends the Chilean Corporations Act to require open stock corporations registered in the Securities Registry to periodically provide information to the Chilean Financial Market Commission regarding the impact of their activities on the environment and climate change.

[Documented] The decarbonization targets of Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. were validated by Science Based Targets after a robust review process, and the Lithium Chile Division achieved a gold rating with Ecovadis for the first time in 2024.

[Documented] The port and storage facilities at the Port of Tocopilla terminal operated by Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. cover approximately 22 hectares and are located approximately 186 kilometers north of Antofagasta, approximately 124 kilometers west of María Elena and Coya Sur, and 372 kilometers west of the Salar de Atacama.
