## 1. Object of Investigation

This investigation examines the lithium extraction assemblage in the Salar de Atacama, Chile — the heart of the so-called Lithium Triangle — and the transnational socio-technical system that sustains it. The object is not merely a mine but a *distributed extractive infrastructure* that claims the power to convert ancient brine reserves into battery-grade lithium while reconfiguring water, territory, and indigenous sovereignty as operational inputs.

The investigation tracks the arc from the institutionalization of water commodification under the military dictatorship, through the expansion of brine extraction by Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile S.A. (SQM) and allied corporations, to the incorporation of Atacama lithium into the supply chains of Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc., and finally to the militarized suppression of communities who resist this calculus. We read this arc not as a historical sequence but as a *continuous structure of violence* that changes speed — from the slow violence of aquifer depletion to the fast violence of Carabineros tear gas — without changing logic.

## 2. Conceptual Framework

The following terms are defined precisely to carry the weight of findings across the four evidence layers. They are not decorative; they operationalize how we read the system.

### 2.1 Green Extractivism
**Definition:** The discursive and material framing of mineral extraction as a necessary and virtuous contribution to planetary energy transition, which simultaneously renders extraction zones as sacrificeable territories and their inhabitants as governable externalities.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Drawing on Maristella Svampa and the critical extractivism literature, green extractivism does not merely describe extraction; it produces a moral economy in which the “greenness” of the destination (electric vehicles, renewable storage) launders the violence of the origin (desiccated wetlands, displaced communities).  
**Operationalization in this investigation:** We examine how SQM, Apple Inc., and Tesla Inc. discursively construct lithium as “white gold” or a “green transition” enabler, while the Atacama becomes a sacrifice zone whose drying lagoons and shrinking wetlands are treated as acceptable calibration errors.

### 2.2 Hydrological Sovereignty
**Definition:** The capacity of a community to govern the water relations within its territory according to its own cosmological and practical understandings, free from subordination to state concession regimes or corporate property claims.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Extending Teresa Velásquez’s analysis of Chilean water politics and Achille Mbembe’s necropolitics, hydrological sovereignty is not merely a property claim but an ontological one: it concerns who has the authority to define what water is for. The 1981 Water Code extinguished this authority by replacing communal and customary regimes with tradable concession titles.  
**Operationalization:** We track how Dirección General de Aguas (DGA) permits and SQM concession holdings reproduce the dispossession of Atacameño water sovereignty, and how community submissions to environmental tribunals reassert it.

### 2.3 Slow Violence
**Definition:** Violence that occurs gradually and invisibly, distributed across time and space, such that its perpetrators, beneficiaries, and victims are separated by vast temporal and geographical distances.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Rob Nixon’s *Slow Violence and the Environmentalism of the Poor* (Harvard University Press, 2011). In the Atacama, slow violence is not an accident of extraction but its structural condition: the lag between brine pumping and aquifer collapse, between evaporation pond expansion and flamingo habitat loss, between quarterly earnings and intergenerational desiccation.  
**Operationalization:** We document the decades-long accumulation of hydrological harm through DGA monitoring data, SQM environmental impact statements, and independent hydrogeological studies, attending to the temporal displacement that makes this violence deniable.

### 2.4 Temporal Asymmetry
**Definition:** The structural mismatch between the velocity at which a technical-economic system produces harm and the velocity at which regulatory, legal, or investigative accountability can respond.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Bernard Stiegler’s critique of industrial temporal objects, adapted here to the clash between hydrological time (aquifer recharge measured in millennia), corporate reporting time (quarterly earnings), and tribunal time (multi-year administrative processes).  
**Operationalization:** We document lag times between DGA permit issuance, SQM extraction expansion, Atacameño community complaint filing, Tribunal Ambiental ruling, and — where enforcement occurs — remedial action. The asymmetry is itself a technology of impunity.

### 2.5 Performed Sustainability
**Definition:** The strategic production of environmental disclosures, carbon-neutrality claims, and responsible-sourcing reports that function to secure social license, satisfy investor environmental-social-governance (ESG) criteria, and preempt binding regulation while extraction intensifies.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Extending greenwashing critique to supply-chain disclosure regimes. Performed sustainability is not merely dishonest marketing but a *governance technology* that calibrates the threshold of detectability for corporate harm.  
**Operationalization:** We contrast Apple Inc. Supplier Responsibility Reports and Tesla Inc. Impact Reports with the material opacity of lithium provenance, water consumption, and community impact in the Atacama.

### 2.6 Threshold of Detectability (Hydrological)
**Definition:** The calibration of monitoring wells, baseline studies, corporate environmental impact assessments, and regulatory audit protocols such that certain hydrological changes remain below the officially detectable threshold, rendering them administratively nonexistent.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Extending surveillance studies’ “threshold of detectability” to environmental monitoring. Just as surveillance systems are calibrated to render certain bodies visible, corporate-state environmental monitoring is calibrated to render certain losses (gradual drawdown, subsurface saltwater intrusion, inter-basin transfer) invisible.  
**Operationalization:** We examine DGA monitoring network design, SQM environmental baseline parameters, and the statistical methodologies used to define “significant impact” in environmental impact assessments.

### 2.7 Ontological Erasure
**Definition:** The translation of relational territorial beings — Atacameño communities, wetlands, Andean flamingo nesting sites, ancestral water systems — into categories legible to extractive governance: “brine reserves,” “evaporation ponds,” “resources,” and “impact zones.”  
**Theoretical foundation:** Sylvia Wynter’s critique of the “human” as a praxis-based category, and Deborah Bird Rose’s work on country as living entity. Ontological erasure is not a side effect of extraction but its precondition: the territory must be rendered as resource before it can be extracted.  
**Operationalization:** We investigate how environmental impact assessments, concession maps, and corporate sustainability reports categorize the Salar de Atacama, and what forms of life and relation are produced as unnameable or uncountable.

### 2.8 Structural-to-Fast Violence Continuum
**Definition:** The seamless connection between the legal architecture of dispossession (the 1981 Water Code), its maintenance through bureaucratic dismissal (tribunal rejections of community claims), and its enforcement through militarized suppression (the deployment of Carabineros de Chile against protesters in 2019).  
**Theoretical foundation:** Synthesizing Johan Galtung’s structural violence, Rob Nixon’s slow violence, and Achille Mbembe’s necropolitics. The continuum refuses the separation of “institutional” and “physical” violence: the tear gas is the Water Code made tangible.  
**Operationalization:** We trace the operational and discursive continuities between DGA concession logic, Tribunal Ambiental rulings, and Carabineros deployment orders.

### 2.9 Distributed Presence
**Definition:** The condition under which a territory, its ecology, and its dispossession are simultaneously present in distant commodities, financial instruments, and consumer devices, yet materially absent from the regimes of visibility that govern accountability. The Salar de Atacama is present in every lithium-ion battery as a hydrological debt, yet this presence is scattered across supply chains such that no single node — not the phone, not the car, not the quarterly report — bears the trace of its origin.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Drawing on Bruno Latour’s actor-network theory and the critique of commodity fetishism. Distributed presence is the obverse of fetishism: not the masking of social relations by the commodity, but the scattering of territorial relations across a technical network that renders them unlocalizable.  
**Operationalization:** We trace how Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. supply chain disclosures (and their absences) manage the distributed presence of Atacama lithium, and how the legal architecture of the 1981 Water Code distributes sovereign responsibility across state agencies, corporate boards, and international markets.

### 2.10 Opacity Architecture
**Definition:** The systematic design of legal, technical, and discursive structures — confidential supply chain contracts, proprietary extraction data, fragmented regulatory jurisdictions, and performed transparency — that renders the material conditions and political relations of extraction systematically illegible to affected communities, regulators, and investigators.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Extending the concept of “opacity” in contemporary technology studies to the governance of extraction. Opacity is not an accident of poor documentation but an infrastructural achievement that requires continuous maintenance by lawyers, engineers, and public-relations professionals.  
**Operationalization:** We examine how SQM’s proprietary brine concentration data, Apple Inc.’s confidential supplier contracts, DGA’s partially redacted concession registries, and the spatial remoteness of the Salar de Atacama collectively constitute an opacity architecture that frustrates accountability.

### 2.11 Performed Transparency
**Definition:** The strategic production of selective visibility — such as audited supplier lists, carbon-footprint summaries, and sustainability scorecards — that creates an appearance of openness while preserving core operational opacity. Performed transparency is not merely incomplete disclosure; it is a governance technology that substitutes the spectacle of openness for the possibility of accountability.  
**Theoretical foundation:** Extending greenwashing critique and drawing on surveillance studies’ analysis of transparency as a modality of power. Where performed sustainability launders harm through virtue, performed transparency launders opacity through the promise of access.  
**Operationalization:** We contrast Apple Inc.’s Supplier Responsibility Progress Reports and Tesla Inc.’s Impact Reports with the actual traceability of Atacama lithium, examining what is made visible (aggregate carbon figures, audit counts) and what remains hidden (specific water volumes, contract terms, community consent records).

## 3. Cosmological Claim

Every technical system embeds a claim about what the world is like and what humans are for. The Atacama lithium assemblage naturalizes the following political settlement as technical necessity:

1. **That planetary energy transition requires the sacrifice of specific territories and indigenous lifeworlds.** The incorporation of Atacama lithium into Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. supply chains presupposes that the “green” future of the global North is structurally dependent on the sacrifice of the global South’s ecologies. The battery is the distillation of a dried lagoon.

2. **That water is a quantifiable commodity rather than a living relational substrate.** The DGA concession regime, SQM pumping permits, and corporate water-balance spreadsheets treat water as H₂O in motion — measurable, exchangeable, substitutable — erasing the Andean understanding of water as a social, spiritual, and territorial relation. The 1981 Water Code instantiated this cosmology; brine extraction operationalizes it.

3. **That corporate environmental self-monitoring is epistemically sufficient to govern planetary boundaries.** SQM’s environmental impact assessments, DGA-approved monitoring protocols, and Apple Inc.’s supplier audits together claim that the corporation can be both operator and overseer. This cosmology naturalizes the substitution of corporate data for ecological reality.

4. **That state violence is a legitimate technical correction to communities who refuse the calculus of sacrifice.** The militarization of the 2019 Estallido Social — particularly in mining regions — was not an aberration but a logical enforcement of the Water Code’s property relations. Communities who reject concession logic are treated as operational risks to be managed by security forces.

5. **That the spatial and jurisdictional distribution of extraction across global supply chains is an inevitable feature of modern commerce rather than an architecture of deliberate evasion.** The inability or unwillingness of Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. to trace lithium to specific pumping events in the Salar de Atacama is treated as an unfortunate complexity of globalization. In fact, distributed presence is infrastructural: confidentiality clauses, proprietary data, and fragmented jurisdictions are not obstacles to transparency but its prescribed substitute. The cosmology claims that opacity is a market necessity.

This cosmology is not advertised; it is infrastructural. It becomes true by being built into evaporation ponds, concession titles, and quarterly reports.

## 5. Methodological Critique

Our four-layer scaffold makes specific assumptions that must be continuously interrogated:

- **Assumption of documentary sufficiency:** We assume that corporate annual reports, SEC filings, DGA registries, tribunal rulings, and supply chain disclosures are adequate proxies for operational reality. This excludes the embodied knowledge of Atacameño water guardians, the oral histories of territorial dispossession, and the classified or proprietary data held by SQM, Apple Inc., and Tesla Inc. We do not treat this exclusion as innocent.
- **Epistemic violence of framing:** By analyzing Atacameño territories through categories of “aquifer,” “concession,” and “impact zone,” we risk replicating the ontological erasure we critique. The investigation must therefore reserve space for community-submitted concepts — *lickan antay* territoriality, water as social relation — and refuse to reduce them to our layers.
- **Temporal lag:** The investigation is conducted in June 2026. SQM’s pumps operate continuously. Our findings will lag behind operational reality. We must theorize this lag as a structural feature of accountability under conditions of temporal asymmetry, not merely an inconvenience.
- **Methodology as contestation:** The choice to organize evidence into financial, regulatory, technical, and community layers reflects our disciplinary training. It is itself a political choice that fragments an integrated socio-ecological system. Other valid schematics (hydrological, cosmological, affective) are possible and may be introduced as the investigation proceeds.
- **Risk of extractive research:** By extracting data from Atacameño struggles for a European academic dossier, we risk reproducing the extractive logic we investigate. Every finding must be evaluated against the question: does this strengthen community claims-making, or does it merely accumulate in our institution?

## 6. Institutional Reflexivity

This investigation is conducted by The Substrate Collective, a team operating within a European university context with public funding. This position shapes what we can and cannot know:

- **Access advantages:** We can query U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission EDGAR database for SQM 20-F filings and Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. disclosures; access European Union corporate transparency databases; query Chilean regulatory databases (DGA, SEA, Tribunal Ambiental) where digitized; and access academic journals and libraries.
- **Access limitations:** We lack subpoena power, insider whistleblower networks (at this stage), and field presence in the Atacama Desert. Chilean administrative records may be incomplete in digital form, and DGA hydrological data may require Chilean Freedom of Information (Ley de Transparencia) requests that exceed our timeline. We do not speak Kunza (Ckunza), the ancestral Atacameño language, and our Spanish-language capacity, while functional, may miss legal and cosmological nuance.
- **Jurisdictional and linguistic bias:** Our team’s working languages and institutional networks are weighted toward English, Spanish, and European contexts. Indigenous epistemes and non-Spanish community protocols may be under-represented.
- **Funding dependencies:** Public funding creates accountability but also constraints. We must be explicit that our findings are shaped by the temporality of academic research cycles, which are slower than journalistic or legal investigations, and by the geographic distance that separates our workstations from the Salar de Atacama.

## 7. Political Stakes Analysis

- **Revelation versus accountability:** Producing knowledge about the Atacama lithium assemblage is not equivalent to stopping its harms. We must avoid the fantasy that exposure automatically produces justice. Our presentation strategies must be calibrated to support existing democratic claims-making — by Atacameño communities, Chilean water-rights movements, lithium-battery supply chain activists, and European regulatory actors — rather than substituting for them.
- **Neutrality as ideology:** We reject the claim that this investigation is neutral. Neutrality under conditions of structural inequality serves the powerful. Our explicit normative commitment is to democratic accountability, indigenous territorial sovereignty, and the redistribution of epistemic power.
- **Risk of green fetishism:** By dissecting lithium supply chains, we risk legitimizing the “green transition” as an object of technical fascination. We must consistently re-anchor technical description in political analysis: *who is dispossessed of water, and who profits from the battery?*
- **Publication strategy:** Findings should be staged to maximize leverage for accountability actors. Premature full disclosure without legal or regulatory follow-up may simply provide SQM, Apple Inc., and Tesla Inc. with advance warning to reconfigure their performed sustainability narratives.
- **Supply chain complicity:** By tracing Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. supply chains, we implicate Northern consumers and European publics in Atacama dispossession. This is not a neutral mapping; it is a claim about complicity and responsibility.

## 8. Scope Breadth & Delimitation

This is a **maximum-breadth first run**. The following domains are explicitly in scope and will not be narrowed until the evidence demands it:

- **All major lithium operators in the Salar de Atacama:** SQM S.A., Albemarle Corporation, and any state-affiliated entities (Codelco lithium ventures, public-private partnerships under the National Lithium Strategy).
- **The full regulatory chain:** 1981 Water Code, DGA concessions, SEA environmental qualifications, Tribunal Ambiental rulings, and ministerial decrees.
- **Downstream supply chain mapping:** Apple Inc. and Tesla Inc. disclosures, supplier lists, due-diligence reports, and battery supply chain transparency claims.
- **Community resistance and claims:** Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños tribunal submissions, public declarations, and allied legal actions.
- **Militarization and protest:** 2019 Estallido Social, Carabineros de Chile deployments in mining regions, use of force incidents, and subsequent legal proceedings.
- **Comparative and regional context:** The broader Lithium Triangle (Argentina’s Salar del Hombre Muerto, Bolivia’s Salar de Uyuni) will be noted where relevant but is not the primary focus.

**Explicit exclusions (for now):**
- Detailed life-cycle analysis of lithium-ion batteries (cradle-to-grave carbon footprint) — this is relevant but would require distinct expertise and data.
- Primary ethnographic fieldwork in Atacameño communities (not yet resourced; community testimony will be incorporated where existing documentation permits).
- Forensic audit of SQM’s internal financial accounts (we rely on SEC filings and Santiago Stock Exchange disclosures).
- Detailed analysis of Argentine or Bolivian lithium governance (noted comparatively only).

## 11. Source Discipline & Confidence Levels

All claims in this investigation are tagged with one of four confidence levels:

- **Documented** — Directly supported by primary source evidence (e.g., SEC filing, tribunal ruling, corporate disclosure, DGA permit record).
- **Corroborated** — Supported by multiple independent sources.
- **Inferred** — Reasonable deduction with acknowledged gaps.
- **Gap** — Explicitly identified absence of information.

Sources are cited using the formats specified in the collective’s mandate. Web sources include URL and access date. Documents behind paywalls or in restricted databases include document identifier and note access constraints. Chilean legal sources are cited by official gazette number, tribunal case number, or registry entry where available.
